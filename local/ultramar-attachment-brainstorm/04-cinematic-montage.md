## 1 — PRIMA / Hadrian Voss

Kadaku wilderness does not forgive neat diagrams.

The bunker is a concrete tooth half-swallowed by jungle and mud. Its blast doors stand open like a throat that has decided, for one hour, to spit civilians instead of swallow them. Evacuation traffic pours out the back routes — wounded, children, Administratum clerks who have learned the price of paper. At the open front, the Astra Militarum remnant that stayed to buy that hour is already thinning into arithmetic.

Hadrian Voss of PRIMA does not go inside.

He takes the dirt in front of the doors — bolter in one fist, power gladius sleeping at his hip — and becomes the reference mark the whole stand must calibrate to.

— Turrets to my lanes — he voxes across Guard channels, forge-flat. — Not theirs. Mine. Autoloaders: three-burst, then pause. If you sing continuous, you teach the swarm your song. Squad marks: hold the flanks. Do not chase. Anything that wants the doors must want me first.

A lieutenant starts a protest about Astartes chain of command.

A Tyranid Warrior answers it for him by stepping out of the green like a cathedral given claws.

Voss’s first bolt packet is not panic. It is priority: joint, joint, synapse-adjacent meat. The Warrior staggers into the kill-funnel of two automated defense nests Voss has already retasked with a serf’s hard-key and a gauntlet that does not ask permission from doctrine. The nests fire ugly and correct. The Warrior dies teaching nothing.

Gaunts come in a ribbon.

The Guardsmen who remained — mud on their lasguns, saints on their lips — fire because a grey giant told them where the geometry lives. Voss walks the open ground like a forge overseer walking a bad shift: one step left when the swarm leans right, one gladius wake when a Lictor tries to make the doorframe into a blind. The Lictor is patience and camouflage. Voss is calibration. He lets it believe it has his back for half a heartbeat, then puts the power field through the place camouflage does not protect: the decision to be clever.

Blood hits open bunker doors.

He does not wipe it.

Civilians are still moving. Time is still a resource.

More Warriors. More Lictors peeled off the canopy like knives deciding to become a pack. The swarm has noticed the grey hinge and done what swarms do when they find a loud standard: it sends weight to break the standard.

Voss keeps their attention the way PRIMA keeps a pattern — by being the loudest correct thing in the field. Bolter bark. Gladius arc. A shoulder-check that turns a Warrior’s charge into a gift for the turret nests. When a Guardsman breaks and starts to step back into the bunker mouth, Voss’s vox cuts him without cruelty:

— Outside the doors until the last soft body is gone. Inside is for the living after. You are the living now. Hold.

They hold.

He holds alone in the sense that matters: no other Astartes in the mud, only mortal teeth and machine nests orbiting his mark. Warriors try to make him a trophy. Lictors try to make him a mistake. He deletes priority targets first — anything that could steer the ribbon, anything that could slip past into the open throat — and accepts lesser cuts as invoices. Grey enamel scores. Mud becomes paste. The power gladius returns to the hip only long enough for a reload that sounds like a stamped approval.

Behind him, the last civilian transport clears the rear track.

A Guard vox, raw with disbelief:

— Evacuation complete — Lord Astartes — doors —

— Seal — Voss says.

The blast doors begin to grind shut. For three seconds he is still outside with the swarm’s full regard, a single PRIMA seal between jungle and bunker, bolter empty into a Warrior’s skull-plate, gladius taking the Lictor that thought the closing gap was an invitation.

He steps back through the last man-wide slit as the doors lock.

Inside: panting Militarum, the smell of fear becoming the smell of survival. Outside: claws testing metal that no longer cares.

Voss racks a fresh magazine, checks the door seals like forge tolerances, and only then speaks to the lieutenant who has run out of protests.

— PRIMA — he says. — Line held. Your people live. Next fault.

An Ultramarine overflight pict, later, will show one grey figure in the mud before open doors — Warriors and Lictors fixed on him like iron filings on a magnet — while the soft column escapes into the green dark.

Nine enough, the pict implies, if the nine are built like this.

## 2 — SECVNDA / Cassian Holt

The hangar queue looks loyal.

That is the first fault.

Refugees, ratings, a Tech-adept with proper seals, three Militarum sergeants herding soft bodies toward water and ration stamps. Ultramarine security sweeps for weapons and wrong papers. Papers can be perfect. Weapons can wait in other hands. Cassian Holt of SECVNDA does not sweep for papers.

He walks the queue the way Silence walks a shift-change: reading what a mortal in this fear, this soot, this hour, usually is.

Gait. Breath. Blink. Where the eyes go when a child cries. The smell under soap.

Most of the line is wrong in honest ways — too tired, too loud, too human.

One man is correct in every channel at once.

Holt stops.

The man wears a munitorum clerk’s grit and a smile timed for comfort. His blinks are even. His grief-phrases are clean. When a serf stumbles, he helps with mirrored care that has no micro-asymmetry in it — no native wear, no private awkwardness, only the rehearsal of kindness.

An Ultramarine sergeant lifts a scanner.

— Clear — the sergeant says. — Move along.

Holt’s vox is soft enough to be mistaken for courtesy.

— That one does not move along.

— SECVNDA, this is not your —

Holt is already inside the clerk’s space. Not a charge. A placement. Two fingers find the throat-angle where a man’s fear should spike. The clerk’s pulse answers like a machine given a script: calm, calm, calm.

Too calm.

— Hands — Holt says.

The clerk’s hands rise in perfect compliance.

Then the compliance ends.

A blade appears from a sleeve that should not have held a blade. A second “serf” in the queue sheds fatigue like a cloak and brings a laspistol to an Ultramarine’s neck-seal. A third soft body — woman, weeping — stops weeping on a timer and reaches for a detonator under ration cloth.

Infiltrated traitors. Not one knife. A cell cut to look like mercy.

Holt’s stalker-bolt speaks first — legally sensitive, measured: one round through the clerk’s decision-making meat before the smile can become a signal. He pivots, gladius waking for the laspistol arm, taking the hand and the plot together. The detonator-woman gets the third gift: a bolt that ruins the device without turning the queue into a crater, because SECVNDA does not confuse justice with massacre.

The hangar detonates anyway — not in fire, in panic.

Ultramarines finally see what ivory saw ten heartbeats earlier. Security closes. Mortals scream. A fourth traitor tries to vanish into that scream, posture shifting toward “victim.”

Holt does not chase the theater.

He tracks the one channel panic cannot fake: the body’s sound under load — joints too smooth, breath too ready for violence. He puts the man on the deck with a shoulder and a restraint cuff from Silence kit, knee in the spine, helm close enough that only the traitor hears him.

— You were perfect — Holt murmurs. — Perfect is the anomaly.

Gadriel arrives into aftermath, bolt rifle up, and finds the cell already broken — three dead, one living for interrogation, the queue bruised but mostly breathing.

— How — Gadriel starts.

Holt is already wiping the gladius with the same economy he uses for assigned targets.

— SECVNDA — he says. — Pattern before papers. Your scanners read seals. I read the animal wearing them. Next fault.

He walks back into the fortress without waiting for the Chaplains to decide whether this was purity or presumption.

Behind him, Ultramarine security relearns a sentence the *Codex* does not print loudly enough:

sometimes the first Astartes to see the traitor is the one who was never looking for a fair fight —

— only for a human being who forgot how to be imperfect.

## 3 — TERTIA / Torven Ash

The approach ditch was supposed to be empty.

It fills with a sound like wet grain pouring — Rippers, a living carpet with too many mouths — and above that carpet the Biovores take their lazy murderer’s stance on the ridge, abdomens pulsing, spore-mines already in the air like blasphemous fruit.

An Ultramarine squad starts to give ground the correct way: angles, cover, *Codex* withdrawal math.

Torven Ash of TERTIA does not give ground.

He walks into the ditch-mouth and becomes a wall that burns.

— Behind me — he voxes, dry as a chem-seal. — Not beside. Behind. If you breathe my wake, you live. If you outrun my wake, you learn what spore does to lungs.

The first mine drifts toward the ditch like a curious god.

Ash does not shoot it late. He shoots it early — bolt into the sac at the range where the burst becomes a curtain he owns. Flame follows from the pyre-tool on his hip, a controlled kiss that turns the drifting orchard into a burning ceiling. Spores cook midair and fall as dirty snow that cannot bloom.

Rippers hit his greaves in a black tide.

He does not dance. TERTIA hazard-war is not bounce. It is denial. The flamer paints a hard line across the ditch — a bright wall of no — and the carpet meets it, cooks, keeps coming, cooks again. Where the flame cannot reach, the melta shortens a Biovore’s future into slag on the ridge. One. Two. The third learns and tries to arc mines over his shoulder into the Ultramarines.

Ash turns just enough.

Bolter for the mine. Flame for the gap. Body for the ditch.

He is the wall that holds: not a speech, a geometry. Every step back the Guard and blue enamel wanted to take, he spends instead as fuel and timing. Rippers climb him. He scrapes them off with a gauntlet and a short gladius wake, then returns to the burn as if cleaning tools between shifts.

A mine bursts close enough to frost his lenses with toxin glitter.

He keeps the line anyway — seals his grille, switches to external auspex, and widens the fire until the ditch is a kiln. Biovores on the ridge become priority the way wet problems become priority: gone. Without their fruit, the air stops inventing new deaths. Without the carpet’s endless forward, the ditch becomes ground again.

Chairon arrives at the rear lip expecting a collapsed flank and finds a grey figure standing in cooked chitin and ash-snow, flamer still ticking, Ultramarines alive behind him like men who have remembered what a wall is for.

— You could have fallen back to the berm — Chairon says.

Ash checks the ridge once more — empty of Biovores, littered with correct endings — and racks the tool as if boredom were a virtue.

— TERTIA — he answers. — Falling back teaches the swarm where soft begins. I am where soft ends. Next fault.

He walks out of the kiln-ditch without looking for thanks.

Behind him, the approach holds.

Not because the *Codex* drew a prettier box —

— because one seal decided the ditch would remain a wall, and made the spore, the Biovore, and the Ripper pay the invoice.

## 4 — QVARTA / Bram Halder

The choke fails as ground, not as courage.

A trench lip shears under Warrior weight and artillery aftershock. Militarum stretchers tip. An Ultramarine fireteam’s neat box becomes a wound in the earth — a hole the swarm can walk through into the soft rear. Something heavier than a Warrior shoulders the far berm: Carnifex mass, patient as a landslide deciding its schedule.

Men start to invent retreat.

Bram Halder of QVARTA invents a door.

He steps into the break, plants, and locks — Stasis made polite. Storm-shield face to the swarm, boots finding depth the way high gravity taught him manners. The first Warrior that tries the hole meets shield and stops existing as forward motion. The second learns the same invoice. Halder does not chase. QVARTA does not chase. He occupies the failure and refuses to lease it.

— Braces — he voxes, almost gentle. — Behind my plate. Wounded through. No speeches. Move.

Guardsmen scramble timber and wreck-plate into place around his silhouette as if scaffolding a saint. Ultramarines put fire over his shoulders. The Carnifex hits the berm again and the whole choke shudders; Halder shudders with it and stays — a locked fist gripping the blade of the world.

That is when the clever death arrives.

A Lictor does not contest the door. It uses the doorstop. Camouflage bleeds down from the trench lip, claws finding Halder’s pack-spine as ladder, body flowing over planted mass toward the stretcher line — the old assassin’s joke: the immovable man as bridge.

Halder does not unplant.

He kills upward without granting the hole.

Power gladius wakes behind the shield rim, a short reverse cut into the place a Lictor keeps its decisions. Camouflage fails as meat. The creature’s weight becomes a sack of ending across his shoulders; he shrugs it into the trench floor in front of him — extra furniture for the Carnifex to trip on — and resets the shield face as if nothing witty has occurred.

— Rude — he says, only once, to no one in particular.

The Carnifex commits. The hole wants to become a story about grey enamel crushed flat. Halder meets the shoulder-check with storm-shield geometry and the kind of stubbornness that makes high-G worlds produce survivors. Bolts and Guard melta find the beast’s seams while he holds the appointment. When it finally folds, it folds against him like weather giving up.

The braces lock. The choke becomes a choke again.

Halder steps out of the doorway only after the last stretcher clears — polite as a man leaving a chapel — and checks the lip for further cleverness. Finding none, he dims the gladius.

An Ultramarine sergeant stares at the Lictor cooling in the mud, then at the Batavi who used himself as architecture.

— You let it climb you.

— I let it choose a mistake — Halder answers. — QVARTA. Gap closed. Next fault.

He walks toward the next broken piece of ground without hurry.

Behind him, the line holds because someone decided a hole was not an invitation —

— and because the thing that tried to walk over that decision learned Stasis has a blade for guests who skip the door.

## 5 — QVINTA / Rurik Fenndrift

The Ultramarines make the stand correctly.

Bolt rifles braid a box on broken ferrocrete. Raveners try the old knife-trick under the lip and meet combat shields. Gaunts pour like bad weather. Gargoyles stitch the air into shrieking weather of their own. A Sergeant calls ranges as if ranges still own the day.

It is a good stand.

It is also about to become a smaller stand.

Rurik Fenndrift of QVINTA does not join the box.

He arrives as weather with a laugh.

The jump-pack howls — not machine panic, storm language — and QVINTA’s reinforced impact boots hit the absolute center of the gaunt carpet like a invoice stamped in meat. The eviscerator wakes in both hands, a two-handed scream of chain, and Rurik opens a red aisle through living chitin as if clearing brush for a landing zone he has already missed on purpose.

— If you can see the landing zone — he voxes, delighted, to anyone still religious about diagrams — you are already late!

Gaunts climb him. He turns them into wet punctuation. Gargoyles dive; he jump-bursts sideways three meters, boots skidding, eviscerator taking wings and intentions together. Raveners break from the Ultramarine lip to taste the louder prey and find QVINTA already inside their timing — pack hiss, plant, cleave, bounce — laughing through the grille like a man who has found work that fits his illness.

— OCTAVA would hate this view — he tells the swarm conversationally. — Doorstops hate open sky. Me? I invoice gravity. Gravity always pays late!

The Ultramarine box hesitates — doctrine colliding with a grey man who has made their front into his personal Wild Hunt.

Then they use him.

Fire shifts to the edges of his wake. The stand becomes a hinge with a moving blade in the middle.

The Tyranid Prime arrives to correct the insult.

It is synapse with shoulders — bigger decisions, uglier grace. Warriors tighten around it. The air goes intelligent.

Rurik’s laugh thins into a howl that is not worship and not fear — just tempo finding a worthy clock.

— Oh — he says. — A captain. Good. I was getting bored murdering the infantry.

He does not plant like QVARTA. He does not burn like TERTIA. He uses the pack as a lie told at thrust-speed: up, left, skimming gaunt-backs, boots kissing rubble, gone before the Prime’s boneswords finish the sentence they started. Each evasion is an insult. Each return is an eviscerator kiss across tendon and plate-seam. The Prime learns the grey thing is not a target.

It is a rhythm problem.

Rurik chatters between bursts — Batavi commentary as ammunition discipline:

— Your jump is ugly! Mine is certified!  
— Kael says I talk too much — true!  
— Hold still — no, don’t — yes — die properly!

A Ravener almost takes his knee. He stomps it with reinforced sabaton, feels the crunch through the boot-stack, and rides the pack into the Prime’s blind cone again. Gargoyles try to blanket him; he drops through them like a stone that changed its mind mid-fall, chain eating a path back to dirt.

The Prime finally catches his pack-wash and commits a killing arc that would end most Captains’ legends.

Rurik is already under the arc — boots planted for one brutal QVINTA second — eviscerator driven up through the place synapse keeps its pride. He rides the falling mass with a howl that makes Ultramarine vox-clicks go uncertain, then kicks free in a spray of wrong-colored blood and spins the chain dry.

The gaunt ribbon loses its conductor. The stand becomes killing instead of drowning.

Rurik lands among the Ultramarines’ forward marks, eviscerator ticking down from murder-song to idle, jump-pack still panting heat. He tips his helm toward the Sergeant as if they have shared a joke in a mess hall.

— QVINTA — he says, cheerful as a war crime. — Your box was pretty. My hole was prettier. Try not to miss me when I bounce again.

He does not ask for orders.

He is already listening for the next patch of sky that needs a storm in it.

Somewhere on a private channel, if Kael Rheden of OCTAVA is hearing any of this, he is almost certainly suffering.

Rurik howls once more — short, bright, alive — and the Gargoyles left in the air learn that laughter can be a targeting solution.

## 6 — SEXTA / Helmut Eisen

The side chapel annex does not belong to Tyranids.

It belongs to dust that remembers names, and to the wrong blue — not Ultramarine enamel, but the powdered eternity of Rubric Marines advancing in a silence that is not Batavi silence. Chaos Spawn boil at their flanks, meat given too many suggestions. Behind them, Scarab Occult Terminators move like verdicts in gold and blue, sorcerous plate answering Ultramarine doctrine with a older contempt.

An Ultramarine kill-team calls for melta and distance.

Helmut Eisen of SEXTA calls for occupancy.

Terminator mass fills the nave aisle. Storm bolter on one fist, power fist sleeping on the other like a closed door. He does not bounce. He does not burn a ditch. He walks into the Chaos line the way a siege walks into a wall that has forgotten it is temporary.

— Lane is mine — he voxes, once. Not loud. Final.

Spawn reach him first — claws, mouths, ideas. The storm bolter stitches them into wet arithmetic: short bursts, no romance, each shell a brick laid in a new wall of ending. A Spawn tries to climb his sarcophagus plate; the power fist wakes and deletes the climb. Another becomes paste against a pillar. Eisen does not admire the work. SEXTA does not admire. It occupies.

Rubric Marines fire in beautiful unison. Bolts spark across his TDA like weather arguing with a mountain. He answers with storm-bolter volume that turns dust-brothers into collapsing shells — empty armor remembering how to fall. One Rubric plants a bayonet-thought against his knee-seal; Eisen’s power fist closes on its torso and the silence inside the shell ends the only way dust can end: scattered.

The Scarab Occult arrive to make the aisle a theorem.

Inferno combi-bolts. Cursed glyphs. Terminator against Terminator — gold contempt meeting grey invoice. Eisen takes a hit that would fold Tacticus plate and keeps the appointment anyway, storm bolter chewing into occult joints while he closes distance like a vault door deciding to become a weapon. He fist-breaks one Scarab’s helm into the flagstones. A second locks with him, power weapons screaming; Eisen turns the clinch into a throw that uses chapel architecture as an anvil. The third scorches his pauldron with witchfire.

Eisen walks through the witchfire.

Not untouched — scored, smoking — but unleased. The aisle behind him is a wreck of Spawn meat, Rubric empties, and Scarab ruin. Ultramarines at the hatch stop calling for distance. Distance has become optional.

Then the Helbrute comes through the far wall as if walls were a suggestion.

Flesh-machine. Sacrilege engine. A coffin that learned hatred and heavy weapons. Its fist swings a wrecking arc meant to end siege myths; its gun-port coughs enough fire to rewrite a squad.

Eisen plants.

Storm bolter empties into the Helbrute’s face-grille and cable nests — not to kill it clean, to blind its rhythm. The monster’s fist hits his storm-shielded shoulder and the whole annex rings. Eisen slides half a meter, boots carving stone, and stays in the room. QVARTA would admire the plant. SEXTA adds the ending.

He steps inside the Helbrute’s reach — the only safe place against a thing that wants range — and drives the power fist into the sarcophagus seam where man used to be. Once. Twice. The third blow finds the truth under blasphemy and opens it. The Helbrute’s scream is machine and soul disagreeing. Eisen keeps striking until disagreement becomes silence, then rips the murder-limb aside so it cannot fall on the Ultramarines behind him.

Smoke. Incense. Wrong blue dust settling on grey.

Eisen’s storm bolter clicks dry. He reloads without theater, power fist dimming to a pilot light.

A Lieutenant finds his voice.

— SEXTA — you were supposed to be hinge support.

Eisen looks at the hole where a wall used to be, then at the living Ultramarines still owning their chapel.

— Hinges need mass — he says. — I brought mass.

He turns and walks back toward the company lane, Terminator tread counting stone like a metronome that has already decided the argument.

Behind him, Chaos has lost a room.

Not to a speech.

To a seal that treats heresy the way siege treats gates: meet it, occupy it, and leave only wreckage where the door used to argue.

## 7 — SEPTIMA / Orin Vitrael

SEXTA took the nave by mass.

What remains of the Thousand Sons tries the glass galleries above — light, lenses, heat-sinks, a place that punishes careless plasma the way Vitreus trained SEPTIMA to hate amateurs.

Rubric Marines climb the gantries in dust-perfect files. Tzaangors flood the lower catwalks with blades and bird-screams. A Sorcerer in silk and spite lifts his axe and starts rewriting local air into geometry that hates Loyalists.

Ultramarines put bolters on the Rubrics and call for Eisen again.

Orin Vitrael of SEPTIMA does not call for mass.

He unslings the plasma incinerator as if correcting a brother’s bad venting habit, and the gallery becomes his kiln-classroom.

— Overcharge is a tool — he voxes, cold. — Not a personality. Stay out of my bloom.

The first shot is not panic-bright. It is correct: a contained sun that unmakes a Rubric knot mid-file without cooking the Ultramarine marks below. Dust-armor falls as empty costume. Tzaangors leap; Vitrael’s plasma pistol takes the lead beast’s decision-making in a shorter, uglier kiss, then the incinerator paints a heat-wall across the catwalk so the herd meets light and ends.

Witchfire reaches for him — the Sorcerer’s first serious insult.

Vitrael steps into a lens-shadow, lets the beam kiss ferrocrete instead of grey, and answers with a bolt of plasma through the staff-hand’s wrist-seal. The axe-work stutters. SEPTIMA does not duel speeches. It deletes the hand that writes them.

Rubrics adjust fire with cursed patience. Bolts hammer his plate. He accepts scores the way glassworkers accept heat: expected, managed, not romantic. A Scarab Occult survivor from the nave fight — gold and blue, half-ruined, still proud — shoulders onto the gallery and raises inferno muzzles.

Vitrael’s voice stays almost kind.

— Still alive — he tells the Terminator, as he told a serf in a hangar once. — Keep it that way. No.

The incinerator’s second overcharge is a ritual performed without religion: vent-check, lock, release. Scarab plate becomes a lesson in why SEPTIMA exists — energy weapons as modal truth, bolter as failure-mode backbone left holstered because today failure is optional. The Occult drops smoking. Ultramarines flinch from the bloom; Vitrael has already angled it into a heat-sink channel the Magi built for industrial sin, not for Astartes theater.

The Sorcerer completes his spite anyway — a warp-flare meant to cook the gallery’s oxygen into a blue murder.

Vitrael jump-steps — not QVINTA bounce, just enough — into the Sorcerer’s cast-shadow and puts the pistol under the horned helm’s chin-line where armor is an argument and plasma is the verdict. One shot. Clean. The flare dies with its author. Rubrics lose the local leash and become what they always were: dust waiting for a better lie.

Vitrael vents the incinerator with monastic care, watching the gauges like a man who has buried brothers that skipped this second.

A Ultramarine Devastator, lenses still polarized against the afterglow, finds words:

— You fight like a Techmarine pretending to be a killer.

Vitrael racks the weapon home.

— SEPTIMA — he says. — I fight like a killer who refuses to cook his own Chapter. Your gallery still has a ceiling. You’re welcome.

He walks the catwalk through settling dust and ozone, plasma-heart cooling, and does not look back at the Thousand Sons wreckage.

Blue enamel can have the sermons.

SEPTIMA keeps the temperature.

## 8 — OCTAVA / Kael Rheden

The Ultramarines do not see OCTAVA join the fight.

That is the point.

Their remnant is three brothers and a dying fourth, backed into a service throat under a shattered transit spur. Gaunts have already spent themselves on the outer marks. What remains is worse: Lictors in the pipes, a pair of lesser stalker-forms the Militarum call ghost-knives when they are being optimistic, and — somewhere in the wet dark ahead — a Deathleaper wearing patience like a second skin.

The Sergeant puts combat shields into a last box and prepares to die correctly.

Something in the dark dies incorrectly.

A Lictor drops from a duct without completing its drop — throat opened, no bolt-report, no battle-cry, only a soft wet punctuation and a body that was alive becoming furniture. A ghost-knife shrieks once on the left flank and becomes silence mid-shriek. Another Lictor tries to use an Ultramarine’s pack as ladder the way one tried QVARTA; the Ultramarine feels a breeze, smells copper, and finds the climber already sliding off him in two thoughtful pieces.

— Who — a brother starts.

No answer.

Shadows work does not answer.

Kael Rheden of OCTAVA is not on their auspex as a friend-mark. He is black-pauldron absence: knife and gladius, Watch habit, void-corridor manners, moving through the throat’s blind seams where even Tyranid camouflage expects to own the dark. He kills the things that kill quietly — and he kills them quieter. A stalker turns toward a soft Ultramarine breath; Kael is already past its decision, blade through ganglion, body guided to the floor so the thud does not become a lesson for the Deathleaper.

The Ultramarines only notice the census changing.

Threats they had numbered become absences. The dark stops producing new knives. Their dying brother gets one more minute of air because nothing finishes him.

Then the Deathleaper commits.

It has been waiting for the moment blue enamel thins into meat. It unfolds from a wall that was not a wall, talons aimed at the Sergeant’s throat-seal — the legendary end, the pict that Chaplains hate — and for one frank heartbeat the stand is over.

Kael appears into that heartbeat.

Not with a howl. Not with a jump-pack sermon. He simply stops being absent: grey and black, doorstop silhouette materializing on the Deathleaper’s kill-line as if the hinge has always been occupied. Gladius takes the primary tendon. Knife takes the secondary thought. The Deathleaper — creature of perfect ambush — discovers it has been the one walking into a closed case.

It fights anyway. That is what apex stalkers do when embarrassed.

Kael does not give it a duel. He gives it OCTAVA: short violence, no theater, body used as a door against the Sergeant’s chest while blades finish the arithmetic. The Deathleaper’s last camouflage flickers out in ugly honesty. Kael rides the weight down, plants a boot, and checks the throat for further cleverness the way he checks locks.

Empty.

He rises.

Three Ultramarines stare at the Batavi who was not there and then was the only reason they still are.

Kael wipes the knife on the Deathleaper’s own cloak of failed invisibility. Behind him, if anyone had light enough and calm enough, they might count the quiet corpses layered through the ducts — Lictors, ghost-knives, things that never reached the box.

No one has that calm yet.

— OCTAVA — Kael says, voice flat as a sealed hatch. — Your stand was loud. I handled the quiet. Try surviving both next time.

He does not smile. Doorstops do not smile.

He tips his helm a fraction — almost apology for existing late in their story — and melts back toward the next blind seam, Shadows again before pride can turn gratitude into a speech.

The Sergeant looks at the empty dark where grey used to be, then at his living brothers, and understands a new geometry:

sometimes the Victrix-pretender is not the man in the pict mid-swing —

— he is the absence that kept the pict from ending.

## 9 — NONA / Severin Marck

The relay spur is not lost to bolters.

It is lost to voices.

Chaos has seeded the approach with lesser witches — cult psykers burning their own followers for range — and behind them a knot of true Sorcerers, silk and spite, turning loyal vox into suggestions. Ultramarines on the spur start aiming at ghosts. A Sergeant almost orders a charge into an empty lane because something wearing his own voice asked nicely.

Severin Marck of NONA walks into that weather as ballast.

Nullity cobalt at the right pauldron. Bolt rifle for the flesh that still needs holes. Null-rod cells at his hip like quiet threats. He does not howl. He does not burn. He does not bounce. He pins the wrong quiet in place until men can hear their own orders again.

— Ignore the pretty voices — he voxes, flat as threshold law. — Mine is uglier. Obey the ugly one.

A cult witch lifts her hands and the air goes sweet with lies.

Marck’s first bolt takes her before the lie finishes conjugating. The second and third take her choir — not for drama, for signal hygiene. Warp-pressure tries to invent panic in the Ultramarine marks; Marck sets a ward-bead, stands in the pressure like a man standing in weather he has already priced, and the panic finds nowhere to breed.

Then the Sorcerers notice the grey problem.

Witchfire. Force-blades of impossible angle. A telekinetic shove meant to throw NONA into his own allies. Marck rides the shove into a pillar, plants, and answers with Nullity grammar: null-rod wake cutting a casting mid-syllable, bolt rifle stitching the assistant psyker who thought distance was safety. One Sorcerer tries to wear Marck’s face in the Ultramarines’ helm-displays.

Marck kills the face by killing the man.

— Pattern debt — he tells the corpse, almost soft. — Paid.

The spur’s shout-lane — the reason NONA exists in Chapter myth, even here on foreign stone — clears enough for real vox to pass: wounded counts, brace calls, a Captain’s actual order instead of a daemon’s forgery. Ultramarines who were about to invent private wars remember chain of command like drowning men remember air.

The last Sorcerer makes it personal — a blunt warp-lance aimed at Marck’s soul-seam, the kind of strike that turns Astartes into sermons about failure.

Marck steps into it.

Not untouched. Blood in the grille. Lenses flickering. But Nullity is not the absence of pain; it is the refusal to let pain become doctrine. He closes through the lance’s after-image, null-rod cracking the Sorcerer’s focus like a spine, gladius finishing what cobalt started. The warp-noise in the spur drops so hard mortal ears pop.

Silence returns — Batavi silence, not Rubric silence. The useful kind.

Marck resets the ward-bead, checks the Ultramarines for the idiocy that follows psychic weather — brothers staring at empty air, fingers too ready on triggers — and puts a palm between one Sergeant’s shoulder blades the way he guided serfs in a hangar once.

Not blessing.

Ballast.

— NONA — he says. — Your guns were fine. Your thoughts were compromised. I fixed the thoughts. Keep the lane loud with honest orders only.

He walks toward the next place where voices are too beautiful, Nullity kit ticking down like a metronome for reality.

Behind him, the spur holds — not because Chaos lacked power,

— because one seal decided psykers do not get to rewrite the Wall’s chain of command, and collected the invoice in cobalt and blood.
