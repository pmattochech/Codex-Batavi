The room Guilliman chose was unadorned by design.

It was not a chapel, not a strategium, not a gallery built to teach visitors what to feel. It was a transit-cabinet cut into the inner hull of the fortress—an antechamber that smelled faintly of machine oil and cold stone, with a single lumen strip set high and angled away from the eyes. The walls held no banners. No honors. No saints.

Guilliman stood with his back to the door until the locks sealed. Only then did he turn.

Alaric von Helis waited in the center of the chamber the way a load-bearing pillar waits: upright, still, and already accounting for every angle. His helm was on. That fact, by itself, would have meant nothing among the Legiones of old. But Guilliman had read the briefings. He had read the rules this Chapter wrote into itself to remain what it claimed to be.

He had learned, in the most recent age, that a rule is a confession given a spine.

Guilliman folded his hands behind his back. No retinue. No skalds. No scribes.

— You understand why I asked for privacy.

Alaric’s answer came without delay, a flat certainty that made the words feel like a measurement rather than reassurance.

— Yes.

Guilliman watched him for a moment longer than was comfortable. He had spent centuries—two lives’ worth, by some measures—watching men lie, watching them try not to lie, watching them lie to themselves first. The Batavian did not fidget. Did not gild the air with obedience. Did not offer the small social lubricants that made hierarchy feel less like gravity.

He simply remained present.

That, Guilliman found, was already a kind of statement.

— In the old Imperium, he said, a commander’s identity was granted by the legion that made him. Heraldry, chain of command, archives with clean ink. The new Imperium makes identities in the dark. Sometimes by necessity. Sometimes by opportunism.

He let the words hang as if they were a blade and he wanted to see whether the man in front of him would step into it.

Alaric did not move.

— Your Chapter exists.

The words were simple. The weight behind them was not.

— Yes.

— It has a culture. A doctrine. A law-of-self. It has habits severe enough to be mistaken for superstition and precise enough to be mistaken for mathematics.

Alaric’s voice remained spare, unornamented.

— We maintain what holds.

Guilliman had been told the motto. He had read the phrases that circulated at the edge of open record: Unbent. We hold. He had read the internal memoranda too—the ones that did not travel in public, the ones that assumed the reader already accepted the burden.

He had not expected the man himself to speak like the paper.

— You know what my sealed briefings say about you, Guilliman continued.

The helm did not nod. The stillness itself implied assent.

— You know what they suggest, and what they refuse to confirm.

— Yes.

Guilliman stepped to the side, changing the geometry of the room. Not a threat. An instinct. He had learned long ago that the body spoke before the mind chose its politics.

Alaric’s stance did not alter to match. He tracked Guilliman, not with obvious turns, but with the economy of a man who refused to perform awareness.

Guilliman found himself thinking, absurdly, of a fortress that did not waste stones on decoration.

— I have met many kinds of loyalty, he said. I have met men who love the Imperium and therefore obey it. I have met men who hate what surrounds them and therefore cling to duty because it is the last clean thing.

He paused.

— And I have met men who obey because obedience is the only way to avoid becoming something worse.

The lumen strip hummed softly. Somewhere deep in the fortress, a machine cycled.

Alaric answered the way he answered everything: by refusing the lyric and keeping the instrument.

— Discipline prevents drift.

— Drift into what? Guilliman asked.

A fraction. A pause so small it would not have registered to most. Guilliman recognized it anyway: the moment a man decides how much truth the listener can carry.

Alaric chose the minimum that remained honest.

— Into relief.

Guilliman’s gaze sharpened.

— Relief.

— Relief becomes devotion. Devotion becomes permission.

He did not elaborate. He did not need to. Guilliman had read the after-action patterns—units that broke not from fear, but from wanting the equation to end. Wanting an outside force to take responsibility away.

He had seen that same hunger in mortals and Astartes alike. He had simply never seen a Chapter make a cage for it and call the cage a virtue.

— Your affliction, Guilliman said at last.

The words felt inadequate. Affliction sounded like a fever. What the briefings described was a fault line through an entire culture.

— In my age, the Legions had flaws too. Some were loud. Some were private. I have watched flaws become identities and identities become civil wars.

Alaric remained still.

— I am not here to condemn you for what you did not choose, Guilliman said.

Alaric did not reply with gratitude. He did not reply at all. Silence, in him, was not a tactic; it was an element.

Guilliman found that he could not tell whether the silence was mercy or refusal.

Perhaps both.

— I am here, Guilliman continued, to understand whether you have built a wall inside yourself… or only a story about a wall.

Alaric’s answer was immediate, and it was not defensive.

— A wall is procedure. Not belief.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed, not with suspicion but with the kind of focus one gives to a difficult theorem.

— Most of my sons would have said the inverse. Belief first. Then procedure.

— Belief is unstable, Alaric said. Procedure can be audited.

The words had the chill of a scalpel. Guilliman, who had commanded worlds and written codes meant to govern billions, felt a small, involuntary recognition.

He knew the appeal of auditable things.

He also knew their limits.

— Do you trust anyone? Guilliman asked.

It was the first question that stepped fully out of inspection and into the human.

For the first time, Alaric’s stillness shifted—not in the stance, but in the air, as if the room itself had been asked to bear a different kind of weight.

— Yes.

Guilliman waited.

Alaric did not offer names. Guilliman realized, with a sudden clarity, that this man’s intimacy lived behind firewalls of policy.

A leader whose only luxury was to remain functional.

— Your sealed records, Guilliman said carefully, speak of a single place where your tension eases. A domestic node. A human anchor.

Alaric did not correct him. The absence of correction was confirmation enough.

— I will not ask you to explain it, Guilliman added. I do not need the details.

He hesitated, then chose the truth that mattered.

— I need to know whether that anchor is a redundancy… or a single point of failure.

Alaric’s response was not the response of a man defending a secret. It was the response of a man who had already tried to break his own dependency and found the cost unacceptable.

— It is both.

Guilliman exhaled softly. A primarch’s breath did not fog the air, but it carried the faint human sound of fatigue.

— That is what frightens me, he admitted. Not the existence of the anchor. The honesty of the admission.

Alaric’s helm did not tilt.

— Honesty reduces future casualties.

Guilliman almost smiled. Almost.

— You speak like my logisticians.

— Logistics keeps walls alive.

Guilliman walked again, slow. He did not circle Alaric. He did not pace like a judge. He moved like a man thinking with his body because the mind alone was not enough.

— I have been briefed on your campaigns. Your casualty ratios. The way you file deaths. The way you refuse victory language.

He stopped.

— I have read a report that called your Chapter “a wall that bleeds.” It was meant as praise and accusation simultaneously.

Alaric’s reply was quiet.

— Walls bleed when they hold.

Guilliman studied him.

In the old days, he would have compared this man to Dorn out of instinct. The geometry. The refusal of ornament. The calm pressure of a command voice that did not try to be loved.

But Dorn’s sons had always been built in the light of a known father. Their austerity was an inheritance. Their hardness was a tradition.

This was something else.

A culture that had forged austerity as medicine.

— Tell me about the man, Guilliman said finally. Not the commander. Not the Chapter Master. The man.

It was an unfair question. Guilliman knew that even as he asked it. It was a question designed to expose something that might not exist in the way mortals understood it.

Alaric answered anyway.

— The man is the same as the commander.

Guilliman held his gaze on the helm lenses.

— That is not an answer. It is a defense.

Alaric did not deny it.

— The man cannot be permitted to diverge.

— Why? Guilliman pressed.

The silence that followed was longer this time. Guilliman waited without moving, without interrupting, as if time itself were a resource he could afford to spend.

At last, Alaric spoke.

— Divergence becomes indulgence. Indulgence becomes exceptions. Exceptions become permission.

Guilliman felt the words land like stones. He understood the chain. He had written chains like that into law.

He also knew what it cost to live as if you were a statute.

— And if I told you, Guilliman said, that a man who never allows divergence eventually becomes brittle.

Alaric’s answer was not argument. It was simply the bearing of a load.

— Brittleness is preferable to breach.

Guilliman’s eyes softened, a fraction.

— That is Dorn’s logic.

Alaric did not react to the name. No reverence. No rivalry. Just the quiet acceptance that two fortresses could be built from the same principles.

Guilliman let the silence breathe for a moment.

— The briefings call your gene-line “undisclosed.” That is the public language. The sealed language is more careful, more clinical, more afraid of being wrong.

He stopped himself from saying too much. Even in private, even with sealed access, words could become weapons later if repeated.

— You were not made in my image, Guilliman said.

Alaric did not claim otherwise.

— No.

— You were not made in Dorn’s. Not officially.

— No.

— And yet you have built a culture that could stand beside the VIIth and not dishonor itself.

Alaric’s reply was so small it was almost nothing.

— We do not seek honor.

Guilliman nodded once.

— I know.

He stepped closer, stopping at a distance that made the space between them feel deliberate. Not intimate. Not confrontational. An inspection distance that acknowledged the man in front of him could kill him if the universe was perverse enough.

— I am going to give you a constraint, Guilliman said.

Alaric did not flinch. Constraints were the only currency he trusted.

— Your Chapter will be observed. Not because I suspect treason. Because I have seen what unobserved suffering does to institutions. It breeds either pride or collapse.

Alaric’s voice remained level.

— Observation is acceptable.

— You will not be asked to perform, Guilliman added.

He realized, abruptly, how strange that sentence would sound to any other commander.

— Good, Alaric said.

One syllable. A door closing cleanly.

Guilliman felt, unexpectedly, the edge of something like relief.

Not devotion. Not comfort. Something colder and more useful: the sense that, in this fortress, at least one wall knew it was a wall.

He turned toward the door, then paused with his hand near the release rune.

— One more question.

Alaric waited.

Guilliman did not ask about ancestry. Not directly. He did not ask about the sealed architecture. Not explicitly.

He asked the only thing that mattered to him as a ruler who had awoken into an Imperium full of compromises.

— If the Imperium demands something that violates your Chapter’s self-law… what do you do?

Alaric answered without hesitation, as if the question had been carved into him long before Guilliman’s return.

— I hold.

Guilliman looked back at him.

— That is not specific.

Alaric’s voice did not change, but the meaning tightened.

— I hold the Imperium to the constraint that keeps it alive.

Guilliman felt the words settle into place. A dangerous answer. A necessary one.

He nodded once—no flourish, no blessing.

— Then we understand each other.

Alaric did not offer a vow. He did not offer gratitude. He offered the only thing he had that could not be mistaken for performance.

Presence.

— Yes, he said.

Guilliman opened the door.

The corridor beyond was louder, busier, full of the Imperium’s endless machinery and the weight of watchers. For a breath, Guilliman remained in the threshold and let the private air behind him fade.

Then he stepped out, and the locks sealed again, leaving the fortress and the man inside it to do what they had been built to do.

Hold.
