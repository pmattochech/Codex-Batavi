Guilliman did not call the ninth encounter a continuation.

He called it a widening of aperture.

The eighth had shown him how the Batavi argued with their own geometry until the geometry improved. That was internal machinery—necessary, admirable, and dangerous in the way all good machinery is dangerous: it can be mistaken for insubordination by anyone who mistakes obedience for love.

The ninth would show him something else.

Not softness.

Not theater.

The Chapter’s relationship to flesh that was not transhuman: not as pets, not as flock, not as audience. As weight. As schedules. As names that repeated until they became routes in a mind.

He had chosen Khan-Bator Prime first because the dossiers described it in the only language he trusted without poetry: **hazard**. Agri-industrial sprawl under chem moons, oxygen-rich fires waiting for a single mistake to become doctrine, silos tall enough to make gravity feel like an opinion. If the Batavi were going to perform virtue for a primarch, they would perform it where the air could punish pretense.

He arrived without fanfare.

The sky over the transit spine was the color of scorched sugar. Refinery stacks coughed heat into a haze that made distance look like a bruise. Somewhere beyond the mesh, the Nine Phalanx did what it always did: pretended stability was a law rather than a bet.

Theron Brach, Legatus of III Vexillatio — Tertia, was already waiting at the silo spine transfer node.

Not pacing. Not performing patience for a crowd that did not exist. Brach stood like a man who had decided his body would be the first instrument calibrated before anyone else’s pride got involved.

Brach wore his authority like a man wore a respirator: not proudly, but because the alternative was unconsciousness. His armor carried the faint satin sheen of chem-resistant treatments. His helm’s audio grille clicked once—small habit—when his personal auspex ticked ambient oxygen and found it within tolerance. Guilliman noticed it the third time it happened and filed it as discipline made audible.

When Guilliman’s boots met the deck, Brach dipped his helm once—military acknowledgment, not court flourish—then fell in at offset escort distance: close enough to answer, far enough not to crowd a primarch’s killing radius.

They did not move through a parade route.

They moved through a working artery: maglev sidings, silo gates, labor gangs in high-visibility strips, servo-haulers coughing particulate. The sound was not battle. It was worse in a way battle rarely was: battle at least had the honesty of intent. This sound was the Imperium’s appetite turned into vibration.

Guilliman watched the humans work.

He watched the way they did not look at him for long.

Not because they were forbidden.

Because looking at a primarch while carrying a misbalanced load was how spines got ruined.

That, too, was a kind of respect.

Ahead, a Batavi patrol moved against the flow—four battle-brothers in staggered spacing, bolters slung, auspex lenses cycling in slow arcs. Their pace was not parade pace. It was the pace of men who expected the corridor to become wrong without warning.

The lead was not a sergeant showing off for a visitor.

The lead was Brother Idwal Rhys, III Vexillatio — Tertia, ident tags worn where a human eye could read them without having to ask a vox channel for permission.

Rhys did not glance at Guilliman.

He did not glance at Brach.

He glanced at oxygen, at roofline shadow, at the seam where a silo door met ferrocrete—places where failure liked to hide.

Guilliman slowed without deciding to slow.

He had learned, in two lives, that the body sometimes chose wisdom before the mind signed the order.

Rhys’s helm turned a fraction.

Not toward Guilliman.

Toward a mortal.

The man was middle-aged in the way laborers became middle-aged early: compressed discs in the set of his shoulders, sun-and-chem weathering on the backs of his hands, a name patch stitched crooked because tailors were expensive and time was not. The patch read **GANZORIG**.

Erden Ganzorig was dragging a pallet jack with a single overloaded crate bound in chain and waxed canvas. The jack’s wheels had hit a ridge in the decking—some past war’s scar, some maintenance debt, some small cruelty of infrastructure—and the load had begun to yaw. Ganzorig’s boots slid. His jaw set. He did not shout. He did not look around for help.

He simply committed harder, the way men committed when embarrassment was more dangerous than pain.

Rhys stopped.

The patrol stopped with him—not theatrically, not in a cinematic freeze. They stopped the way a machine stops when a sensor trips: clean, immediate, without wasted drama.

Rhys did not speak.

He stepped in, gauntlet closing on the chain where it would not crush fiber, other hand finding the pallet’s leading edge at a vector that turned yaw into alignment. He lifted—not high, not impressive—just enough to take the false pivot out of the deck ridge.

Ganzorig’s eyes widened.

Not in gratitude first.

In the animal recognition of **relief**.

Rhys did not ask permission.

He did not say *let me*.

He did not offer a smile.

He adjusted the load once, checked the jack’s wheels with a nudge of his boot, then released in a controlled sequence that left the crate stable and Ganzorig upright instead of skidding toward a cracked clavicle and a lost shift.

Only then did Rhys speak.

— Wrong line — he said. — You want the secondary track two meters left. Less ridge.

Ganzorig swallowed.

— Yes, lord.

Rhys’s voice stayed flat.

— Not lord. Track.

Ganzorig nodded too fast, then slowed himself as if remembering a supervisor’s lecture.

— Yes, ser.

Rhys stepped back into patrol spacing.

The patrol moved on.

No applause.

No crowd.

No story.

Silence was a tool. Silence made men fill emptiness with truth because lies needed ornament.

Guilliman remained still for two heartbeats longer than dignity preferred.

He had seen Astartes lift loads before. He had seen Astartes lift *worlds* before. This was not strength flexing. This was not kindness performed for witnesses. This was a man stopping his patrol because a familiar route had intersected a familiar failure mode, and the failure mode had a familiar face attached.

Guilliman turned his head slightly.

Brach was already watching—not Rhys, not Ganzorig, but the **geometry** of the interaction: clearance arcs, how much time the stop had cost the patrol, whether the sidings behind them had created a temporary blind pocket.

Brach’s auspex clicked again.

Ganzorig exhaled, adjusted his gloves, and pushed the jack onto the secondary track Rhys had named. The roll smoothed. The laborer’s shoulders dropped a fraction. He did not look at Guilliman at all now—because Rhys had turned the moment back into work, and work was safer than primarchs.

Guilliman waited until the patrol’s tail had crossed the next hazard stripe.

Guilliman let silence do its work.

Then he spoke.

— Explain this — he said.

Brach did not pretend confusion.

He did not ask *which part*.

He answered the way a Legatus answered when he knew his listener could smell evasion.

— You saw a brother interrupt patrol to correct mass on a deck, my lord — Brach said. — You also saw him do it without being asked. Ganzorig works this silo line eleven months a year. His crew feeds the transit spine. Rhys has walked this patrol for three years. They are not friends. They do not eat together. They do not tell stories. No, my lord, they are **known**. Known means predictable failure. Predictable failure is a liability we can budget. Unknown failure is what kills harvest windows.

Guilliman listened to the word *budget* applied to a human spine.

He did not flinch.

He had been alive long enough to know the Imperium always budgeted flesh. The question was whether the budget was honest.

— Is this a Khan-Bator dialect of duty — Guilliman said quietly. — Or Chapter law everywhere, merely spoken with your accent.

Brach’s posture tightened by a hair—discipline, not defiance. A Legatus did not forget what stood beside him.

— By your leave, my lord — Brach said. — I cannot speak for another seat’s air. I can speak for **this** air. On Khan-Bator, chemistry and harvest windows punish sentiment faster than heresy trials punish sinners. What you saw was not kindness. It was **risk accounting** wearing a human face because the human face is where the failure mode lives. We train it as **telemetry**, not as affection. Rhys did not stop because he likes Ganzorig. He stopped because a sprained loader becomes a delayed train, which becomes an empty silo, which becomes a hungry corridor, which becomes a riot, which becomes a purge. The Chapter pays for purges in time we do not have.

He did not step back.

He did not look away.

He still chose his next word as if it could burn him.

— If I have misspoken, my lord — Brach added. — Correct me. I would rather be corrected than be smooth.

Guilliman felt the corner of his mouth threaten something that was not quite a smile.

He killed it.

Management was also a kind of love, in the Imperium. He could not afford to enjoy being disarmed.

— And if Rhys had not known him — Guilliman asked.

Brach’s reply was immediate.

— Then Rhys still stops — Brach said. — If the load threatens a corridor casualty window. The difference is speed. Familiarity removes questions. Unfamiliarity forces questions. Questions cost seconds.

Guilliman turned fully toward Brach now, the movement deliberate—not threat, but the primarch’s version of leaning over a map.

He did not hand Brach a ladder to salute.

He did not teach the Batavi their own catechism.

— You are describing ordered obligation — Guilliman said. — I will not put words in your mouth. Give me the Chapter file. How *you* stack it when the corridor narrows.

Brach’s helm dipped—fractional, involuntary respect breaking through drill.

— Aye, my lord — Brach said. — Neophytes learn it as **internal** filing, not as a sermon for outsiders. First: live brothers and the sealed operation—because dead heroes do not hold silos. Second: the Vexillatio objective and the corridor window we are sworn to keep open—mass in motion, not glory. Third: the labor lattice under our writ—the nodes that feed the mesh we depend on, because if they starve, we bleed next week. Fourth: **named** continuity—faces we can predict, failures we can price before they become casualties.

Guilliman held the silence again.

This time Brach did not fill it.

Good.

Guilliman wanted to see whether Brach could tolerate being audited without compensating.

— This is imprint — Guilliman said at last.

Not a question.

A classification.

Brach’s voice tightened by one increment—still not anger, but the heat Brach carried under ice.

— My lord — Brach said. — If that word leaves this corridor unchanged, outsiders file it as *favoritism*. Inside Khan-Bator, we hear *continuity*. Ganzorig is not family. He is a node. Rhys is not his protector. Rhys is a moving seal on a pressure system.

Guilliman nodded once.

He filed the correction without accepting it wholesale.

Words were politics.

Behavior was evidence.

— I will use the word I need — Guilliman said — until I understand whether your continuity is discipline… or a cult with better logistics.

Brach’s helm turned toward him.

For a heartbeat, the Legatus’s posture was not a courtier’s posture.

It was a soldier’s—then Brach checked it, as if remembering gravity.

— My lord — Brach said. — If you must test us, test us by outcomes. Not by whether we perform tenderness for a witness.

His voice did not soften.

It simply remembered rank.

Guilliman did not answer with argument.

He answered with motion.

He walked the secondary track Ganzorig had taken, eyes on the deck ridge, measuring the scar with his boot’s edge as if he could feel the Imperium’s deferred maintenance through ceramite.

When he looked up again, the patrol was a line of shoulders at the silo’s vanishing point.

Rhys never turned.

Guilliman spoke to Brach without looking at him.

— I will weigh what I saw here against what I see elsewhere — he said. — Without pretending the comparison is fair to you.

Brach’s auspex clicked.

— As you command, my lord — Brach said. — Khan-Bator does not require fairness. It requires **accuracy**.

Guilliman exhaled once, slow, controlled.

He had wanted the ninth encounter to widen aperture.

Instead, it had widened something less comfortable.

**Doubt about his own vocabulary.**

Not doubt about Batavi loyalty.

Doubt about whether the High Lords’ categories—mercy, favoritism, duty—could still map cleanly onto a Chapter that treated love like oxygen:

Invisible until missing.

Measurable when it killed you.

He walked on toward the next transit gate, toward the rest of a day that would not stay on Khan-Bator forever.

Aethelgard smelled like lightning stored in cloth.

Not metaphor. Ozone stitched into damp hemp and salt crust, a planet that had decided weather was a weapon it would teach you to respect instead of enjoy. Guilliman descended from orbit the way he always descended when he meant business: without parade, without escort wings meant to look like wings. The sky over the landing shelf was bruised violet. Wind came in counted pulses, as if the atmosphere itself had learned to speak in cadence.

Armin Sturmwahl, Legatus of V Vexillatio — Quinta, was already on the shelf when Guilliman’s boots touched ferrocrete.

Helm on. Hands empty. Weight shifted slightly into the wind as if the planet were a drop zone and he refused to be the variable that slipped.

Sturmwahl did not waste breath on welcome. He wasted breath on **readiness**—the small sub-audible check of his grav-comp harness before every transition, the habit of a man who had turned survival into metronome. His armor’s crimson trim was scuffed at the edges, kissed by hulls and gantries and honest impact. When he moved, he moved like closure speed made flesh.

— My lord — Sturmwahl said. — Aethelgard stands ready. We do not polish grief here. We weigh it.

Guilliman inclined his head once.

Sturmwahl fell in at escort offset—same measured distance Guilliman had already seen on Khan-Bator, different planet, same refusal to crowd a primarch’s line.

They did not go to a fortress gallery.

They went to the shore.

The tide flats stretched like a ledger written in wet ink: channels, ribs of black stone, longboats pulled high enough to survive the next hungry rise. Mortals moved among the boats with the economy of people who had learned that drama was a luxury and timing was not. Their skin carried ritual striping—white ash and blue salt—meant to be read at a distance: **who** belonged to **which** line, which family owed which boat, which mouth spoke law when the storm came.

In the middle of it stood a Batavi battle-brother without ceremony.

Brother Rurik Degen, V Vexillatio — Quinta, helm on, bolter mag-locked to his back like an admission that this was not a range day. He did not loom over the mortals. He did not instruct them as if they were children. He stood where the elders told him to stand, feet in the surf line, and accepted a coil of harpoon line with the same attention he would have given a det-cord spool.

The creature came later.

First came the **marking**.

An old woman—face carved by sun and squint, hands steady—pressed a braid of soaked fiber into Degen’s gauntlet until the weave bit into ceramite knuckles. She spoke a name Guilliman did not know. The crowd answered it in chorus, not loud, not worshipful. The sound was closer to an acknowledgement stamped into wood.

A younger hunter pressed a smear of fat and ash along Degen’s pauldron, following a pattern older than the Imperium’s claim on the mesh. The marine did not flinch. He did not perform humility. He simply remained still while the pattern was completed, because flinching would have been insult, and insult would have been a failure mode.

Guilliman watched the Batavi become **included**.

Not adopted.

Not humanized.

Included—like a load-bearing member slotted into a truss.

When the boats slid out, the mortals did not treat Degen as a god.

They treated him as **extra weight they could use**—a fact more intimate than prayer. Degen took an oar station without being asked. His strokes did not show off strength. They matched the count the coxswain called, because mismatch was how spines snapped when the sea turned mean.

The leviathan analog broke the surface miles out: a long, wrong-backed silhouette, barnacle-armored, breath like a bellows exhalation that turned spray into mist. Guilliman’s mind catalogued it without romance—**mass**, **momentum**, **kill radius**, **civilian hull integrity**. The mortals catalogued it as **Ash-Back**, a name that sounded like weather and debt.

The hunt was not a duel.

It was a **procedure** written in rope and blood.

Lines went taut. Boats slewed. Someone sang a counting song and the song was not pretty. Degen’s station became the pivot when the creature’s tail tried to capsize the center hull; the marine threw his weight into the geometry the way Rhys had thrown his weight into a pallet jack—without announcement, without pride, with the horrible precision of a man who understood leverage as morality.

When the killing thrust came, it was not clean.

It was never clean on Aethelgard.

Guilliman watched Degen take the spear-path the elders opened for him—not because he was the hero, but because he was the **most likely to survive failure** and therefore the least immoral choice under time pressure. Blood misted. The sea tried to claim its due. The mortals worked the lines like a machine made of prayer and muscle.

When Ash-Back finally stilled, the silence was not victory silence.

It was **bookkeeping** silence.

The old woman touched Degen’s helm with two fingers—light, almost obscene in its gentleness compared to the violence behind them.

— Line holds — she said.

Degen answered in High Gothic, quiet, flat.

— Line holds.

Guilliman did not speak during the return.

He let the boats come in under a sky that looked ready to punish relief.

Only when the hull kissed shale and the mortals began their unloading math did Guilliman turn his head toward Sturmwahl.

Guilliman let silence do its work.

Then he spoke.

— Explain this — he said.

Sturmwahl’s harness gave its tiny readiness hum, involuntary, as if the Legatus’s body refused to stop being honest.

— By your leave, my lord — Sturmwahl said. — You saw a brother take witness debt in public. Not as charity. As **closure**. Aethelgard does not file “kindness.” We file **line-debt**. The tribe marks him because his failure would kill *their* children first. His success buys their next season. The ritual is not cosplay. It is a contract written where Administratum ink does not stick.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— When the boats are dry and the tide politics return — Guilliman said. — What survives of this binding? Ritual ash… or obligation.

Sturmwahl’s voice stayed clipped, but it did not forget rank.

— By your leave, my lord — Sturmwahl said. — On Aethelgard, what survives is **expectation**. The tribe does not keep songs about your kindness. They keep count of whether the giant returns when the sea turns wrong. If Degen vanishes after the kill, they will name him coward in the only language that matters here: **absence**.

— You bind him to mortality with ceremony — Guilliman said.

Sturmwahl did not step back.

— We bind him to **predictability** — Sturmwahl said. — Mortals predict what they can name. If Degen is named into the line, they will shout at him when he drifts. They will shame him when he reaches for glory. They will treat him like family in the only way that matters on a tide moon: **they will expect him to show up**.

Guilliman listened to *expect* as a threat and a gift at once.

Sturmwahl’s harness gave its tiny hum again—honesty, not theater.

— I do not speak for forge seats — Sturmwahl said. — I do not speak for chem silos. I know what *Quinta* buys with blood in the surf.

Guilliman looked back toward Degen.

The battle-brother was helping drag a wet-wrapped cut of meat up the shale—**not** the trophy strip for officers, not the pretty proof. The work piece. The part that fed the line.

Rhys had not looked back at a primarch.

Degen did not look at Guilliman either.

Guilliman filed that repetition like evidence.

— And if the ritual becomes worship — Guilliman said.

Sturmwahl’s reply came without hesitation.

— Then we burn the pattern and write a new one — Sturmwahl said. — My lord, Aethelgard already burned us once. We do not confuse love with obedience. We confuse love with **showing up**.

Guilliman exhaled once, slower than before.

The ninth encounter had widened again.

Not because Aethelgard had answered every question.

Because Aethelgard had answered Khan-Bator in a different tongue—and still sounded like the same Chapter.

Noviomagus did not greet Guilliman with weather.

It greeted him with **grey**.

Grey sky pressed against grey stacks. Grey dust found ways to enter seals that had been engineered by proud men who believed seals were perfect. Forges coughed rhythm into the city the way hearts coughed rhythm into ribs—constant, unromantic, too honest to be called music.

Corbec Ardens, Legatus of I Vexillatio — Prima, was already waiting at the mouth of the workers’ strip—still as a datum line, *Alabarda* presence reduced to its smallest honorable shape: no retinue, no herald, no excuse.

Corbec did not offer tour-guide enthusiasm. He offered **presence** the way a gauge offers presence: steady, minimal, already oriented to throughput.

— My lord — Corbec said. — Noviomagus is ready. If you want sentiment, this strip will disappoint you. If you want **why we stay**, follow the small motions.

Guilliman inclined his head once.

Corbec fell in at escort offset, the same measured distance Guilliman had already catalogued twice—discipline filed across worlds without looking like a rehearsed performance.

They did not go to the high galleries.

They entered the workers’ strip where hab blocks leaned close enough to share shadow: wash-lines, shuttered vendors, a child’s chalk battlefield drawn on ferrocrete where promethium stains had been scrubbed until only ghosts remained.

Brother Seren Kael, I Vexillatio — Prima, was not performing for a crowd.

He was not even performing for the primarch.

Helm on, he knelt on one knee in the street with the same economy he would have used clearing a weapon jam. In his gauntlet he held a **wooden sword**: not a toy shop’s bright lie, but a carved plank shaped like a blade, edges softened, grip wrapped in cord that had been oiled until it darkened.

Across from him stood a boy—thin shoulders, older eyes, hair cut badly by a tired hand. The child’s name patch on his coat had been stitched twice, the second pass correcting a backwards letter like someone had learned literacy under pressure.

**HART** — and below it, smaller, the word **GUARD** stamped into the fabric by a machine that did not care about grief.

The boy’s name was **Elias Hart**.

Elias held his own stick—shorter, cruder—like a soldier trying to remember a father who had left on a tithe ship and would not send letters that made sense.

Kael did not say *be brave*.

He did not say *your father is a hero*.

— Distance first — Kael said. — Measure with your feet before you dream with your hands.

Elias swallowed and nodded too fast, then slowed himself as if he had been corrected before.

Kael tapped the chalk line with the wooden sword’s tip.

— Here is your edge — Kael said. — Not the enemy. The edge of what you can afford to lose. If you step past it, you do not become brave. You become **wrong**.

Guilliman watched the street breathe around them.

Mortals pretended not to watch. They failed in the way mortals always failed: eyes kept returning, mouths kept stilling mid-sentence, hands kept working slower on bundles and latch-keys.

No one genuflected.

No one cheered.

That mattered.

Kael moved Elias through footwork with the patience of a man teaching a reload sequence—repeatable, boring, survivable. When the boy’s wrist rolled too wide, Kael corrected with two fingers, not a shout. When Elias overreached, Kael stopped him with the flat of the wooden blade against the boy’s shoulder—not pain, **boundary**.

— Again — Kael said.

Elias breathed in through his nose like a child pretending he was not about to cry.

— Again — Kael said, softer. — Not because I like repeating myself. Because repetition is how you keep your father’s name from becoming a story that kills you.

The sentence landed without theater.

It still hurt.

Guilliman felt the hurt as data.

He did not interrupt.

When Kael finally let Elias stop, the boy looked up—once—toward Guilliman’s silhouette, then flinched as if he had touched a hot rail.

Kael’s helm turned just enough to put himself between the child’s eyes and the primarch without making it a dramatic shield.

— Eyes on me — Kael said. — The street is full of things you cannot afford to look at yet.

Elias obeyed.

Kael stood, wooden sword offered back to the boy’s hands handle-first, the way you handed a weapon to someone you intended to **survive**.

— Practice the steps — Kael said. — Ten counts tonight. No more. Fatigue makes lies.

Elias nodded.

Kael walked away without waiting for gratitude.

He did not look at Guilliman.

Guilliman let silence do its work.

Then he spoke.

— Explain this — he said.

Corbec’s voice stayed low.

— By your leave, my lord — Corbec said. — You saw a brother invest time where time is the scarcest coin. Not because the child is cute. Because the child is a **node** in a lattice we need stable. Father gone to Guard tithe means mother working double shifts, which means latchkey, which means street, which means **recruitment drift**, which means cult mouth tomorrow. We do not save souls on Noviomagus. We save **schedules**.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— If a Munitorum auditor heard you — Guilliman said. — He would file this as recruitment grooming.

Corbec’s helm dipped—fractional respect, not submission theater.

— By your leave, my lord — Corbec said. — Let him file it. *Prima* files it as **stability spend**. We do not buy the boy’s love. We buy his mother’s week. We buy the street’s silence. We buy one fewer knife drawn over a rumor. If that looks like grooming, then the Imperium’s categories are blind to how Noviomagus eats itself when we are not looking.

— You weaponize compassion — Guilliman said.

Corbec did not flinch.

— We weaponize **continuity** — Corbec said. — My lord, *Prima* files this under **billet truth**. The wooden blade is not a gift. It is a **tool** that keeps a boy’s hands busy with something other than myth. Myth kills faster than knives here.

Guilliman listened to *billet truth* and heard the Chapter’s private language doing what private languages always did: making ethics sound like logistics so logistics could stay ethical.

Guilliman looked down at Elias.

The boy was already practicing the first step in the chalk square, counting under his breath, trying not to look at the giants again.

Guilliman filed the image without blessing it.

He had seen enough worlds to know that inventory could be mercy.

He had seen enough worlds to know that mercy could become a **quota**.

He walked on, and the grey of Noviomagus followed him like a second sky.

The workers’ strip gave way to stairs that did not believe in comfort.

Guilliman descended into heat that had weight—forge underlevels where the city’s true pulse lived, where lumen strips fought soot and lost, where every surface wore a film of metal dust that turned sweat into paste. Corbec did not narrate the transition. He simply moved as if the route were a supply line and silence were part of its clearance.

At the mouth of a wide bay marked only by a stamped plate of regulations nobody read aloud, **Kadmos** was already at work.

Master of the Forge. Mark III bulk scuffed to honest dullness. Mecadendrites folded behind his shoulders like tired predators, one limb extended with a jeweler’s insulting gentleness, the bronze prosthesis catching furnace light without flaunting it. He did not look up at Guilliman first.

He looked at **hands**.

The Ogryn’s name was **Gromm**—Munitorum issue tags on a rebreather harness, knuckles like hammer heads, eyes small and earnest in a face built for breaking doors. Gromm held tongs the way a man held a baby he feared he would crush: too much strength, too little geometry.

Kadmos tapped the tongs once—tap, not slap—aligning the bite on a practice billet that would never become a blade worth naming.

— Heat first — Kadmos said. — Color tells you when the metal is honest. Sound tells you when it lies.

Gromm swallowed.

— Want learn — Gromm rumbled. — Want make good.

— Then listen with your hands — Kadmos said. — Not with your pride.

He guided the lift—slow, incremental—until the billet’s skin began to change the way dawn changed when you watched long enough to admit it was not instant. Gromm’s breath hitched. Kadmos stopped the motion with a single raised finger, not touching the Ogryn, not shaming him.

— There — Kadmos said. — That is the window. Narrow. Expensive. Do not blink through it.

Gromm did not blink.

He sweated instead, salt cutting tracks through forge grime.

Kadmos let him hold the tongs alone for three heartbeats—long enough to be trust, short enough to be control—then corrected with two words and a micro-shift of wrist angle.

— Again — Kadmos said.

— Again — Gromm rumbled, like an oath he had been taught to repeat until it became muscle.

Mortal forge adepts moved around them in a wide berth, not from fear of the Ogryn alone, but from the same instinct that kept men from walking through a primarch’s sightline when they still had fingers they liked. Guilliman stood in shadow and let the lesson continue until the billet cooled enough to be quenched without screaming.

Steam rose.

Gromm flinched at the hiss, then laughed once—ugly, surprised—when nothing exploded.

Kadmos allowed himself one nod.

— Good — Kadmos said. — Not finished. Good.

Only then did the Master of the Forge turn his head toward Guilliman and Corbec.

His posture remembered rank before his mouth did.

— My lord — Kadmos said. — If this is an inspection, file what you see. If it is a visit, do not make him perform for you. He is not a trick.

Guilliman let silence do its work.

Then he spoke.

— Explain this — he said.

Corbec’s voice stayed low.

— By your leave, my lord — Corbec said. — You saw **Armourium time** spent where most Chapters would spend **none**. Gromm is sanctioned labor on this floor. Untrained Ogryn strength breaks presses, warps rails, kills mortals when a billet slips. Trained strength is cheaper than dead adepts and cheaper than halted output. Kadmos is not teaching him fairy tales. He is teaching him **tolerance windows**.

Guilliman’s eyes moved to Kadmos.

— You speak to your weapons as companions — Guilliman said. — Do you speak to him the same way?

Kadmos did not smile.

— I speak to him like a machine that can learn — Kadmos said. — My lord, Gromm wants the work. Want is a torque vector. It can tighten a line… or snap it. My job is to keep the torque pointed into the forge instead of into someone’s skull.

Gromm looked between them, confused by High Gothic speed, but not confused by tone. He straightened as if standing at attention might make him smaller.

Guilliman considered the political smell of it—abhuman hands on a Chapter-adjacent floor, a primarch watching, an Administratum tag still stamped on a harness.

— If a Mechanicus quaesitor saw this — Guilliman said. — He would file it as tech-heresy flirtation.

Corbec’s helm dipped.

— By your leave, my lord — Corbec said. — Let him file it. *Prima* files it as **throughput insurance**. The Mechanicus liaison signs what must be signed. The Ogryn signs with sweat. Kadmos signs with hours. If that looks like heresy, then the Imperium’s categories are blind to how Noviomagus keeps its fists closed around survival.

Guilliman looked back at Gromm.

— And if he fails — Guilliman said.

Kadmos answered without heat.

— Then we do what we always do — Kadmos said. — We stop the line before the line stops us. My lord, failure here is not moral. It is **metallic**. It makes noise before it makes martyrs.

Gromm swallowed.

— Gromm not fail — Gromm said. — Gromm **learn**.

Kadmos’s mecadendrite lifted a fraction, not threatening—directing.

— Then pick up the file — Kadmos said. — Not the fantasy. The file.

Guilliman filed the scene the way he filed battle maps: not as proof of kindness, not as proof of cruelty, but as proof that the Batavi would spend **scarce genius** on problems other Astartes would dismiss as beneath them.

He walked out of the heat without offering benediction.

The grey followed him again.

But now it smelled like metal, too.

---

The ninth encounter’s field audit ended where audits were supposed to end: not in applause, not in confession, but in **weight carried long enough to become habit**.

Guilliman had watched chem silos, tide blood, chalk squares, and forge tongs. He had watched the Batavi spend time the way other institutions spent ammunition—deliberately, resenting waste, refusing to pretend that love and logistics were different species when the mesh was hungry.

What remained was not sentiment.

What remained was **a question too large for any Legatus to answer alone**.

He turned his steps toward the corridors that did not belong to labor registries—toward the gold that did not ask permission to be heavy.

Somewhere ahead, the Throne’s sentinel waited with a century’s worth of silence already filed.

The inspection was not finished.

It had only reached the door where **verdict** lived.
