The Strategium was not built to hold tenderness.

It was built to hold pressure.

Maps layered over maps. Realspace vectors. Supply corridors. Attrition curves. The kind of data that made mercy look like a rounding error. Even the air felt regulated — cool, dry, scrubbed of anything that might carry scent or comfort too far.

Guilliman dismissed the chamber.

Not with a flourish. Not with the voice of a primarch who expects obedience to echo. He dismissed it the way a commander closes a bulkhead: because the work on the other side required silence.

When the last footfall faded and the doors sealed, he did not sit.

He did not ask Alaric to sit.

Alaric stood helmed, still as a fixed point on a chart. The iron economy of his posture had become familiar now: a man who refused to spend motion on reassurance.

Guilliman watched the maps for a moment, then spoke without turning.

— Your Chapter invites outsiders.

Alaric answered.

— Yes.

Guilliman turned then, eyes clear.

— You invite them not as guests.

A pause.

— As instruments.

Alaric did not deny it.

— As teachers. As witnesses. As constraints.

Guilliman nodded once. The sealed briefs had called it exchange, but the way the Batavi practiced it was closer to controlled exposure — deliberate, audited contact with other cultures, taking what survived scrutiny and discarding the rest.

— Salamanders — Guilliman said.

Alaric’s reply came without pride.

— Yes.

Guilliman’s gaze tightened.

— Why them.

Alaric did not reach for ideology.

— Because they keep the human without calling it weakness.

The words landed quietly and still managed to change the room. Guilliman felt the shift, not in the air but in the shape of the conversation. The Strategium did not like this subject. That was precisely why it mattered.

— Many Chapters claim that — Guilliman said. — Few survive it.

Alaric answered.

— They survive it.

Guilliman stepped closer, inspection distance again, but the inspection was not of numbers this time.

— And what do you take from them.

Alaric’s helm remained forward.

— Heat.

Guilliman’s brow moved a fraction.

— Explain.

Alaric’s voice stayed spare.

— Not fury. Not devotion. Heat as endurance without numbness.

Guilliman held his gaze.

— And what do you give.

Alaric answered after a pause — long enough to feel like a gate being opened reluctantly.

— Silence.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— Silence is not a gift.

Alaric did not react.

— It is when the enemy is inside the self.

Guilliman’s expression tightened. He understood. He also distrusted how cleanly the sentence fit. Clean sentences were dangerous. They tempted men to stop thinking.

— Your exchanges are a controlled infection — Guilliman said.

Alaric did not deny it.

— A controlled exposure.

Guilliman nodded once.

— You are building a Chapter that can metabolize other cultures.

Alaric answered.

— We absorb what proves its valor.

— And you do not fear dilution — Guilliman pressed.

Alaric’s reply came like a line on a map.

— We fear stagnation more.

Guilliman let the words settle, then moved to the second subject without warning — because warning gave men time to armor their lies.

— Martha.

Alaric did not move.

Guilliman watched him carefully.

— Your fortress has a kitchen that appears in sealed reports with the weight of a shrine.

Alaric did not correct the implication.

He answered with the smallest possible truth.

— It is a human place.

Guilliman’s gaze sharpened.

— Most Chapters do not allow “human places” to sit at their center.

Alaric’s voice remained level.

— Most Chapters do not carry what we carry.

Guilliman nodded once. A primarch’s nod was not agreement. It was an admission that the datum existed.

— It is a risk — Guilliman said. — A leverage point.

Alaric did not deny it.

— Yes.

Guilliman’s voice hardened.

— Then why keep it.

Alaric answered with a sentence so bare it almost sounded like surrender.

— Because it works.

Guilliman held still.

— That is not enough.

Alaric’s helm turned a fraction — minimal motion, but enough to make the next words land with intent rather than procedure.

— It is the only place where our restraint is not mistaken for hatred.

The Strategium’s hum seemed louder for a moment, as if the machine-spine of the fortress disapproved of the confession.

Guilliman did not soften.

— You are describing morale.

Alaric replied without flinching.

— I am describing literacy.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— Explain.

Alaric’s voice stayed spare, but the meaning deepened.

— Astartes are not readable to mortals by default. We become readable there.

Guilliman felt something shift inside him — an old memory of the Great Crusade, of compliance worlds that mistook silence for cruelty, of legions that believed the solution was spectacle.

— And you allow this readability to exist in one room — Guilliman said.

— Yes.

Guilliman’s tone tightened.

— That is a single point of failure.

Alaric answered immediately.

— Yes.

Guilliman stared at him.

— You are unusually honest about your failure points.

Alaric’s reply was flat.

— Honesty reduces future casualties.

Guilliman accepted the answer because he had learned, painfully, that denying a failure point did not remove it. It only made the collapse more theatrical.

He moved to the third subject as if turning a blade in a wound he had already decided must be cleaned.

— Elara Solis.

Alaric did not move.

The helm made him unreadable, and yet Guilliman had learned to read the economy of stillness: what remained locked because opening it would cost too much.

Guilliman spoke anyway.

— A human with Astartes-level authority.

A pause.

— The Imperium is not designed for that.

Alaric’s voice did not change.

— The Imperium is not designed for what we became.

Guilliman’s eyes tightened.

— That is not an answer.

Alaric gave him one.

— She is the translation layer.

Guilliman’s gaze sharpened.

— Between what and what.

Alaric’s reply came after a pause that felt like restraint imposed on something that wanted to speak too much.

— Between the wall and the people who live behind it.

Guilliman held still. He had seen translation layers before: remembrancers, iterators, compliance officials. He had also seen what happened when the translator became an idol.

— That is a risk — Guilliman said.

Alaric answered.

— Yes.

Guilliman stepped closer.

— You notice a pattern?

Alaric did not answer. Guilliman did not wait for permission to press the point.

— You keep saying “yes” when I name the risk.

He watched Alaric.

— And you keep the risk anyway.

Alaric answered with a truth that did not try to be noble.

— Because there is no safe configuration.

Guilliman’s jaw tightened. There it was — the central cruelty of the era he had awoken into. Not good choices and bad choices. Only choices that kept something alive and choices that killed it cleanly.

— Viggo — Guilliman said.

Alaric’s stillness tightened. It was small. It was enough.

Guilliman’s voice lowered.

— Your records treat him as anchor.

Alaric replied.

— Yes.

— Why.

Alaric’s answer was quiet.

— He carried the human continuity when we could not.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— “Could not,” Alaric.

He let the words hang.

— Or “would not.”

Alaric answered without defensiveness.

— Both.

Guilliman stood with that for a moment, then spoke with the cold clarity of a man who had once built an empire out of systems and now had to accept that systems did not keep souls intact.

— You have built a Chapter that survives by regulation, redundancy, and controlled absorption.

He looked past Alaric to the maps, as if speaking to the fortress as much as the man.

— And yet you keep a kitchen.

A pause.

— You keep a family.

Another pause, quieter.

— You keep a human authority node.

The Strategium did not change. Guilliman did. The slightest shift of posture, the smallest human admission: that he was not only judging whether the structure could stand, but whether it deserved to.

— Tell me the truth — Guilliman said. — Is it doctrine.

Alaric did not answer immediately.

Guilliman waited.

At last, Alaric spoke, and the answer did not sound like procedure. It sounded like a man trying to remain lawful while saying something that could be used against him.

— It is a refusal.

Guilliman’s eyes sharpened.

— A refusal of what.

Alaric’s voice remained low.

— A refusal to become only a machine.

The words sat in the air, dangerous because they were not a slogan. They were too specific to be safe.

Guilliman nodded once.

— That is Salamanders.

Alaric did not deny it.

— Yes.

— And it is also you — Guilliman added, more quietly than before. — Despite everything.

Alaric’s helm remained forward.

— Yes.

Guilliman’s gaze tightened with something that was not quite approval and not quite fear.

— You said earlier your Chapter is contagious.

Alaric did not react.

Guilliman continued.

— This is what truly spreads.

He gestured, not to the maps, but to the absence in the room — the invisible architecture of kitchen, family, and human translation that the Strategium could not model.

— Not your numbers. Not your silence. Not your wall.

His voice lowered.

— The insistence that the wall still serves people.

Alaric answered with the simplest sentence he had.

— Yes.

Guilliman held the silence for a moment longer, then delivered his constraint — not as threat, but as the only form of care he trusted.

— Your exchanges will continue.

Alaric did not move.

— Your kitchen will remain untouched by policy — Guilliman said. — Not because I romanticize it.

A pause.

— Because I will not be the man who turns your refusal into a doctrine of punishment.

Alaric’s voice was quiet.

— Understood.

Guilliman stepped toward the door, then stopped with his hand near the release rune.

— One last question.

Alaric waited.

Guilliman did not ask about logistics. He did not ask about casualty ratios. He did not ask about primarch lessons or gene-line rumors.

He asked about the man again, because in the end that was the only datum that decided whether all the rest was fortress or prison.

— Do you love them.

Silence.

Not long. Not theatrical.

Long enough for a decision.

Alaric spoke.

— Yes.

Guilliman did not smile. He did not soften. He simply nodded once, as if marking the answer into an internal ledger he would carry into future audits.

— Then do not let them become your excuse — Guilliman said.

Alaric answered with a vow that refused ornament.

— They are my constraint.

Guilliman opened the door.

The Strategium’s noise returned — the measured roar of an Imperium that never stopped bleeding.

He stepped out carrying no comfort.

Only a final, dangerous proof: that the Batavian wall did not stand for itself. It stood for what it refused to erase.
