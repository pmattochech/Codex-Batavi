Guilliman chose the third room for its visibility.

Not visibility to an audience — there was none. Visibility to the fortress itself.

It was a small strategium annex with a glassed-in datum wall that displayed nothing sacred: only the things an empire pretends are neutral because they are “just numbers.” Rotations. Resupply bands. Wake windows. Repair cycles. A ledger of what had been spent and what remained.

No banners. No altar. No comfort.

When the locks sealed, Guilliman did not turn immediately.

He watched the wall for a full breath, as if letting arithmetic speak first.

Alaric stood behind him, helm on. The breach of the previous meeting was caged again. Rule restored. The chamber felt colder for it — not worse. Safer.

Guilliman turned at last.

— I want to understand your chain of command.

Alaric answered without hesitation.

— Yes.

Guilliman’s eyes moved past the man, measuring the Chapter implied in every habit.

— If I remove you from the equation, what happens.

Alaric did not flinch.

— The work continues.

Guilliman nodded once, then lifted a hand to the datum wall and traced the ten lines that formed the Chapter’s operational spine.

— Ten Vexillationes.

— Yes.

— Legati carrying the war.

— Yes.

Guilliman let his hand fall.

— And yet the Chapter survived the Silent Vigil. It continued to function while its highest authority was present and hollowed-out.

He watched Alaric carefully.

— Not many institutions survive that without turning it into a myth.

Alaric’s reply carried no pride.

— We prepared for it.

Guilliman’s voice tightened.

— Show me how.

Alaric spoke as if reciting a schematic.

— Each Vexillatio is structured to prosecute a campaign alone, resupply alone, and maintain command continuity under attrition. Command roles are layered. Duties are taught as functions, not as personalities.

Guilliman’s gaze sharpened.

— Functions.

— Yes.

— So the Chapter does not depend on a man.

— Yes.

Guilliman stepped closer, inspection distance — but this inspection was not of armor seams.

— That lesson did not originate with you.

Alaric’s stillness held.

— No.

Guilliman did not ask for the name. It was already in the sealed briefings, already present in the prior meetings like a shadow behind the numbers.

He said it anyway.

— Radulf Castrin taught you this.

The air tightened. Not theatrically. Like a pressure door sealing.

— The Old Wolf, Alaric said.

— Leviathan chassis, Guilliman added, clinical.

— Yes.

Guilliman watched him, then pinned the doctrine to the room with a single sentence.

— “The work continues without us.”

Alaric answered without ornament.

— Yes.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— Explain it without making it a slogan.

A pause. A gate. Then Alaric complied.

— If the Chapter requires one mind to remain coherent, it will fail. If it requires one voice to remain lawful, it will fail. If it requires one presence to hold the line, it will fail.

Guilliman nodded once.

— Redundancy as mercy.

Alaric’s reply was immediate.

— Redundancy as survival.

Guilliman turned back to the wall, to the sections of numbers that were not battle lines — industrial output, wake protocols, arming-vault schedules, the boring work that keeps wars possible.

— Your numbers, Guilliman said.

Alaric did not deny the premise.

— We operate with what the theater demands.

Guilliman’s voice hardened.

— That is a justification. Not an audit.

Alaric’s reply came like a bar set into place.

— The audit is survival.

Guilliman turned fully to face him again.

— “Almost heretical numbers,” Alaric.

He did not raise his voice.

— Tell me how you keep those numbers from becoming corruption.

Alaric held the question like weight.

— By assigning them purpose.

Guilliman’s gaze sharpened.

— Purpose can become religion.

Alaric answered flatly.

— Purpose is a constraint.

— Who decides, Guilliman pressed.

— The Triumvirate, Alaric said.

Guilliman’s expression tightened.

— Speak precisely. I am not asking about titles. I am asking about authority.

Alaric’s answer was immediate, and for once it sounded less like doctrine and more like a line drawn to prevent misunderstanding.

— Drusus, Varro, and I carry the same authority.

Guilliman held his gaze.

— Equal.

— Equal.

Guilliman stepped closer.

— That is unusual.

Alaric did not decorate it.

— It is necessary.

Guilliman’s voice narrowed into a knife.

— If you are removed from the equation.

Alaric did not flinch.

— The Triumvirate remains.

Guilliman’s eyes sharpened.

— Without you.

— Yes.

— And if Drusus is removed.

— The Triumvirate remains.

— And if Varro is removed.

— The Triumvirate remains.

Guilliman stared at him.

— Your answer is too clean.

Alaric’s reply did not change.

— It must be clean.

Guilliman turned away again, back to the wall, as if giving the man the dignity of not being watched while he was measured.

— I have seen this tension before, Guilliman said quietly. — A man holding an empire’s weight in his hands, trying to make himself into the thing that will not break, because the alternative is unacceptable.

A brief pause. Guilliman’s expression tightened with something that was not politics.

— That is what Ferrus lived.

He did not linger on the name.

— He would like parts of what you’ve built.

Another pause.

— He would also recognize the risk: turning a warning into chains.

Alaric did not answer at once.

When he did, his voice was spare, but the truth behind it had teeth.

— We do not punish the human.

Guilliman’s gaze sharpened.

— Then what do you do.

Alaric answered simply.

— We prevent the human from becoming an excuse.

Guilliman held still.

— You said the Legati carried you through the Silent Vigil, Guilliman said, turning back. — Not you.

Alaric did not deny it.

— Yes.

— So why elevate the Triumvirate to equal authority instead of placing everything on the Legati.

Alaric’s answer came with the cold clarity of a man who had watched failure modes up close.

— Because war can continue on courage alone.

A pause.

— Survival cannot.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— Explain.

Alaric’s voice became pure instrument again.

— The Legati carry war. The Triumvirate carries the conditions that keep war lawful.

Guilliman stared at him.

— Survival is war.

— Survival is the condition that allows war to remain lawful.

The words landed with structural finality.

Guilliman studied him, then let the question step out of structure into the human without turning indulgent.

— What do you fear, Alaric von Helis.

Silence.

Then Alaric spoke, and the answer was not cosmic.

— Leaving things undone.

Guilliman’s expression tightened with recognition.

— And so you built a Chapter that can continue without any one of you.

— Yes.

Guilliman stepped back, and the room released a fraction of pressure with him.

— Then your structure will be tolerated, he said. — And it will be audited.

A pause.

— Not because I want to break you.

He glanced once at the numbers again.

— Because I want to ensure your redundancy does not become a new devotion.

Alaric’s reply was immediate.

— Understood.

Guilliman turned toward the door.

— One last thing.

Alaric waited.

Guilliman’s voice was hard, and beneath it was a human edge he did not decorate.

— You are correct about unfinished work.

He paused at the threshold.

— Do not let that truth become a chain you wrap around the living.

Alaric’s answer came like a vow without poetry.

— I will not.

Guilliman opened the door, and the fortress’ noise returned — watch rotations, machinery, the Imperium’s endless grind.

He stepped out carrying no comfort.

Only a map of a Chapter built like clockwork — meant to keep time even after any hand that wound it was gone.
