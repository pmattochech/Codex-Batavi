Guilliman did not mistake gold for comfort.

Gold was a statement of jurisdiction. It said: *this corridor answers to a different calendar than yours.*

The annex the Batavi kept for Throne business was not a chapel and not a throne room. It was a **hall**—high ceiling, auramite ribs in the architecture like ribs under skin, lumen refracted until the air itself looked expensive. The floor had been polished until it punished carelessness: a man who rushed here would look foolish even if he survived.

Guilliman had rushed nowhere.

He entered alone.

Not because he feared escort—he had learned, in two lives, that escort could become a story told about you before you spoke—but because this conversation required **one ledger**, not three retinues pretending they were one mind.

At the far end, motionless, spear vertical: **Shield-Captain Tyvar**.

The Custodian did not stand like an Astartes officer waiting to be impressive. He stood like a datum that had decided to occupy space until truth finished its work. Auramite caught light without begging for it. The helm did not lift in greeting.

Guilliman stopped at the distance etiquette demanded—not fear, **geometry**. Even a primarch learned where another institution’s personal space began when that institution had been built to deny assassins for ten thousand years.

— Lord Guilliman — Tyvar said.

The words had no warmth.

They also had no insult.

Inventory tone.

— Shield-Captain Tyvar — Guilliman answered.

He did not offer his hand.

Tyvar would not have taken it.

Denial was not rudeness here. Denial was **protocol** made visible: no seat offered, no refreshment implied, no domestic frame. Only vigil.

Guilliman let silence do its work.

Silence, in this hall, behaved differently than silence in a trenchworks node. Here it did not invite confession. It invited **precision**.

— I have walked your watch’s perimeter in flesh — Guilliman said. — Not as theater. As habit. Chem silos. Tide debt. Forge floors. I have seen how this Chapter spends time when it believes nobody is grading sentiment.

Tyvar did not shift his spear.

— You grade it anyway — Tyvar said.

Guilliman inclined his head once—acknowledgment, not concession.

— I do — Guilliman said. — That is the function of a regent who refuses to become a myth. I am not here to ask you to love them.

Tyvar’s helm tilted by a fraction.

— Good — Tyvar said. — Love is a contaminant in audit ink.

Guilliman felt the corner of his mouth threaten something that was not quite humor.

He killed it.

— I am here for what you can say without breaking your orders — Guilliman continued. — And for what you can say **only** because your orders require truth to remain usable.

A pause.

Tyvar filled it without filling it—economy as discipline.

— Ask — Tyvar said.

Guilliman did not pretend the question was small.

— In your filing — Guilliman said. — Is the Cohors Batavorum still an instrument that knows it can fail… or is it becoming an idol that believes failure is impossible because it has survived too long.

Tyvar did not answer quickly.

For a Custodes, that was already an answer.

When Tyvar spoke, his voice stayed flat—still not warmth, but not cruelty either. The tone of a man who had decided long ago that cruelty was a waste of torque.

— The instrument documents its flaws — Tyvar said. — Voluntarily, in places most Chapters would bury. It monitors actively. It requests supervision instead of avoiding it. That is not a guarantee of reliability. It is evidence the mind still works.

Guilliman listened to *supervision* and heard the iron collar named without self-pity.

— And the risk — Guilliman said.

Tyvar’s spear did not move.

— The risk is what it always becomes when transhuman discipline succeeds — Tyvar said. — Success breeds confidence. Confidence breeds economy of doubt. Economy of doubt breeds catastrophe. My watch exists to reintroduce **doubt** at a constant rate until the Throne revokes the mandate.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— You are describing me as part of the hazard — Guilliman said.

Tyvar did not flinch.

— I am describing **any** sovereign gaze that stays too long — Tyvar said. — Lord Guilliman, your presence improves alignment. It also increases the temptation to perform alignment. I do not accuse you of vanity. I name the variable.

Guilliman accepted the naming.

He had spent too many years turning variables into policy to mistake honesty for insubordination.

— Then name the output — Guilliman said. — Not for the Chapter’s ears. For mine. After what you have watched—decades, not days—do you recommend loosening the collar… or tightening it.

Tyvar’s silence returned—brief, surgical.

— Loosening is not on the slate — Tyvar said. — Not because I desire cruelty. Because loosening would be interpreted as permission, and permission is how frontiers rot. Tightening is also not automatically correct. Tightening can fracture an instrument that is already holding under torsion.

Guilliman waited.

Tyvar continued.

— I recommend **continuity** — Tyvar said. — Same mandate. Same rotations. Same refusal to merge categories. If the Captain-General wishes a different shape, he will write it. Until then, my duty is not to invent new drama for your inspection. It is to keep the watch **boring**.

Guilliman exhaled once, slow, controlled.

Boredom, in the Imperium, was a rare luxury.

— And Alaric — Guilliman said.

Tyvar’s helm did not turn away.

— The Castellan is not a saint — Tyvar said. — He is a wall that chose to remain a wall while biology insisted otherwise. That choice is visible. It is also exhaustible. Exhaustion is where idols are born—when men mistake fatigue for fate.

Guilliman filed the sentence without flinching.

— Your verdict — Guilliman said. — On the Chapter’s survival as a loyal instrument. Not victory. Survival.

Tyvar answered as if reading a sealed line he had memorized because memory was safer than tablets.

— Survival is plausible — Tyvar said. — Not because they are pure. Because they are **ashamed in the right direction**. They fear relief more than death. That fear can become tyranny. It can also become law. My watch is here to ensure which direction the vector points when the wall trembles.

Guilliman nodded once.

Not approval of Tyvar.

Approval of **procedure**.

He had come for a verdict that could survive contact with Terra.

He had received one that could survive contact with **gold**.

— Then I will say what I can say without pretending I have become your captain-general — Guilliman said. — The Batavi remain. Not as a romance. Not as a cautionary tale told for applause. As a frontier instrument under **dual** audit: mine where the Imperium’s logistics demand it—yours where the Throne’s survival demands it.

Tyvar’s spear dipped by the smallest angle—acknowledgment in a language that did not waste motion.

— Understood — Tyvar said.

Guilliman turned to leave.

At the threshold, he paused without turning fully.

— Shield-Captain — Guilliman said.

Tyvar waited.

— If the wall trembles and you must choose — Guilliman said. — Choose without theater. The Imperium already has enough stories. It has too few functioning doors.

Tyvar’s voice came behind him, quiet as a blade returning to its sheath.

— Lord Guilliman — Tyvar said. — That has always been the watch’s job.

Guilliman walked out of the golden hall and back into grey corridors where the air did not apologize for being utilitarian.

The inspection ended there.

Not because every question had been answered.

Because every question that **could** be answered without lying had finally reached the last witness who mattered.
