Guilliman did not sleep.

Sleep was a mercy the new Imperium could not afford him, and mercy—he had learned—was a currency that became a weapon the moment it was spent without constraint.

He stood alone in a chamber that belonged to nobody: not a strategium, not an apothecarion, not a chapel. A corridor-node with a single lumen strip and a blank datum panel. The kind of place the fortress used to move bodies and orders through without leaving fingerprints of sentiment.

He had chosen it because it gave him nothing to lean on.

No maps to hide behind. No numbers to excuse himself with. No witnesses to turn a thought into policy before it was ready.

Only the weight of what he had seen.

Alaric von Helis was a variable Guilliman could not ignore.

Not because the Batavian Lord was loud. He wasn’t. Not because the Chapter was large. It wasn’t, not in the way some institutions tried to be. Not because their blood ran “wrong,” as frightened men would say. Guilliman had lived through an age where “wrong” was often simply a name for what frightened the speaker.

It was because Alaric’s existence forced an audit of the Imperium itself.

Guilliman let his thoughts drift backward, not into nostalgia, but into simulation. The Great Crusade was a board he knew intimately—every piece, every flaw, every beautiful lie.

What would it have been, if Alaric had stood there then?

Astartes were not interchangeable. The Emperor had never pretended they were. Each Legion was a solution to a kind of war, and each solution carried its own failure mode like a fuse in its spine.

Alaric was a solution too.

Not a bright one. Not inspiring. Not glorious.

A wall.

If he had walked the Crusade’s corridors, Guilliman could picture how his brothers would have treated him.

Dorn would have recognized the geometry at once. Not “friendship”—Dorn did not spend himself that way—but an austere respect. A commander who did not need applause to hold. A fortress-mind that understood that victory was often nothing but a refusal to yield an angle. Dorn would have asked only one question, the only one that ever mattered to him.

Can it fail?

And when he learned it could—when he learned the Batavian “curse” was not merely a battlefield hazard but a culture-shaping stressor—Dorn would have done what Dorn always did.

He would have demanded compartments. Redundancies. Hard rules. Protocols carved so deep they could survive panic.

Alaric would have agreed, because Alaric had already done the same to himself.

The Angel would have been different.

Sanguinius would have looked at the Batavian and seen the most dangerous kind of kinship: a monster that chose discipline without turning discipline into a crown. Sanguinius would have understood that the Batavi did not fear death most.

They feared relief.

They feared the moment a man stopped thinking and called it peace.

Sanguinius would have spoken to Alaric not as a commander to a subordinate, but as a man to a man carrying a private war behind public duty. He would have offered warmth—not as indulgence, but as a stabilizer.

Guilliman could also picture the risk: Sanguinius’s warmth could become a new kind of permission if Alaric ever let himself believe he had earned rest.

Ferrus Manus—

Guilliman’s jaw tightened at the name, as it always did, a small bodily betrayal that reminded him he was not made of law alone.

Ferrus would have liked the Batavian methods. That was the simplest truth.

Ferrus would have liked the refusal of theater. The contempt for weakness-as-excuse. The insistence that function mattered more than comfort.

Ferrus would also have recognized the poison hidden inside that same metal: the temptation to punish the living for being breakable. The temptation to turn “the flesh is weak” into a chain you wrap around people until they stop being people.

Alaric, Guilliman suspected, would have seen that hidden message too. Not copied Ferrus. Not decorated himself with the Xth’s scars.

Learned.

Learned without worship.

And then there were the brothers who would have found Alaric useful in the worst possible ways.

Perturabo would have understood him quickly. Too quickly. A wall that did not ask to be loved. A mind that treated morale as a variable, not a sacred thing.

They would have built horrors together with perfect efficiency—until the day pride demanded payment and found none. The day Perturabo realized the Batavian did not need recognition, and therefore could not be controlled by withholding it. That would have become resentment. Resentment would have become geometry. Geometry would have become fratricide.

Lorgar would have tried to name Alaric’s restraint as faith. Guilliman could see it: the urge to sanctify the wall, to turn “holding” into a scripture that absolved the weak from thinking. Alaric would have resisted, not with argument, but with refusal—no speeches, no slogans, no permission for relief.

Curze would have called the Batavian a hypocrite and meant it as truth. A monster pretending to be lawful. A judge hiding behind procedure. And then Curze would have discovered the difference that mattered: Alaric did not use law to enjoy punishment. He used law to prevent himself from enjoying anything at all.

Even Horus—

Guilliman stopped himself before the memory became indulgence.

Horus would have smiled, would have found the lever, would have tried to make Alaric feel seen.

And if Alaric had been younger then, if he had not yet learned that being seen is not always mercy, Guilliman could imagine the catastrophe: a wall stepping aside because a warm voice asked it to.

But the Alaric Guilliman had met now was not young.

He was built from decades of betrayal, attrition, and an internal war against his own biology. A commander who had survived long enough to learn that the most seductive lies are the ones that feel like relief.

And in that older board—this one, the Imperium Nihilus—there were other pieces too.

The Emperor.

Malcador.

Guilliman’s mind returned to Terra, to the cold clarity of the Sigillite’s gaze. Malcador would have treated Alaric as a sealed file from the moment the first anomaly appeared. He would have understood the value: a Chapter that could metabolize other cultures without dissolving into them, a wall that could hold where faith was unstable.

He would also have understood the risk: an institution that could absorb could also be absorbed, and an institution trained to treat relief as poison might one day treat joy the same way.

The Emperor—

Guilliman did not allow himself to imagine approval. Approval was a childish hunger. The Emperor’s love, if it existed, was not shaped like reassurance.

He imagined instead the Emperor’s use.

A wall placed where it must hold, and never asked whether it wanted to.

A tool that would not complain.

Alaric would have been good at that. That was the problem. The Imperium rewarded men who could be used.

Guilliman had awakened into an Imperium full of tools and too few men.

He breathed in the filtered air and felt, with bitter clarity, that his interrogations had been less about gene-line or numbers or doctrines.

They had been about one thing.

Can a monster remain human without turning humanity into permission?

He had asked Alaric. He had asked Drusus and Varro. He had asked Ventris, who was dangerous precisely because he could like a wall and therefore be tempted to lean on it.

He had learned the shape of the answer.

Alaric was not an idol by intent.

Alaric was an idol by gravity.

People did what mortals always did when they were tired and afraid: they tried to build a god out of whatever held the dark back. They tried to outsource conscience to a wall.

The Batavi understood that temptation better than most, which was why they fought it so violently. Why they hated slogans. Why they audited affection. Why they built redundancy until it bordered on heresy.

Why they kept a kitchen anyway.

Why they kept a human translation layer anyway.

Why they kept the memory of a wolf’s warmth inside a fortress of iron.

Guilliman’s mind returned to one image he could not quite file into policy: Alaric’s face, bare for a breath, the rule broken like a bone set deliberately out of place.

Not devotion. Not theater.

A gamble.

A man unable to reach a reasonable answer and choosing, briefly, to risk being seen.

It had not felt like weakness. It had felt like the only kind of strength an immortal machine could afford: the strength to refuse becoming only useful.

Guilliman spoke aloud, because sometimes a thought had to be made into sound to be treated as real.

— What do I do with you.

The words vanished into the corridor’s sterile air. No echo. No answer.

He turned the problem the way he turned every problem: into constraints.

If he tried to break the Batavi—force them into a more conventional shape, sand down the edges until they fit neatly into a Codex diagram—he would destroy the very mechanism that kept them lawful under their particular pressure.

If he indulged them—allowed their uniqueness to become romance, allowed their suffering to become myth—he would create the idol himself and then pretend he had merely observed it.

Both paths were failure.

So he chose the only thing that had ever worked in the Imperium, when it worked at all.

Audit.

Not cruelty masquerading as purity. Not mercy masquerading as permission.

Audit as restraint.

He would not isolate Alaric. Isolation was how walls became gods in empty rooms.

He would not elevate Alaric into spectacle. Spectacle was how devotion found a mouth.

He would do something harder: ensure the Batavi doctrine belonged to the Batavi—not to one man. Ensure their Legati carried the same audit language, the same refusal to merge categories, the same refusal to abandon, until it lived in the Chapter’s bone rather than in Alaric’s silhouette.

He would keep the Triumvirate as a check not only on the Chapter, but on Alaric himself. Three masks, equal authority, each dangerous in a different way, each preventing the other two from becoming the whole truth.

He would permit the exchanges—and he would make them formal, measured, and repeatable. Controlled exposure as doctrine, not as novelty.

He would keep the kitchen out of policy hands. Not because he was sentimental. Because he had learned what happened when a ruler touched the one human place an institution used to stay sane.

You turn refusal into chains.

He would keep Elara close enough to matter and distant enough not to be worshipped. Translation layers were necessary. Translation layers were also potential idols. He would insist that she remain a constraint, not a banner.

And he would write one sealed line for himself alone, the kind of line that did not belong in proclamations.

Do not let the wall stand alone.

Guilliman exhaled. It was not relief. It was acceptance of burden.

He turned toward the door, toward the fortress’ noise, toward the Imperium that would keep bleeding whether he chose or not.

Before he stepped out, he allowed himself one final simulation—one more glance at that older board, the Great Crusade, the brothers who had fallen, the brothers who had held, the father who had built them all.

If Alaric had been there then, perhaps some fractures would have been delayed. Perhaps some would have come sooner. Perhaps nothing would have changed at all, because the Imperium’s failure modes were older than any one wall.

But the thought that lingered was simpler, and it was not strategic.

If Alaric had been there then, Guilliman suspected, the Emperor would have used him without asking.

Guilliman would not repeat that mistake.

He stepped into the corridor and let the locks seal behind him, carrying his final output like a weapon that had to be kept pointed inward as much as outward.

Alaric von Helis would remain.

Not as a saint.

Not as a suspect.

As a wall under audit—held up by more than his own hands.
