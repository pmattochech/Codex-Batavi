The second time Guilliman asked for privacy, he did not choose an antechamber.

He chose a place with a chair.

Not a throne. Not a command dais. A simple, bolted thing that looked as if a dockworker had once used it between shifts. Its presence in a fortress of Astartes-scale corridors was almost obscene. A small accommodation to the human body. A reminder that not every weight in the Imperium could be carried standing.

Alaric did not sit.

Guilliman did not ask him to.

The door sealed. The lumen strip was dimmer than last time, angled so it would not brighten a face by accident.

Guilliman waited until the lock cycle completed.

— You told me the man cannot diverge.

Alaric’s voice was the same sparse instrument it had been before.

— Yes.

Guilliman took one step, then another, not toward Alaric, but toward the chair. He did not sit. He touched the backrest with two fingers, as if verifying it was real.

— I have asked a thousand commanders about doctrine, Guilliman said. They answer because doctrine is safe. It is armor.

He looked up.

— I am going to ask you about things you keep sealed because they are not safe.

Alaric did not answer immediately. The pause was not hesitation. It was a gate.

— Proceed.

Guilliman did not soften the first name.

— Your predecessor.

The air seemed to tighten. Not theatrically. Like a pressure change.

— The one now entombed.

Alaric’s helm remained forward. Guilliman could not see eyes through the lenses, but he could see what mattered: the body did not move away from the blow. It simply absorbed it.

— He is not “entombed,” Alaric said. — He is sustained.

Guilliman’s expression did not change, but his tone did—fractionally, into something that acknowledged the insult without punishing it.

— Leviathan chassis.

Alaric’s reply came with the first hint of something like fracture. Not in the words. In the way he refused ornament so fiercely the refusal itself became a shape.

— Yes.

Guilliman stepped closer. Inspection distance again, but this time the inspection was not of armor seams.

— Tell me what you learned from him.

Alaric’s answer was too quick.

— Duty.

Guilliman stopped.

— That is a slogan.

The word struck harder than any accusation of treason. Because it was accurate.

Silence held for a count that Guilliman did not measure with impatience. He waited like a man who had learned that forcing truth out of another human being only produces a better liar.

At last, Alaric spoke again.

— He taught me what a Chapter is when it is starving.

Guilliman’s brow tightened.

— Explain.

Alaric’s voice stayed controlled, but the content shifted—less like a report, more like a memory filed against pain.

— A Chapter is not its heraldry. Not its rituals. Not its Founding story. It is what remains functional when you have lost the right to be proud.

Guilliman held still.

— He taught me that “holding” is not a stance. It is maintenance. It is choosing the same correct action until you hate yourself for how small it feels.

A pause.

— And then choosing it again.

Guilliman nodded once, slowly.

— And he is still your Chapter Master.

Alaric’s helm dipped a fraction—almost a nod, almost a refusal to allow the gesture to become sentiment.

— He is a boundary.

Guilliman let the words settle, then moved to the second blade.

— Valerius.

Alaric did not flinch.

That was worse.

— I have read the sealed record of what he did, Guilliman continued. The way he spoke. The way he turned command into a weapon. The way he offered relief as if it were virtue.

He watched the still figure in front of him.

— You called it “permission” last time.

Alaric’s answer was careful.

— Permission is the door.

— And Valerius was the hand on the latch, Guilliman said.

Alaric said nothing.

Guilliman pressed anyway.

— Tell me what you learned from him.

For the first time, Alaric’s voice carried the faintest edge—not anger, but something like contempt for the question’s premise.

— He is not a mentor.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— I did not say he was.

He let the space between them become very quiet.

— I said you learned something.

Alaric’s next words came slower.

— I learned that a primarch can be betrayed by the shape of his own virtues.

Guilliman did not move. He did not react as a ruler. He reacted as a man hearing a knife slide between ribs.

— Repeat that, he said softly.

Alaric obeyed without performance.

— A virtue becomes a weapon when it stops being regulated.

Guilliman held his gaze on the helm lenses.

— And what virtue was it.

Alaric’s answer landed like a verdict and a confession simultaneously.

— Certainty.

Guilliman drew a breath that sounded almost human in its restraint.

— You are describing half the Imperium.

— Yes, Alaric said. — That is why it was effective.

Guilliman’s hand tightened, briefly, on the chair’s backrest. The metal creaked.

— You told me you do not seek honor. You told me procedure can be audited. You told me belief is unstable.

He released the chair.

— You have also been briefed on me. You know what my return means. You know the doctrines I am trying to rebuild. You know what my sons will take from my words.

He stepped closer again.

— If you are so hostile to belief, why do you keep studying primarch doctrine as if it were scripture?

Alaric answered with the first hint of something like genuine heat—still controlled, but real.

— Because doctrine is not scripture if you read it as engineering.

Guilliman’s eyes sharpened.

— Explain.

Alaric’s voice steadied into its most dangerous form: absolute simplicity.

— I study what works.

— You absorb.

— We learn.

— You incorporate other Chapters’ cultures, Guilliman said, not as accusation but as an observed fact. — You take valor and make it part of you.

Alaric did not deny it.

— We do not decorate ourselves with other men’s banners. We take the hidden message between their lines.

Guilliman watched him carefully.

— And you do this with primarchs.

A pause.

— You do this with me.

Alaric’s reply was quiet.

— Yes.

Guilliman felt the weight of it. Admiration and unease braided together.

— Do you see every loyal primarch as a mentor?

Alaric did not answer with reverence. He answered with method.

— Each left a lesson. None left a complete solution.

Guilliman’s voice softened without losing force.

— And what lesson did Sanguinius leave you.

Alaric was still for too long.

When he spoke, Guilliman heard something he had not heard in the prior meeting: a constraint under strain.

— That the monstrous can be carried without being surrendered to.

Guilliman did not interrupt.

Alaric continued, and the words had the texture of a wound kept clean.

— That you can look at your own curse and not romanticize it.

A pause.

— That you can show a face that frightens people… and still be worthy of their trust.

Guilliman’s gaze locked to the helm.

— You have shown your face before.

It was not a question.

Alaric’s voice dropped.

— Once.

— To the sons of Sanguinius, Guilliman said, confirming with sealed certainty.

Silence.

— Yes.

Guilliman let the silence breathe. Then he spoke, careful and exact.

— I am going to ask you something no strategist should ask a weapon.

He stepped closer until the distance was undeniably personal.

— I want to see the man.

The words did not command. They invited. That was, Guilliman knew, a different kind of pressure—harder to refuse, because it offered dignity.

Alaric did not respond.

The lumen strip hummed.

Seconds passed with the slow gravity of a siege clock.

When Alaric finally spoke, the sound was almost too quiet to be heard as anything but confession.

— I cannot reach a reasonable answer.

Guilliman held still.

— Then why would you do it.

Alaric’s helm angled downward a fraction, the first posture that looked like anything other than readiness.

— Because I have learned that sometimes reason must accept a gamble.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— That is not your doctrine.

— No, Alaric said. — It is theirs.

Guilliman understood. In an instant he understood the peril hidden inside the Batavian method: not imitation, but incorporation. Not copying, but metabolizing. Alaric could take lessons from others and make them part of his operating system.

Even the ones that did not fit him.

Especially the ones that did not fit him.

— Which primarch taught you to gamble, Guilliman asked.

Alaric’s voice did not change.

— Sanguinius.

Guilliman did not look away from the helm.

— Do you believe this will help you.

Alaric’s answer was honest in the worst way.

— I do not know.

Guilliman felt something cold move through him—not suspicion, not disgust. Recognition of a kind of courage he rarely encountered: the courage to admit uncertainty without collapsing into it.

— Then do not do it, Guilliman said.

Alaric did not move.

Guilliman realized, with a sudden tightness in the throat, that the permission had already been granted—not by Guilliman, but by Alaric himself. The door was open inside him, and he was deciding whether to close it again before something entered.

— You said permission is the door, Guilliman said quietly.

Alaric’s voice was nearly a whisper.

— Yes.

Guilliman held the silence for one more heartbeat.

— Then close it.

Alaric’s hands rose.

Not fast. Not dramatic. Measured, as if every millimeter carried a cost.

The seals hissed.

The helm’s locks released with a sound like a tool being unclipped from a belt.

Alaric paused with the helm half-lifted, suspended between identity and exposure. Guilliman could see the tension in the neck, in the shoulders—an unnatural restraint, a body fighting an instinct to remain armored.

The gamble sat there, alive.

Then Alaric lifted the helm free.

For a moment, the chamber seemed too small to contain what it revealed.

His face was not the face of a mortal who had lived a hard life and grown scarred. It was the face of something re-written—human geometry held under discipline, but altered in ways the Imperium’s instinctive mind would call wrong before it found kinder language. A structure that suggested a man and a predator forced to share the same skin and negotiate every expression.

And yet his eyes were steady.

Not pleading. Not performative. Simply present.

Guilliman did not recoil.

He did not soften his gaze into pity.

He looked at Alaric the way he looked at a damaged vault that still held pressure: with respect for what it cost to remain intact.

Alaric spoke, and without the vox-filter the voice was lower, heavier, more human in its texture—still restrained, but now undeniably flesh.

— This is why I made the rule.

Guilliman nodded once.

— I understand.

Alaric’s jaw tightened. The vulnerability was not tears. It was the decision to remain unmasked while still refusing to beg.

— Do you.

Guilliman’s reply came after a fraction of thought—the kind of thought that lives in a man rather than a primarch.

— I understand what you fear.

He took a breath.

— And I understand what you refuse to let that fear make you into.

Alaric’s gaze held. The altered features did not distort into rage. They did not bloom into performance.

They remained controlled, almost painfully so.

— You asked about the man, Alaric said. — The man is the one who keeps the rule.

Guilliman answered softly.

— And the man is the one who breaks it.

Alaric’s mouth moved in something that was not quite a smile and not quite the memory of one. A micro-expression that looked like a habit the body did not fully remember how to enact.

— Yes, Alaric said. — That is the gamble.

Guilliman’s eyes stayed on his.

— Tell me about Valerius as a man, Guilliman said. — Not as a traitor.

Alaric’s throat worked once.

When he spoke, it was not an indictment. It was worse: it was accuracy without hatred.

— He was exhausted.

Guilliman’s expression tightened.

— That is not a justification.

— No, Alaric said. — It is the vector.

He inhaled. Slowly. Regulating himself in real time.

— He wanted relief more than he wanted to be lawful.

The words sat in the room like a contaminant that had to be contained.

Guilliman nodded once, grim.

— And your predecessor.

Alaric did not look away.

— He wanted law more than he wanted to be alive.

Guilliman’s hand tightened at his side. Not anger. A kind of grief without indulgence.

— And you.

Alaric’s answer came with the barest, most lethal honesty.

— I want a way to carry both without merging them.

Guilliman held his gaze. He did not give benediction. He did not offer an easy conclusion.

He simply spoke the only kind of support that matched the Batavian method: a constraint, clean as a line on a map.

— Then keep learning.

A pause.

— Keep incorporating what is worth incorporating.

Another pause, and Guilliman’s voice lowered further.

— And do not incorporate my mercy as permission.

Alaric’s eyes did not flicker.

— Understood.

Guilliman watched him a moment longer, then nodded—once, final.

— Put it back on.

Alaric did not move at first.

He held the gamble in his bare face for one more breath, as if proving to himself that exposure did not automatically become collapse.

Then he replaced the helm.

The locks sealed.

The hiss returned, and the room became safer again—not because the man was hidden, but because the rule had resumed its function.

Guilliman turned toward the door.

— One last thing, he said.

Alaric waited.

Guilliman spoke without looking back, voice the sound of a wall deciding to remain a wall.

— If your Chapter is what you say it is—if it absorbs valor and makes it law—then understand this.

He paused at the threshold.

— You will be watched. Not because you are suspect.

He looked back then, eyes hard and clear.

— Because you are contagious.

Alaric’s reply came without heat.

— Good.

Guilliman opened the door.

And when he stepped out into the Imperium’s noise, he did not carry comfort with him.

He carried a colder thing.

A proof that a monster could still choose regulation over relief—at least for another day.
