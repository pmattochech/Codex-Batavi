The room Guilliman chose for Ventris had no viewports.

Not because Guilliman feared being watched — he assumed he was always watched — but because he did not want the stars to turn the conversation into a story.

It was a records cabinet converted into a private office: a narrow chamber lined with sealed dataslates and paper-analogue folios, the air dry and filtered, the only light a single lumen strip that made everything look clinical. The door closed with a sound like a verdict.

Uriel Ventris stood at attention, helm under one arm, posture exact. Not theatrical. Not relaxed. The posture of a man who had learned that discipline is not a costume you wear for superiors; it is how you stay lawful when the world wants you to become convenient.

Guilliman did not sit.

He did not ask Ventris to sit.

For a moment he said nothing at all. He looked at Ventris as if trying to locate the seam between the Captain and the man inside the armor. He had done this with Alaric, and with Drusus and Varro. The difference was that Ventris did not feel like a fortress. He felt like a line drawn on a map with a steady hand.

Guilliman spoke at last.

— You served in the Watch.

Ventris answered without pride.

— Yes, my lord.

— You served with Alaric von Helis.

Ventris did not hesitate.

— Yes.

Guilliman’s eyes stayed steady.

— Tell me what he is.

Ventris did not reach for a compliment. He didn’t begin with “loyal” or “strong” or any of the words men used when they didn’t want to say something harder.

— He is restraint given a body.

Guilliman’s gaze narrowed.

— That is what Varro called him.

Ventris’s voice stayed level.

— Then Varro sees him accurately.

Guilliman held the silence for a breath.

— I did not bring you here for agreement.

Ventris didn’t move.

Guilliman stepped closer by half a pace. Not threatening, but reducing distance the way one reduces uncertainty.

— I want one thing from you, Captain.

Ventris waited.

Guilliman’s voice dropped. The question came out quiet, and because it was quiet it carried more weight than any shouted demand.

— Is he a wall… or an idol in the making.

Ventris did not answer immediately.

He looked down for a fraction of a second, not in fear, but in the way a man looks inward when he needs to choose between competing truths. Guilliman waited without pressing. He had learned that if you force an answer you get a weaponized one.

Ventris raised his eyes.

— He can become either, my lord.

Guilliman’s expression did not change.

— You are not allowed that ambiguity.

Ventris didn’t flinch.

— Then I will be precise.

A pause.

— He is a wall by intention.

Another pause.

— An idol by risk.

Guilliman watched him.

— Explain.

Ventris answered as if writing an after-action report that would be used to decide who lived.

— The Watch strips away chapter-shape.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

Ventris continued.

— In the Watch, he could not rely on his title, or his Chapter’s fear, or his reputation. He had to earn obedience in the moment, under pressure, from men who did not want to be impressed.

Guilliman nodded once — one of the rare gestures that meant “continue.”

Ventris’s voice stayed level.

— Alaric asked for the Watch because he wanted to remove his own blind spot. He said — explicitly — that he could not demand the burden of his Legati if he had not carried it himself.

Guilliman held still.

— That is not a common primaris instinct.

— No, my lord.

Guilliman’s voice remained quiet.

— That’s the wall.

Ventris answered.

— Yes.

Guilliman did not let him escape the second half.

— And the idol.

Ventris inhaled once. He didn’t look away.

— The idol happens when people begin to treat his restraint as absolution.

Guilliman’s gaze sharpened.

— Meaning.

Ventris replied, precise.

— When they say: “He holds, therefore I do not have to.” When they outsource conscience to the wall.

Guilliman didn’t react outwardly. The words landed anyway; they always did.

— Give me an event, Guilliman said.

Ventris nodded once, as if he had been waiting for the question.

— Kill-team action.

Guilliman did not prompt further. Ventris began.

— We were four. Myself. A Salamander. A Crimson Fist. And him — then only a Batavian officer, not the figure your sealed briefings describe now.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed slightly.

— Where.

Ventris answered without turning it into myth.

— A void-hulk segment. Confined corridors. Vox interference. Xenos inside the walls.

Guilliman waited.

Ventris continued.

— The Salamander wanted to evacuate mortal survivors pinned behind a bulkhead breach. The Crimson Fist wanted to seal the corridor and burn it clean — time pressure, enemy behind us, no room to maneuver.

Guilliman’s gaze was steady.

— And you.

— I wanted the plan to be lawful and executable.

Guilliman’s voice stayed quiet.

— And Alaric.

Ventris’s answer came carefully.

— He did not speak first.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

Ventris went on.

— He watched. Not like a man enjoying the argument. Like a man listening for the point where fear becomes “relief.” The point where someone chooses a simple answer because thinking costs too much.

Guilliman felt the familiar shape: the Batavi obsession with permission. The refusal to let exhaustion write doctrine.

— The Crimson Fist said something, Ventris continued, and his voice tightened by a degree, — something like: “We can’t carry them. We’re not made for it.” It wasn’t cruelty. It was the arithmetic of mass and time.

Guilliman’s jaw tightened.

Ventris kept going.

— The Salamander replied: “That’s exactly why we must.”

A pause.

— I could feel the team splitting. Not politically. Functionally. Two virtues turning into two weapons.

Guilliman’s eyes sharpened.

— And Alaric.

Ventris’s expression remained controlled.

— He moved.

Guilliman waited.

Ventris spoke with the careful precision of a man who still remembered the sound of the corridor.

— He didn’t deliver a speech. He didn’t quote doctrine. He didn’t shame anyone. He physically placed himself at the junction between us and the breach.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— A wall.

— Yes, my lord.

Ventris continued.

— He keyed the channel and said two sentences. Only two.

He paused, then repeated them exactly.

— We do not merge.

— We do not abandon.

Guilliman did not move.

Ventris went on.

— Then he gave each of us a job that preserved our virtues without letting them become absolutes. The Salamander would lead the mortals out. The Crimson Fist would seal and hold the corridor behind us. I would coordinate timing and fields of fire.

A pause.

— Alaric took the worst position. The one where he would absorb impact if the timing failed.

Guilliman’s gaze stayed locked.

— What happened.

Ventris answered.

— The extraction succeeded.

Guilliman waited, because success was never the whole story.

Ventris added the part that mattered.

— Afterward, the Salamander tried to thank him.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— “Tried.”

Ventris’s voice stayed neutral.

— Alaric refused gratitude. Not coldly. Like a regulation. He said: “Do not decorate it. File it.”

Guilliman’s expression tightened. Familiar phrasing. Batavi language. The refusal of theatre even when theatre would feel good.

Ventris finished, and the finishing line was not dramatic.

— He prevented three different forms of relief from entering the room. Relief by purity. Relief by sacrifice. Relief by heroism.

Guilliman held that in silence.

Then he spoke.

— And the idol.

Ventris did not hesitate.

— The Salamander began to look at him like a standard.

Guilliman’s eyes sharpened.

Ventris continued.

— The Crimson Fist began to look at him like permission: “If he is here, I can go numb and it will still be lawful.”

A pause.

— I saw it. Alaric saw it too.

Guilliman’s voice was very quiet.

— What did he do.

Ventris answered.

— He made it uncomfortable.

Guilliman’s gaze tightened.

Ventris elaborated, precise.

— He redistributed burden on the next operation. He put the Salamander in the worst corridor. He put the Crimson Fist in the moral-choice position. He kept doing it until the team stopped orbiting him.

Guilliman’s expression changed by a fraction. Not warmth. Recognition.

— He prevented worship.

Ventris answered.

— He prevented dependence.

Guilliman looked at him for a long moment.

— Calgar would trust your judgment, Ventris.

Ventris’s voice stayed flat.

— Yes, my lord.

Guilliman’s eyes remained steady.

— I will too.

Ventris did not react as if he had been given a gift. He reacted as if he had been given a constraint.

— Understood.

Guilliman turned slightly toward the sealed records cabinet wall, as if speaking not only to Ventris but to the weight of what he’d awakened into.

— Then the question becomes simpler.

Ventris waited.

Guilliman spoke.

— How do we keep the wall… and kill the idol.

Ventris answered without pride, without flattery, without any false certainty.

— By never letting him be the only one who understands his own doctrine.

Guilliman’s gaze sharpened.

— Meaning.

Ventris replied, and the line felt like a knife drawn clean.

— Teach the Batavi to each other. Force their Legati to carry the same audit language he carries. Make “we do not merge” and “we do not abandon” belong to the Chapter, not to one man.

Guilliman held the silence for a breath, then nodded once.

— Good.

He stepped toward the door.

— You will file this. Sealed.

Ventris answered.

— Yes.

Guilliman paused at the threshold.

— One more thing, Captain.

Ventris waited.

Guilliman’s voice lowered.

— Did you like him.

It was the wrong question for an official debrief. That was why it mattered.

Ventris did not smile.

— Yes, my lord.

Guilliman waited.

Ventris added the truth beneath it.

— And that is why I am dangerous as a witness. Because liking a wall tempts you to lean on it.

Guilliman’s eyes stayed steady.

— Then we are agreed.

Ventris answered.

— Yes.

The locks cycled. The door opened. The noise of war returned, as if the fortress itself resented private truths.

Guilliman let Ventris go, and remained for a breath alone with the conclusion.

A wall that knew how to prevent worship.

An idol that other men would try to build anyway.

And the simplest, cruelest audit rule of all:

The wall must never be allowed to stand alone.
