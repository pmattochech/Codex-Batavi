The Ministorum tithe-ship *Ledger of Saint Mhorus* had already been opened like a fruit.

Red Corsairs moved her spine corridors in looted void-plate and Corsair red, boarding claws still biting the outer hull. Tithe-vault seals screamed under melta. Serfs fled. A confessor tried a litany and was used as a doorstop. The prize was simple: coin, relics, gene-tithe coffers, anything the Ecclesiarchy had fattened for transit.

Lufgt Huron — Blackheart, Tyrant, master of the Corsairs — walked the breach without hurry. The Tyrant’s Claw flexed. Around him, his chosen killed with pirate joy dressed as Astartes efficiency.

— Empty the vaults — he voxed. — Burn what we cannot lift. Leave the corpse-Emperor his receipts in ash.

Then the ship’s internal auspex stuttered.

Not Navy. Not Arbites. Something heavier had matched vectors in the dark and kissed the *Ledger* on the opposite flank — a docking that felt like a vice, not a raid.

Iron-grey Astartes came through the far bulkheads as if the corridor had always belonged to them.

No Chapter badge Huron’s archives could name at a glance. Industrial grey. Wolf-echoes on helm and pauldron geometry — not Fenris theater, not quite. Movement without pirate flourish. They did not howl. They did not announce. They sealed.

Behind the first shield-line came Terminators and veteran killers in a density that made Huron’s smile thin: this was not a random patrol. This was an anchor guard — a lord’s own teeth — though he did not yet have any true name for the formation that moved like a single decision.

At their center walked a giant in artificer mass, iron-headed lupine helm, presence like a sealed gate.

Huron felt the recognition land wrong in his chest.

Not brothers. Not cousins. Something worse: the shape of an argument he had once won for himself. Wardens of a brutal edge. Men who believed the center would not come, so the edge must take and hold by its own law. In the days when he had still worn loyal colours over Badab’s pride, he had told himself a similar story — Maelstrom logic, necessary tyranny, the Imperium’s unpaid bill.

These unknowns had kept the part he threw away.

They had kept the hold.

— Who are you — a Corsair sergeant spat over open vox, already firing.

No answer-name. Only lanes closing. Bolters in friction-efficient bursts. A Red Corsair who charged for glory met a Terminator’s fist and became a lesson in geometry. The iron-grey did not loot. They did not preach. They put bodies between the vault and the exit and made the vault irrelevant.

Huron laughed, low, ugly.

— I know this breed — he told his claw-guard, almost to himself. — Edge-dogs. Wall-priests without a shrine. They look like what we were before we learned the galaxy pays in blood, not ledgers.

He advanced to meet the iron-headed lord because Tyrants do not send sergeants to read mirrors.

The duel was not elegant. Corsair fury and claw-theft met siege mass and refusal. Huron’s claw tore paint and ceramite; the grey lord took the hits the way a breakwater takes tide — not unhurt, unmoved in purpose. Around them, the unnamed elite killed Red Corsairs who still thought boarding was a sport. Tithe-crates became cover. A Ministorum cherub-servitor kept singing inventory until a stray bolt ended the hymn.

Huron hooked, cut, tried to make the grey lord answer in kind — rage for rage.

The grey lord’s voice came calm on open channel, sparse as a stamped form:

— You left the wall.

Huron snarled.

— I took what the Imperium owed the edge—

— You left the wall — the iron head repeated, as if correcting a false coordinate. — This is what failure wears. Red. Loud. Hungry. Empty.

No speech. No chapter history. Just the audit.

For a heartbeat Huron hated them more than Loyalists in gold, more than the Inquisition’s old warrants. Gold he could mock. This was a Chapter that looked like his pre-fall alibi still walking — still holding — while he boarded a priest-ship for coin.

— Name yourselves — Huron demanded, claw locked against a gladius that did not tremble.

The grey lord did not give him a name. Names are doors. He gave him geometry.

— We hold.

Around that single protocol, the iron-grey elite tightened. Corsair boarding lanes collapsed. A squad that had been laughing at serfs died against Terminators who treated laughter as a friction error. The *Ledger*’s tithe-vault remained closed — not from Ministorum faith, from grey bodies that would not kneel to plunder logic.

Huron understood first contact the way a pirate understands a reef: too late to pretend the chart was empty.

These were not Space Wolves playing dress-up.  
Not Ultramarines with worse paint.  
Not his Astral Claws returned as ghosts.

They were the failure-measure: what a frontier Chapter looks like when it refuses to become Corsair.

He disengaged because Tyrants who want a tomorrow do not die on the first unnamed wall. Red Corsairs fell back toward their claws and void-locks, dragging what loot they could, cursing a Chapter without a name in their mouths yet — grey wall-dogs, iron wolves, the ones who will not take.

Behind them, on the *Ledger*’s spine, the iron-headed lord did not chase for glory. He reset the corridor. He stood. He held.

Huron, sealed again in Corsair dark, flexed the Blackheart’s claw and filed the encounter where it hurt.

They look like the lie I told myself before I fell.  
Their lord named the fall without preaching it.  
Next time, we bring enough guns to break a wall — or we do not come.
