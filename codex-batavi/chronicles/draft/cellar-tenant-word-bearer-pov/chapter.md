# [CHRONICLE] Cellar Tenant — Word Bearer first contact

**Classification:** Draft combat narrative — Word Bearer POV · first contact with iron-grey Astartes (Batavi unknown to narrator)  
**When:** Present era (post–CV y13 *Executor* helm on the wolf-skull) — Imperial ~M42 filing approximate  
**Where:** Void-station mid-deck corridor (unnamed to XVII records at contact)  
**Who:** Brother **Kharax** (Word Bearers); Dark Apostle **Malethor**; lesser Neverborn infantry; iron-grey loyalists; the black wolf-skull priest (Varro — unnamed to XVII)

Brother Kharax had already decided the corridor was theirs.

The void-station’s mid-deck stank of vent-steam, bolt-propellant, and opened men. Icons of the True Pantheon hung from Word Bearer pauldrons like trophies cut from a lesser temple. Ahead, the loyalists in iron-grey plate were breaking — not fleeing in a clean rout, but failing the way walls fail: one brace at a time, then all at once.

A Dark Apostle walked the breach as if the deck were a nave.

Apostle Malethor’s crozius was a scripture of brass and screaming faces. Lesser Neverborn loped at the file’s heels — not princes, not throned abominations, only the infantry of the unreal: clawed hunger-shapes, half-born things with too many joints, warp-hounds whose mouths opened sideways. They drank the Word Bearers’ noise the way fish drink current. Every chant fed them. Every corpse-Emperor scream from a dying mortal made them brighter.

Kharax put a bolt through a grey helm and watched the body fold.

— See — Malethor voxed, almost fond. — Russ-get. Wolf marks on iron. Another Fenris bastard-line wearing loyalty like a pelt. Break their priests. Break their howl. The Gods will write the rest.

It fit. The plate had lupine echoes. The survivors fought with a predator’s refusal to yield cleanly. Kharax had killed Space Wolves before. He knew the shape of that arrogance.

Then the air changed.

Not the sweet, loud tear the Legion loved — the warp’s open throat. Something else. The lumen-strips seemed to lean away from the far junction, as if light preferred not to touch what was coming.

The Neverborn felt it first.

A hunger-shape that had been licking marrow from a ruptured cuirass went still. Its sideways mouth closed. Another daemon — all elbows and scripture-rot — hissed without sound and tried to step *behind* a Word Bearer as if ceramite could be a mother’s skirt. The warp-hounds’ ears flattened. Their joy thinned into animal wrongness: not the terror of holy gold, not the flinch before a Saint’s lie, but the instinct of vermin when a cellar door opens and what waits under the stairs is awake.

Malethor’s smile, behind his grille, hardened.

— Hold the song — he ordered. — Something comes to preach.

The grey survivors heard footfalls before the Word Bearers did — or rather, they heard the *absence* that walked with those footfalls. Kharax saw it in them at once: shoulders dropping not in surrender to Chaos, but in a colder recognition. One wounded iron-grey — Kharax had no true name for his Chapter — stopped trying to rise. Another straightened as if a lash had found the exact nerve of duty. A third made a small sound over open vox, not a war-cry.

Fear, yes. Not of Malethor. Not of the Neverborn.

Of the thing arriving.

The figure that entered the junction was Astartes, absolute black over industrial night, robe frayed like a burial shroud, breastplate ribbed as if something had been dug out of a grave and told to stand. Wolf-skull helm. Cowl. Incense venting past the mandible in constant audit-plumes. Where red lenses should have burned, the eyes were fire-orbs without free flame. Above the pack, a thurible sat where an iron halo ought to preach, candles flanking it, flame held against a draft that should have killed it.

The fire-orbs found Kharax first.

Not a glance. A look into the eyes behind the lenses — as if the skull could reach through glass and inventory what lived there. Kharax had stood in circles where Neverborn watched through meat. He did not frighten easily. Still, something in that gaze was not rage and was not mercy. It was judgment practiced as a trade: weigh, pin, end.

Then the orbs moved to Malethor and stayed.

Malethor laughed — genuine, delighted, priest to priest.

— Ah. Their Wolf Priest. Ulrik’s shadow on a lesser world. Come, son of Russ. Sing your ice-god lies. I will answer with the truth that unmade Colchis’ old temples.

Around the black figure, the surviving grey Astartes changed mood the way a pack changes when the headsman enters the yard. No cheer. No relief of rescue. A tightening. One whispered on their net — Kharax’s vox-thieves caught only the tone, not the tongue’s meaning — the tone of men who feared the archenemy less than the sentence walking toward them. As if damnation were theoretical and this priest were *close*, real, able to leave a soul standing while condemning what remained of its humanity to shame and regret.

The Neverborn tried to surge and failed to enjoy it.

They did not flee like weak things before a Golden Throne lie. They *cringed*. One infantry-daemon’s outline frayed where the incense touched the air, not burned by faith-light but denied purchase — as if the room had acquired a predator that ate *attention*. A clawed hunger-shape spat a prayer-fragment it had stolen from a dead mouth and found the fragment dead in its own throat.

Malethor raised his crozius and poured the Pantheon’s names into the corridor.

The black priest answered without matching volume.

On a tight loyalist channel — low, measured, almost clerical — a line landed:

— He carries the weight. We carry the wall.

No answering frenzy from the grey file. Only a crozius lifted once, vertical, ledger opened. Silent liturgy: thurible marking time, incense thickening by will, candles steady. The wounded grey ones stopped bleeding panic into the warp and started bleeding obedience.

Malethor’s eyes narrowed.

— Not Russ — he murmured, first crack in the Fenris theory. — Russ-get howl. This one… hushes.

Bolts crossed. A Word Bearer lost his painted verse mid-syllable. Malethor advanced in a storm of brass scripture, lesser daemons hitching themselves to his wake like remoras. The wolf-skull came to meet him without theater — cold hunt, rosarius shimmer, hammer like an oversized judge’s mallet.

Malethor had expected a Wolf Priest’s rhythm: impact, howl, impact.

What he got was wrong in the joints.

The black priest moved inside the first brass arc as if the Dark Apostle’s timing belonged to a slower species. Not teleportation. Not warp-cheat. Muscle and intent wound past Astartes norm — a body that had lived too long at the edge where predator math replaces pride. The Iron Verdict met the screaming crozius not with matching fury but with placement: a short, ugly redirection that sent Malethor’s weight into a stanchion hard enough to dent scripture.

Lesser Neverborn lunged for the robe’s hem.

The wolf-skull’s free hand caught a hunger-shape by what passed for a throat and *closed*. Ceramite fingers should not have crushed warp-matter that easily. They did. The thing came apart like wet parchment denied a reader. A warp-hound leapt for the thurible; the black priest turned his hip — too fast, too clean — and the hound’s sideways mouth met a rising knee that ended its song mid-yelp.

Kharax fired. The bolt should have taken center mass.

The black priest was already half a step aside, incense plumes drawing a false silhouette where a man had been. Return fire: one pistol crack. The brother beside Kharax sat down without his face.

On the loyalist net — low, measured, no roar — the wolf-skull began to speak as he fought, each line a tool, not a performance:

— Iron remembers what flesh forgets.  
— Flesh obeys because the wall is watching.  
— The wall does not kneel. The wall does not bargain. The wall does not feed the dark its names for comfort.

From the grey wreckage, voices answered — not hysteria, not relief-screams. Action-answer. Bolters that had been dying found lanes. A wounded warrior who had been curling inward stood, planted, and put three rounds into a Neverborn’s knee-proxy until it forgot how to be a knee. Another dragged a fallen brother behind cover with one hand and fired with the other, breath steady.

Kharax tasted it: the fear in them had changed species.

Moments ago they had dreaded the wolf-skull the way condemned men dread a real judge — closer than Gods, able to leave them alive and ashamed. Now the same skull had looked, weighed, and — somehow, without kindness — *kept* them. The corridor’s survivors fought like men told they were still tools worth using. Honour without smile. Approval without embrace. The monster beneath the door had judged them worthy to stand, and standing became possible again.

Malethor felt the spiritual weather shift and hated it.

He poured Colchis into the air — pantheon-names, wound-gospels, the old invitation to make silence scream. Lesser daemons brightened on his breath, then flinched as the black priest’s incense thickened by will. The Neverborn did not burn in golden lie-fire. They simply found less room to *be*. Infantry of the unreal, used to feeding on noise, discovered a predator that ate attention and left them thin.

The wolf-skull stepped through a brass flurry that should have broken an Astartes guard. His crozius — industrial mallet, judge’s geometry — landed in Sentence against a daemon-wedge and unmade it into unfinished hunger. Then he was on Malethor again, faster than the Apostle’s sermon-tempo, strength that made locked weapons scream in both sets of gauntlets.

— He carries the weight that crowns do not understand — the black priest continued, flat, metallic, furnace-hum under the words. — We do not worship the chair. We carry the wall beneath it.  
— He carries the weight.  
— We carry the wall.

The grey file answered with motion: advance a meter, seal a lane, drag a mortal auxilia behind their bulk, kill what tried to cross. No one looked away from the fight to praise him. Praise would have been insult. They fought as proof of receipt.

Malethor laughed blood into his rebreather and swung for the thurible.

— Russ-lie! Ice-shaman trash—

The wolf-skull caught the brass crozius on the Iron Verdict’s haft, pivoted with that wrong agility, and drove Malethor back three steps that felt like a verdict being written. Fire-orbs locked on the Apostle’s lenses — into the eyes, through them — and something pressed into Malethor’s inner sight that was not a corpse-Emperor’s glare, not Fenris’ storm-totems, not a Wolf Priest’s shaman theater.

It was a wolf already past living: ash, iron, penitent posture without kneeling — the skull-face of a hunt that had accepted being the necessary end. Not a god. Not a holy man. The monster that waits beneath the door while lesser men debate whether to open it.

Malethor’s next holy name died half-born.

— From the Needles where the tide-wolf waits — the black priest said, still moving, still killing the space around Malethor as much as Malethor himself. — Into the black water that takes the armored and the proud.  
— Break the joint. Break the skull. Break the second breath before it learns a prayer.  
— No second breath.

— No second breath — came back from the grey net, sparse, certain, and the survivors surged as if the line had been a gate opening outward. An iron-grey met a Word Bearer chainaxe with shield-angle and boot, then finished him with a bolt at contact range. Another cut a Neverborn’s leash-tendril and stamped what remained until it stopped pretending.

They were no longer the breaking wall.

They were the wall that had been inspected and allowed to remain load-bearing.

Kharax saw one of them glance once at the wolf-skull — not for orders, for confirmation — and find it in the simple fact that the skull had not turned Sentence on *them*. That absence was honour. That silence was approval. Their fear of his judgment had not vanished; it had been satisfied, and what remained was clean violence.

Malethor tried to make the duel theological again, forcing a bargain into the clinch: kneel, scream, be rewritten.

The black priest headbutted him — wolf-bone to brass scripture — with force that starred the Apostle’s eye-lenses, then spoke into the crack:

— Do not feed the rift your names.  
— Do not sell your dead for volume.  
— Hold the door shut. Hold it with iron. Hold it with breath you refuse to waste on gods that listen for weakness.

— Hold — the grey answered, and under it rode a nail of certainty: — We hold.

Silent liturgy braided through the spoken: thurible one hard arc; candles unblinking; incense spiked so dense the lesser daemons gagged on absence. The Iron Verdict rose once.

— Named — said the wolf-skull.  
— Weighed — and his fire-orbs did not leave Malethor’s eyes.  
— Ended.

Malethor was not ended yet — Apostles are hard meat — but the word landed in the corridor like a future already scheduled. A Neverborn infantry-thing tried to flee *up* the warp-grain and came apart mid-climb, vermin discovering the cellar’s true tenant had stepped onto the floorboards.

The black priest pressed the Dark Apostle with hammer-work that was not gladiator vanity: each blow a placement, each step a denial of escape lanes, strength above Astartes baseline used like a vice, agility used like a closing hatch. When Malethor’s crozius scored the black pauldron and drew sparks, the wolf-skull did not roar. He adjusted angle and paid the debt back into Malethor’s wrist-servos until brass screamed.

Around them the iron-grey survivors killed without looking hunted.

They looked *kept*.

Physical and spiritual, the duel braided: Malethor’s crozius a conversion argument, Neverborn claws stitching at the black robe’s edges; the wolf-skull refusing argument because argument is a door. Kharax felt it secondhand and hated the despair that came with it — theological despair, the kind that says *there will be no bargain here*.

Malethor spat a pantheon-curse and tried to open the song wider.

The wolf-skull leaned in, fire-orbs in the Apostle’s eyes, and made the song find less air.

In the crash of brass and iron, Kharax understood — too late for pride — that they had not found Russ’s get.

They had found something that used wolf-shapes the way a headsman uses an axe: not as ancestry, as *tool*.

And the tool was looking him in the eyes.

The wolf-skull was not their door against the dark.

He was the monster under it — and his own pack feared his judgment more than Chaos, until that judgment came down as worthiness. Then they stood. Then they answered his longer iron with action. Then the corridor stopped belonging cleanly to the XVII.
