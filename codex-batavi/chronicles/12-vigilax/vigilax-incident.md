# THE VIGILAX INCIDENT: TWO LAWS, ONE CERAMITE FLOOR

*(350.M42 — Vigilax Prime, Castra Vetera Subsector)*

---

## I. THE WORLD THAT WAS NOT A FORGE

Vigilax Prime was not Noviomagus. It had no forges swallowing the horizon, no adamantium in the crust like a backbone. It was a second-tier civilized world — layered hives, stable tithe, Adeptus Mechanicus satisfied enough not to complain aloud. The air in middling cities smelled of wet recycling, cheap oil in the pipes, and cheap incense in neighborhood chapels, all mixed into a single note the *Administratum* called *acceptable productivity*.

The *Administratum* logged the system under **Batavian logistical supervision**: convoy routes, anti-piracy escort, arbitration when local nobles confused tithe with private extortion. It was not occupation. It was geometry. The *Cohors Batavorum* drew the perimeter where the Imperium could still levy without bleeding — a line traced in dispatches, wax seals, and occasional **industrial grey** plate on the docks, never in a victory parade.

That season, the **III Vexillatio** anchored the sector with the routine of those who watch a map: long patrols, short reports, silence on the *vox* when there was nothing to add beyond numbers.

What Vigilax did not expect — what **no** world under logistical mandate expects — was the noise of another law landing without warning.

---

## II. ORBITAL YARD SEVEN HUNDRED FOUR

The Imperial liaison officer entered the code three times. Three times the terminal returned the same dry denial, the same bureaucratic irritation that, on ordinary days, would end in contained cursing and cold tea in the waiting room.

On the third failure, he stopped cursing.

Because the yard was not empty.

The **Marines Malevolent** had disembarked as if stepping off a roof: no queue, no ceremony, none of the patient ritual of those who ask passage. The justification that reached the *vox* was a sentence cut like a razor — captain’s voice, tone of someone who had already decided explanation was weakness:

— *Suspected widespread corruption.*

There was no **Ordos** seal Vigilax authorities could verify in the package. There was no coordination with the Legatus of the III Vexillatio. There was, in the cold language of the records, no **approved operations plan** for that landing — only the arrival of rust-yellow ceramite and eyes behind visors that looked at civilians as if at infected tissue.

The liaison transmitted the alarm. The alarm became a route. The route became fire.

---

## III. SERRAGRIS — THE DISTRICT THAT BURNED

At eighteen hundred local, the lower district **Serragris** began to burn.

There was no declared cult. There was no column of mutants down the main avenue. There was, instead of a clean narrative for pamphlets, what always remains when someone decides to **weaponize fear as policy**: windows bursting under light cannon pressure, corridors smelling of wet powder and melted plascrete, and human voices — thin, ragged — trying to find a stair, a gate, any crack not watched by bolters.

**Medusa** siege guns trained on residential facades as if brick could confess. **Bolters** spoke in rooms where children slept under cheap plascrete ceilings, and the sound was not heroic; it was the dry crack of policy applied with a trigger.

The Malevolent were not there to filter the guilty. They were there to teach a lesson the Imperium had, in theory, already written into a thousand codes — only there the lesson tasted of ash in the mouth and blood in the baseboard.

Captain **Morwen Hale** — a name Batavian records would file without honorific epithet — positioned his company as a ceramite wall between survivors and any escape route. The order, shouted in High Gothic that sounded like a sermon, was simple enough to fit on a single line of horror:

— *No one leaves until the hive temple confesses.*

In the temple, they prayed. In the temple, they dirtied their hands with incense. In the temple, **no one** knew what Hale was talking about.

That did not matter.

What mattered was the geometry: square sealed, rifles raised, civilians pressed like livestock against ferrocrete barriers thrown up in haste. What mattered was the smell — sweat, fear, fuel. What mattered was the obscene fact that, in that instant, Vigilax was two empires at once: the one of columns and tithe, and the one of fire that asked for no receipt.

---

## IV. ACT I — THE VERDICT OF IRON

The first Batavi to cross the ferrocrete barricade was not a line squad. It was **Varro**.

He came without chant. Without an escort of junior chaplains screaming liturgy. The black cowl framed his **wolf-skull helm** — the same savage lineage as Fenrisian **Wolf Priests**, but filed for **Judex** verdict and Chapter law, not pack sacrament — and made his head a point of absence. In that absence, the **crimson** lenses were the only living marks in the penumbra of distant fire. Behind him, twenty legionaries in **industrial grey** kept protocol distance: enough not to look like invasion, enough to form **interlocking fields of fire** if someone misread the geometry. Some of those chassis were already **Primaris** — taller, heavier, the stride of those who learned war on a later assembly line. Varro did not seem larger than them. He seemed bred to the **same war** with **another** ceiling of servos and nerve.

The Chapter does not print pamphlets on **Exanimus**. But unaugmented flesh, in the square, learns without theory: there are transhumans who obey the *Codex* and the weight of ceramite, and there is what happens when the Wolf’s Curse does not destroy the operator — it only **hones** what remained.

Varro did not accelerate on approach. There was no ramp speech. There was only the sound of heavy ceramite on cracked slab — a rhythm that asked no permission, **slow** until the instant it stopped being slow.

When he spoke, the voice did not rise. It did not need to. The frontal plate *vox* carried the metallic tone to the last Malevolent in the square, flat as a blade:

— **Withdraw heavy weapons from the facades.** Pause. Enough for silence to become a question. — **This sector is under Castra Vetera mandate. Your presence is not on the approved operations plan.**

Hale laughed.

It was a dry sound, of someone who had decided mercy was weakness and weakness contagion — and therefore the cure was to make the example before the example was made of him.

— **Chaplain.** He spat the word like blood. — **Do not confuse your rosary with jurisdiction. We burn the plague before it catches your Chapter with its pants down.**

Two Malevolent raised bolters.

It was not theatrical insubordination. It was **habit** — the reflex of a Chapter that learned to resolve ambiguity with caliber.

Varro moved **once**.

Not “fast” in the sense of spectacle. **Fast** in the sense of **perceptual lag**: the human eye in the square registered a leap — Varro here, Varro **already** glued to Hale’s arc — as if someone had deleted a frame. The servos of the nearest Malevolent even whined, lagging: target acquired, solution suggested, **invalid target**; recalculate. A Primaris, in that interval, would have covered half the distance with tactical economy. Varro covered **all** of it and still had margin for the arm to rise.

The ***Crozius Arcanum*** — the Verdict of Iron, an adamantium block on a shaft a common transhuman could barely lift — did not descend in *Sentence Mode*. It came down in **Reeducation Mode**: the inverted field did not pierce the Captain’s ceramite like a knife; it went through it like a wave and **crushed the Black Carapace** beneath with the delicacy of industrial press applied to the nervous system. The arm that delivered the blow did not tremble under recoil. It was not theater strength. It was **torque** of someone carrying hypertrophy and fiber tuned beyond the parameter the *Apothecarion* uses for “standard Astartes” — beyond, too, what **Primaris** chassis usually spends in close combat without overload alarm.

Hale did not fly.

He **folded**.

Knees locked by spasms that were not combat — they were flesh forced to remember, in a single second, that not all pain is honorable. A noise escaped the helm — half scream, half vomit of broken protocol.

The square went quiet in a way different from death’s silence.

It went quiet like a classroom before the first blow — the instant when even a child understands the rule changed without warning.

Varro stood on cracked slab, hammer low, still smoking on low calibration. The smell was not only ozone; it was something almost liturgical, as if the air had been confessed and absolved in the same motion.

— **Noviomagus law does not ask me to explain twice.** The cold was not rage. It was worse: **order** with empty space where once there might have been negotiation. — **Whoever points a weapon at civilians in this perimeter answers to me, not to your temper.**

The two who had raised bolters hesitated.

They hesitated long enough to commit the second error — thinking hesitation bought life.

Varro did not hesitate.

They had trained against Primaris in inter-Chapter exercises — against the long stride, mechanical recoil, firing cadence the *Codex* teaches as “enough.” **Enough** assumed a target on the same reaction curve. Varro was **outside**: finger on the trigger, sights aligning, and it was already too late — not by luck or training failure, but because the time between **deciding** to fire and the round **crossing** space no longer matched where the Chaplain **was**: their motor lag became ballistic error.

Two shots from **Absolution** — one per skull, through the visor, with the symmetry of someone closing parentheses before the sentence ends. Work too clean to be called rage; efficient too much to be called passion. Then the *Crozius* changed protocol with the same calm as swapping tools in a workshop — except no workshop teaches the hand to move **like that** without dragging the shoulder, without compensation, as if joint and mass had been recalibrated for war one notch above the **STC** baseline.

The Malevolent **battle banner** — yellowed cloth stitched with the mask and chain symbol — still trembled, lashed to the portable pole, when Varro reached the mast. The distance was short; even a common Astartes would close it in a breath. Varro closed like a **cut**: one step that admitted no argument, arm raising mass that would make a Primaris lock the elbow stabilizer for an instant — and in Exanimus there was no instant, there was **continuity**, the blow already at apex while the observer’s brain still registered intent.

There was no speech. There was **maximum-capacity impact**: the Imperial blessed fabric, reinforced to resist shrapnel, was not torn — it was **atomized** in a band of heat that smelled of ozone and liturgical ash, as if the heraldry itself had been judged and found too heavy to stand.

The pole bent like soft metal rods — not from material weakness, but from **excess** force at a point common armor does not usually spend in a single strike.

The remaining Malevolent did not kneel.

They **froze**.

And in that freeze — hands still on weapons, but brain one tick behind the world — Vigilax learned a lesson the *Ministorum* would prefer not to print on pamphlets: there are silences that do not console.

**They notify.**

## V. NEWS OUTRUNS THE DECREE

What happens in a square becomes echo in a tube.

What happens in a square with Astartes becomes **operational myth** before sunset — not the pretty myth, the myth that makes a sergeant hold his breath and a foreman change corridors.

In sector **Marna-Down**, forty kilometers of tube line away, a minimal Malevolent escort — six Astartes — decided that **anticipating sentence** was loyalty. Maybe they believed the plague narrative. Maybe they only liked the feeling of having an accused with no counsel. The result, in dry logistics language, was the same: they knocked down a civil barrier and lined ten workers against an industrial ceramite wall, as if preparing a human file for binding.

The first bolter shot was not a warning.

It was routine execution.

The answer did not come from Varro.

It came from **three Batavi squads** in position before the first shot — because the Legatus of the III Vexillatio, alerted by **Drusus**, had drawn **triangulation** while the Chaplain still walked Serragris square. It was not prophecy. It was calculation: if one focus blew, another would try to “close the account” before someone on Noviomagus could say *contained.*

When the Malevolent roar met air, the answer was *siege discipline* — suppressing fire, crossed angles, Batavian geometry laid like a technical blanket over panic. The six fell. Four alive. The two who tried fragmentation grenades against the civilian cluster did not survive the count — not cheap vengeance, but a simple rule: **immediate threat to non-combatants** gets terminal priority.

When the noise dropped, what remained was hot metal smell, cough, and the wet sound of someone trying to find kin among bodies that were not kin.

**Drusus** entered the perimeter after.

Without field-medic haste.

With all the haste of someone who would **document** before civilian memory became the wrong number in the report.

He did not kneel first on wounded Malevolent. He knelt on protocol time: triage, minimal stabilization, sample. The voice, clinical, went to the registrar strapped to his forearm — not to survivors, who heard anyway, because hearing is what remains when you cannot flee.

— **Reaction temperature beyond parameter for standard armor thermal stress.** **Mark as *Vermilion*, inter-Chapter incident, sample B.**

Surviving Malevolent heard the file name as one hears sentence without formal trial — and they were partly right: *Vermilion* was not prison.

It was a **box**.

And boxes, in the *Apothecarion*, have a habit of outlasting men.

## VI. ACT II — THE CASTELLAN’S HANDS

The **Marines Malevolent Chapter Master** — a title Batavian records respect for punctuation, not sympathy — demanded a **reckoning audience** on open Imperial channel.

Demanded as if he still believed a loud shout restored hierarchy.

The meeting took place in the transition chamber of the cruiser ***Gray Gargoyle***, neutral platform agreed under protest — neutral on paper, claustrophobic in reality: matte metal walls, emergency lighting permanently “temporary,” adamantium table polished until it looked liquid at certain angles. The air smelled of standby shield ozone and old tobacco no one smoked there but that clung to uniforms of those who lived in corridors.

Present: the Batavi triumvirate in reduced format — **Alaric** at the head of his own gravity, **Varro** a shadow in **wolf-skull helm** and **crimson** lenses (Fenrisian **Wolf Priest** lineage, **Judex** purpose — not pack rite), **Drusus** behind the clinical observation line, registrar lit in silent mode.

Present also: the Malevolent Master with minimum honor guard — yellowed ceramite like a dry wound, hands in fists.

An **Inquisitor** of the Ordo Hereticus, name sealed in Vigilax public records, with the face of someone who had written “complex” on too many reports to believe pretty words.

And, to the right of the table, motionless as obsidian statues, two **Black Templars** — invited not as arbiters but as **witnesses of faith** the *Segmentum* sent so no one would call it a *private accord between wolves.* They did not have judgment eyes. They had **archive** eyes: everything said there would live later on stamped paper.

The Malevolent Master was not a foolish strategist.

He was an **efficient** fanatic.

He lowered his helm before the Inquisitor — gesture of protocol respect, minimum necessary theater — and lifted his chin to Alaric as if pressing forehead to glass.

— **You killed mine.** The voice was sandpaper. — **You humiliated a Captain before plebeians. You burned our banner like heretics burn icons.**

Alaric did not move.

*Castra-Lupus* made his stillness a second layer of armor — not posture of challenge, posture of **wall**: something that does not argue with the tide, only endures until it tires.

— **Yours killed civilians without mandate.** The answer came so low the Inquisitor leaned a millimeter, as if sound were a needle to fish from air. — **The Chaplain applied Castra Vetera law. I endorse.**

The Malevolent Master smiled under the helm — or something Drusus’s sensors logged as muscular elevation compatible with a smile.

— **Law of the betrayed wolf**, — he said, and perhaps knew, the instant the syllable left, he had crossed a line that was not tactical. The voice rose a step, gained the venom of someone who had saved the phrase for the right occasion. — **You cut the throat of the brother who wore your symbol. The pretend *Lupercal*. Do not give me wall now, Castellan — you already showed what you do to your own flesh when it shames you.**

The following silence was not Varro’s.

It was the vacuum **before decompression** — the second when even the air seemed to step back to see what would happen.

Alaric crossed the four meters of the table in **two steps**.

No gladius.

No sidearm.

No the clean *Codex* protocol for officers fallen in disgrace — that choreography that turns death into a publishable lesson.

There were **hands**.

*Castra-Lupus* was not only armor; it was the live tonnage of the man who wore it. Fingers engulfed the Malevolent Master’s helm under the chin — hook, twist, **compression** with industrial-machine torque until ceramite groaned and seal plastic burst like old fruit skin. The scream was muffled first; then **wet**, intimate in an obscene way for a room full of witnesses.

Drusus saw numbers — pressure, time, target heart rate, saturation, peak — and did not intervene.

It was not cruelty.

It was observation: that was not a duel.

It was **limit** being drawn with bone and metal.

When Alaric let go, the body slid along the table — a dry, ridiculous, almost domestic noise — and stopped before the Inquisitor like an offering no one had asked for.

— **Heresy is your word**, — said Alaric, still without raising his voice. The calm was not kindness; it was **continuity** of the wall after impact. — **Treason I have already judged. Do not confuse the tribunals.**

The Black Templars did not move hands to weapons.

One of them — the oldest, tabard stained with campaign ash — made the only gesture the scene would get from them: **crossed arms** over the chest, as if closing a book whose last page had been written before the meeting began.

The Inquisitor nodded once.

Not moral approval.

**Observation**: that would be reported. What happened after depended on how much Terra wanted blood between Astartes on the *Nihilus* frontier — and how much Vigilax could scrub from the floor before the next convoy landed with questions.

## VII. EPILOGUE — THE PRICE OF THE NAME

The Malevolent withdrew from Vigilax in the silence of an open wound — not an orderly victory withdrawal, withdrawal of those who carry a leader’s body and pack undeclared hatred.

In the following years of the Silent Vigil, **mixed patrols** that included that yellowed armor pattern turned about-face when the transponder identified *Castra-Lupus* in the field — not by written order, but by **muscle memory** of those who saw what happens when insult is mistaken for tactic.

Drusus filed the *Vermilion* package with a one-line addendum:

> *Castra Vetera intervention at Vigilax Prime, 350.M42 — jurisdictional clarification by force. Residual psychic stress in civilian population: moderate. Residual psychic stress in foreign Astartes: elevated.*

Varro did not comment on the report.

It was not guilty omission.

It was coherence: some truths do not improve with discussion — only with **silence that notifies.**

Alaric returned to the Noviomagus *Strategium* as one returns to an interrupted equation — **no visible satisfaction**, no perceptible relief on sensors, no cheap charge of sated vengeance. To an outside observer, nothing in the Vigil had changed.

For the Malevolent, **everything** had changed on the ceramite floor where a Chapter Master learned, with the thickness of his own neck, that there are borders even holy hatred does not cross unpaid.

And Vigilax — poor Vigilax, second-tier world, stable tithe — was left with two laws in memory: the one of columns, and the one of impact.

Two laws.

One floor.

And the sound, still sometimes dreamed in Serragris, of something heavy descending **once** — and enough.
