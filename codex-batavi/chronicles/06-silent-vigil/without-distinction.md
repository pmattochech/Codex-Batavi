# NO DISTINCTION

*ca. 280.M42 — Proximal Sector, Cicatrix, Castra Vetera Subsector*

---

The last casualty was collected at three hours and forty-two minutes into the night watch.

Legatus Halvard logged the number on the campaign table terminal — seventeen dead, forty-two wounded, two with permanent reduced-service prognosis — then stood a moment staring at the figure. Not for grief. Grief would be processed on schedule, with the chaplain, in documented form. For the arithmetic. Six hundred three enemies eliminated. Seventeen brothers lost. The math was favorable in any doctrinal analysis. The position was secure. The proximal sector would stay closed at least three more cycles before pressure escalated again.

Complete operational victory.

Halvard powered down the terminal and said to the void:

— Post-campaign protocol. Phase one.

---

The voice needed no audience. The sergeants already knew. Sergeants of the I Vexillatio always knew before the order, because the order was always the same and had been the same longer than any of them could trace. The protocol had no author on record. It had accreted — each Legatus before the I Vexillatio adding a layer to the procedure, cauterizing a weakness seen in himself or read in the record of those who came before.

The result was a protocol that celebrated nothing.

No banner-raising. No mention of enemies eliminated in orders of the day. No rest leave before maintenance obligations were complete. No collective unit praise nor individual squad praise.

What there was was work.

---

Phase one lasted six hours. Armor open, components out, every piece inspected and logged. Ceramite that had taken impact was checked for microfracture before any other assessment — not because general protocol demanded it before biochemical evaluation, but because the I Vexillatio had learned that Marines with certain Graven State variants tended to undervalue physical damage in the immediate post-combat window. Pride left in a Marine’s Furor Graven State did not sit well with admitting compromised plate. Checking ceramite first removed pride from the equation.

That was knowledge written nowhere. It lived in sergeants’ hands, passed one to another as field technique rather than psychological containment doctrine.

Halvard maintained his own gear without delegating. Not performative humility — protocol. A Legatus of the I Vexillatio had no armor assistant during phase one. The rule had been instituted by someone the record did not preserve, and the reason was obvious enough that no one needed it written: a Marine who hands maintenance duty to another Marine is placing something above the present task. Anything above the present task was the first millimeter of drift the protocol existed to prevent.

He worked in silence. Around him, in silence, the rest of the unit did the same.

---

Phase two was caloric replenishment and minimum regulatory sleep — four hours for light wounded, six for the rest, with the *Apothecarion* field biochemistry protocol interleaved. The unit’s field technician was not a full Apothecary, but had been trained by Drusus personally to recognize Graven State instability indicators specific to post-combat context. He knew the difference between normal physical exhaustion and exhaustion pressing the contours of the Axiom.

The immediate post-combat interval was the most dangerous point.

On campaign, Graven State had direction: enemy ahead, task at hand, the *Axioma Rationis* as chain holding biology within acceptable parameters. In the pause after victory, direction vanished. Intensity accumulated during engagement remained in the system with no object to aim at. In Marines from other units, that dissipated with time and rest. In Marines of the I Vexillatio, there was a specificity: they knew what happened when objectless intensity met the void. They had read the record. They had gone down to the cells in the previous sector.

Halvard had first gone down to the cells as a sergeant, under the Legatus who had held command before him. The Legatus had not explained why he brought new sergeants down. He had simply descended, opened the outer containment grate, and stood before the occupant long enough for smell, scale, and presence of what was inside to stop being conceptual.

Halvard had understood at once. He had needed no explanation.

---

The chaplain visit was phase three.

The I Vexillatio had no resident chaplain. It had a visit cycle — the proximal-sector chaplain rotated through units, and the I Vexillatio topped the priority list regardless of operational state. That too had been instituted by someone with no name on record, and the current chaplain had accepted the priority without question because the protocol accompanying the visit was documented and clinically justified.

The I Vexillatio chaplain visit was not confession in standard doctrinal sense. It was calibration. The chaplain asked specific questions — about state at combat peak, about the moment immediately after engagement ended, about anything during the campaign that had resonated with Graven state in an unusual way. Answers were logged. Those diverging from the Marine’s historical pattern were flagged for follow-up.

Halvard had sat with the chaplain, this time, and answered the questions in the usual way. Then there had been a long pause — the chaplain was a man of few words even by frontier standards — and the chaplain had asked, so directly there was no evading:

— Was the six hundred three weighing on you?

Halvard had taken three seconds to answer.

— No — he said. — The seventeen was.

The chaplain had noted. Then had said:

— The seventeen should weigh. It is the correct number to weigh.

Halvard had been silent. Then had said:

— And the six hundred three?

— It should be work — the chaplain said. — Nothing more than that.

It was the correct answer. Halvard knew it was correct before he asked. He had asked because he needed to hear it from outside, this time. His Graven State was not pure pride — something more irregular, a mix Drusus had catalogued as “predominantly Type I with chronic grief saturation” and had written a note Halvard had read once and then asked never be read to him again. Not because it was wrong. Because it was too precise to be useful in the field.

What was useful in the field was the Axiom. And the Axiom, after two continuous campaign cycles and seventeen dead, needed external calibration.

The chaplain provided the calibration. Halvard left the meeting with the same weight he had entered — but the weight in the correct position.

---

Phase four was the descent.

The proximal sector’s cells were smaller and older than Noviomagus Prime’s. Four occupants total — two who had chosen the Iron Prison decades before, one contained before the Testament was formalized, one whose consciousness lapses were still consistent enough to keep him classed as active witness rather than relocate to *Adversarii at Portas*.

Halvard went down alone. Always alone, in this phase. He brought young sergeants only on first visits — afterward each made the path alone, in the time needed, without audience and without mandatory report to the Legatus. The report went to the *Apothecarion* technician, who assessed duration and posture, not emotional content.

Emotional content was private. That was the point.

Halvard stood before the first cell for a time he did not log. The occupant was at the back — the consciousness lapse had passed that cycle, meaning nothing behind the eyes recognized the Legatus’s presence. A 4.3-meter beast leaned against reinforced ceramite wall, breathing slow and steady, no reaction to Astartes scent outside.

Halvard watched.

There was nothing specific to see. It was the same as the last visit, and the one before, and every visit since he had taken command. The descent’s function was not novelty. It was repetition. It was the task of keeping the physical datum present and not conceptual — of ensuring the weight the Axiom carried did not lose the reality that justified its weight.

A Marine who has not descended to the cells recently begins to treat Execratio as doctrinal abstraction. And doctrinal abstraction holds nothing.

Halvard stayed until he felt the datum was present again. Then he went up.

---

The I Vexillatio post-campaign protocol lasted sixteen hours total. Afterward the unit was functional for new orders. No banners. No praise. No honor-book entry that was not operational.

Halvard wrote the phase-four final report on the terminal and watched the unit status field. All indicators within parameters. The *Apothecarion* technician had flagged two Marines for extended follow-up — not as alert, as precaution. They were the correct precautions for the correct state types.

The I Vexillatio was operational.

Halvard killed the terminal light. Somewhere above him, two sectors and five hundred years away, Lord Castellan Alaric von Helis ruled from an Adamant Throne without removing his armor. The Chapter functioned. The frontier held. And the I Vexillatio had left the field with six hundred three eliminated and seventeen dead and no new dishonor to add to the weight it already carried.

It was not distinction.

It was enough. It was precisely enough. And anything beyond would be the first millimeter of drift the protocol existed to prevent.

Halvard slept the six regulatory hours without making room for more.
