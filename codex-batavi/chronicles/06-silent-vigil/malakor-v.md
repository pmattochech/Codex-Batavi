# THE HOOK

**Date:** 050.M42  
**Theater:** Malakor V — peripheral sector of the Nine Phalanx, Cicatrix Maledictum influence zone  
**Characters:** Alaric von Helis, Drusus (rear guard), Varro (doctrinal witness), the Versibar of the I Vexillatio  
**Scope:** **Valerian Heresy** — **I** *Prima* rot; **~300** Chapter losses (**KIA** / **Versibar**) in the wider Malakor theater; first **documented** **battlefield** ***Adversarii at Portas*** correlation ([`projection-aurea-wolfs-curse.md`](../../biological-encyclopedia-bestiary/projection-aurea-wolfs-curse.md) — **Exanimus Testament**).

---

## I. THE HOOK

The intelligence was planted three weeks in advance.

A patrol vector nudged slightly off pattern — not careless enough to read as a trap, but careless enough to read as fatigue. A squad rotation two days late in the log. A supply report that left a four-hour window at a secondary access point to Malakor V’s quarantine zone.

Any experienced commander would read operational wear. An experienced commander who had spent years mapping Alaric’s style, learning which patterns he trusted and where his geometry had the lowest cost of entry — that commander would read opportunity.

Alaric counted on it.

Neither Drusus nor Varro had suggested the approach. Alaric had, alone in the *Strategium*, with a sector map and forty-three years’ knowledge of how one specific man made decisions. The man he had trained. Whose campaign plans had come from the same tactical tables as his own, across three decades of war that had made them indistinguishable as strategists.

That was the problem. And the solution.

*“Mobilize the III and V Vexillationes”* — he had said when the first report arrived, three engagements back. Then the second. Then the third, with logs of two squadrons wiped and enough survivors for the whole sector to know what had happened and have no doubt who had done it.

Each failure was an argument. Each pullback was the proof Valerius wanted to manufacture: that the Chapter could not contain what it had created.

Alaric built the hook on the same table where he had built campaigns with the First Legatus. With the same method. Using the same knowledge that made them equivalent as tacticians.

The only element Valerius lacked was forty-three years’ knowledge of how Valerius made decisions.

---

## II. THE FIELD

**Valerian Heresy — theater accounting:** Malakor V was never only a corridor. **I** *Prima*’s treason had spread across **rot**-linked anchors; **Blutrecht** Tier 3 had **locked** **loyal Versibars** onto **traitor gene-seed** while Lord Castellan **Alpha** **imposed** **predation geometry** — irrational mass **aimed**, not Chapter-wide **catastrophe**. *Strategium* later files the engagement as the first **documented** **battlefield** ***Adversarii at Portas*** correlation: **distinct** from **terminal** Black Cell release (facility lost / indiscriminate **Portas**). Before Alaric met **Valerius** in the maze, the **blood-price** was already **~300** names — against **~500** brothers still **rational** when the **crucible** ended. **~200 Firstboot** would remain on line when the ash settled. The duel was **sentence** on the **First Legatus**, not the whole war.

The interception sector was industrial — extraction platforms abandoned after Cicatrix pressure made mining unviable. Oxidized metal, choked vent chambers, scaffolding no one had inspected in decades. Geography Valerius knew from the same map Alaric had used to build the hook.

The Versibar arrived on the predicted vector.

Alaric was waiting.

No visible troop — the III and V Vexillationes in containment positions four hundred meters out, ordered not to advance regardless of sensor readings. Single combat had been part of the design from the start. Two commanders who had known each other for decades, in space both had mapped: any other geometry would introduce variables neither needed.

The beast that entered the access corridor stood 4.5 meters. Alaric’s *Castra-Lupus* stood 3.5. Scale difference was irrelevant to either — both knew the other’s limits with precision no external sensor could measure.

Across thirty years of war Alaric had taught Valerius the foundations of Batav close-combat doctrine: economy of motion, pressure geometry, the difference between the strike that closes distance and the strike that only seems to. The Versibar of the I Vexillatio used those foundations with competence that, in any other context, would have made the instructor proud.

Alaric answered the pattern as one who had built the pattern.

The voice from the lupine throat was human. Clear.

> *“You planted the report.”*

Alaric did not answer. He adjusted the *Castra-Lupus*’s stance to compensate for the next strike’s angle of entry.

*“I knew it was a hook.”* The beast fell back half a step, reassessing. *“I came anyway. You know why?”*

The right shoulder field absorbed the return impact. Alaric took two steps left — the only move available in that corridor’s geometry without yielding ground.

*“Because even with a hook, you needed four tries.”* Valerius’s voice held something that was not rage. Closer to demonstration. *“Four. To catch a Marine who never needed the Axiom of Reason to hold his mind.”*

The force sword left a thirty-centimeter scar on the *Castra-Lupus* plastron.

*“You came back from Execratio because Varro kept screaming your name.”* The Versibar advanced in an arc — Valerius’s favored maneuver in confined space, learned in the Incus Gravis tunnels, executed with the transformed stage’s added mass. *“I went in alone and I’m still here. With my mind. With no one screaming my name.”*

Alaric let the arc close, absorbed the shoulder impact on the *Castra-Lupus* flank, and used the Versibar’s mass against it — three tons of ceramite and steel meeting four and a half of altered flesh in a corridor with geometry too tight for both.

*“That is not proof of superiority”* — said Alaric.

The first words in forty-one minutes of combat.

The Versibar stopped for one second.

> *“It was the only thing I needed to hear.”*

*“It was not an answer to your argument.”* Alaric adjusted his force sword grip. *“It was a diagnosis.”*

---

## III. THE SENTENCE

The forty-third minute.

The Versibar was on its knees — not from lack of strength, but because the fight’s final angle had been built across forty-two minutes of incremental pressure, each position shift closing an option until only that one remained. The kind of geometry with no visible moment of decision; only an inevitable result that seems to come from nowhere.

Valerius knelt on Malakor V’s oxidized metal, right arm pinned by the *Castra-Lupus*’s weight, Versibar eyes — yellow, slit, fully aware — turned on Alaric.

There was no fear in them.

*“You will carry this.”* The voice was still clear. *“Three hundred names. Everything the First was. You will carry all of it.”*

Alaric raised the force sword.

*“You were carrying enough before me. I know the weight of the cord. I watched you add the plates. You will add three hundred more tonight and you will keep walking as you always have, because that is all you know how to do.”* A pause. *“I do not regret it. You need to know that. Of nothing.”*

Alaric struck.

No words before. No words after.

The sector went silent for four seconds.

Then Alaric’s vox keyed.

*“Sector clear.”* One-second pause. *“Biometric data collection authorized. Drusus’s team may advance.”*

He stood in the corridor while confirmation reports arrived from the four containment points. He looked at what had been First Legatus of the *Cohors Batavorum* long enough to confirm combat had ended.

Then he walked toward the exit.

---

## IV. THE CHAIN

Malakor V, night of the same day.

Drusus reached the corridor with the full *Apothecarion* team. He catalogued biometric data with routine-procedure efficiency — impact angle, penetration depth, incapacitation time, failure model of the altered physiological systems. He logged everything in the same language he used for any Versibar from any campaign.

The data carried no annotation on the subject’s identity.

There was, however, a separate report entry — marked *Vermilion* at once, Triumvirate-restricted access:

> *“Das Blutrecht stage: atypical manifestation. Absence of acute phase. Immediate rebound to deep depressive phase. Hypothesis: when the object of focused hatred is eliminated by the bearer, purpose-collapse is immediate and total. There is no resolution phase. The biological system that organized affective state around the target has no protocol for the target’s absence. Continuous monitoring authorized. Pharmacological intervention not recommended: the state is biological, not clinical. It has no cure. It has duration.”*

Drusus closed the report.

Then he watched Alaric across the landing field.

The Castellan stood by the transport *Thunderhawk*, back to the sector. He was not looking at anything. He was not doing anything. He simply stood — with the *Castra-Lupus*’s weight, and the plates’ weight, and forty-three years of a story that had ended in that corridor with a force-sword stroke and no words.

Drusus did not approach.

That night, aboard the return ship, Alaric spent three hours alone in the mobile *Apothecarion*. Drusus left the instrumentation running and walked out.

When he returned, there were **~300** new plate pairs on the cord — the **Valerian Heresy** ledger, largest single-event accumulation since the **crucible** years: **I** *Prima* rot, loyal dead, **Versibars** judged and catalogued, without distinction of rank or length of service except what the *Apothecarion* required.

Drusus ran a recent-plate auspex scan.

He was checking the count.

He stood still a moment when the data confirmed what the count showed.

There were three hundred pairs.

There were not three hundred and one.

He powered down the scanner and left without logging the observation in any report.

---

## V. THE FIRST DAY OF RAIN

Noviomagus Prime, three days later.

Varro remained on Malakor V for twelve hours after Alaric’s ship had departed. What he did in those twelve hours remains undocumented — field sensors were off, and no coverage team member was ordered to monitor the Chaplain.

When he boarded, he went straight to the flagship *Strategium*. He gathered the Chapter’s eight Chaplains in a chamber with no open vox and spoke the oath that would come to be known in Noviomagus’s corridors simply as *Varro’s word*.

Alaric was present.

He stayed silent while Varro spoke. When the oath ended, he said nothing. He did not deny. He did not qualify. He did not bargain.

Drusus, present, logged that silence as the only moment he had seen the Castellan yield a slice of authority without demanding any condition in return.

---

On Noviomagus Prime, it rained that week — routine acid rain, nothing exceptional in the northern hemisphere’s weather logs. No datum tied the date to the Malakor V incident.

It was not omen. It was coincidence — or the kind of coincidence Batavi learn not to discuss aloud, because the universe rarely cares about cause order.

Batavi who lived the Silent Vigil do not date the beginning by weather. They date it by what changed in the corridors: silence that stopped being only discipline and became habit, the sense that something had been torn from the Chapter’s chest and nothing had filled the void again.

When they speak of “before” and “after,” they speak of Malakor V — not the sound of water on the roof.

Of how the Castellan walked the *Strategium*.

Of how often the command chamber’s lights stayed on past midnight.

Of the fact that when someone entered a room where Alaric stood alone, there was a second — only a second — before the Castellan registered presence. Not because he had failed to perceive. But because he was somewhere unnamed in Drusus’s biometric reports and beyond the *Axioma Rationis*’s reach.

Varro said nothing about that.

Drusus logged the telemetry and filed *Vermilion*.

The Silent Vigil would last **one hundred years**. What changed was the **weight** inside the armor — not a dome that ran fast; a man who stopped being **present** while still standing in every room that mattered.

---

*The period that follows (050.M42 – 150.M42) is catalogued internally as the Silent Vigil. The Chapter’s formal records from that span document campaigns, Vexillatio rotations, alliances, and losses. The informal records — corridor talk, neophyte accounts that never understood why the Castellan seemed to carry more weight than any cord could measure — were not filed.*

*They did not need to be.*
