# THE TITHE OF SWEETNESS

**I. The Anomaly in the Market Sector**

For Neophyte Davian, newly raised from the tenth scout company to the front line, the world was a grid of threat vectors and suppression zones. His tactical visor overlaid green data on Noviomagus’s gray reality: *Subject A: Civilian, medium workload. Structure B: Stable. Threat level: Zero.*

He was patrolling the South Sector Market, following the **Doctrine of Shared Sweat**. Minutes earlier he had used transhuman strength to free a stuck ore hauler with a broken axle—a job that would take ten men an hour, which he finished in twelve seconds. Efficiency. Tithe. Duty.

Davian was about to resume his route when his armor’s proximity sensor chirped. It was not a fast threat. It was slow, organic movement.

A small old woman, bent by years of hive gravity, blocked his path. Davian’s visor tagged her: *Helena K., retired weaver, threat level: None.*

Davian stopped, hand hovering over his bolter by pure muscle reflex. The woman did not tremble. She did not bow in worship of the “Emperor’s Angel.” She smiled. The wrinkles on her face looked like terrain Davian did not know how to navigate.

— For you, son — she said, voice weak but firm.

She held out her hand. In the callused palm lay a sun-pear. The fruit was rare on Noviomagus, grown in the upper agri-domes, golden and fragrant. A luxury for someone of her caste.

Davian froze. His combat protocols had no answer for that. *A bribe?* No—she asked for nothing. *A religious tribute?* She was not praying. *A biological trap?*

— Citizen — Davian’s voice came metallized through the vox, hard and defensive. — There is no tithe-collection protocol in this sector today. Retain your resources.

Helena laughed. A dry but warm sound. — Not tithe for the Throne, iron boy. For you. For the weight you carried back there. You must be thirsty.

She took the giant’s armored hand, opened ceramite fingers that could crush stone, and set the fruit there. — Eat. It’s good for the blood.

Before Davian could process the protocol breach, she turned and vanished into the crowd. The neophyte stood in the middle of the market holding the golden pear like an active plasma grenade about to detonate.

**II. Analysis of the Object (The Sanctum Strategium)**

The door to Drusus’s laboratory opened. Davian marched in, fruit still in hand, and found the Triumvirate assembled. Alaric studied stellar maps. Varro polished his crozius. Drusus calibrated a gene-seed extractor.

— Report, Neophyte — Alaric ordered, without turning.

— Sir — Davian struck his chest with his fist but kept the fruit in the other hand. — An unregulated civilian interaction occurred. A local inhabitant handed me this… biological item. No request. No exchange of service. I suspect population conditioning failure. They seem to have forgotten the fear function our presence should inspire. Should I discard the object for toxin analysis?

Alaric turned. He looked at the pear in the soldier’s hand. Then at Varro and Drusus.

— Analysis — Alaric asked.

Drusus approached, tactical monocle humming as it scanned the fruit in the neophyte’s hand. — Item: sun-pear. Origin: Agri-dome 4. Composition: fructose, water, fiber. Toxicity level: Null. Nutritional value: Low for Astartes physiology, but considered a high monetary delicacy for a lower-class civilian.

— And the spiritual intent? — Alaric asked, looking at Varro.

The Chaplain, in his skull helm, moved closer to Davian. — Did she call you “sir”? “Angel”?

— Negative, Chaplain — Davian answered, confused. — She called me “son.”

Varro made a guttural sound of approval. — Then it is not conditioning failure, Neophyte. It is recognition of function. She did not see the weapon. She saw the shield. And shields need not be feared—they need to be kept.

Davian looked at the fruit. — I do not grasp tactical utility. If I eat, it is a waste of civilian resource. If I do not eat, it is an offense to interaction protocol.

Alaric sighed. The sound was like steam leaking from a boiler. He pushed off the tactical table. — Theoretical instruction was inefficient — the Chapter Master said. — Keep the artifact, Davian. We are going to the practical lesson.

**III. The Laboratory of the Soul (The Kitchen)**

Davian expected to be taken to the simulation chamber or the chapel. Instead Alaric guided him to the palace’s rear, to a sector where weapon oil’s smell was replaced by roast meat and bread.

When they entered Martha’s enlarged kitchen, Davian’s auditory sensors needed a second to filter the chaos. Children were running. There was laughter.

And there was **Elara Solis**.

Davian stopped in the doorway. He had seen Admiral Solis at military parades and on vox transmissions. To neophytes she was the “Root of the Wall,” a near-mythic figure of authority and strategic cold, untouchable and sacred.

But the woman at the table wore no medals. She wore a simple tunic. She was laughing at something Jonas had said, and there were bread crumbs at the corner of her mouth. Little Leo was trying to put a spoon in her shirt pocket.

— Master Alaric? — Davian whispered, shocked. — Perimeter security is compromised. The Admiral is… exposed.

— She is home, Davian — Alaric answered, gently pushing the neophyte inside.

Martha, matriarch of that chaos, spotted the newcomers. She did not bow. She wiped her hands on her apron and walked straight to Davian, who stepped back one pace, intimidated by that small woman’s authority. She looked at the pear in his hand.

— Ah, I see Mistress Helena liked your patrol — Martha said, smiling. — She grows the best pears in the sector, but never eats them. She saves them for whoever “deserves” them.

Without asking permission, Martha took the pear from Davian’s armored hand. The neophyte looked at Alaric, expecting an order to intercept the theft, but Alaric only watched.

Martha went to the counter, cut the fruit into precise slices, drizzled a thread of honey, and set it on a plate. She returned and pushed the plate against Davian’s ceramite breastplate.

— Sit down, boy — she ordered him. — You cannot protect the world if you do not know its taste.

\--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

**IV. The Final Lesson (The Taste of the Tithe)**

Davian sat at the table’s end, rigid as a statue. He held the plate. Around him the “Royal Pack” continued dinner. Drusus explained anatomy to Sofia using a chicken bone. Varro let Mia decorate his power staff with ribbons.

Then Alaric sat beside Davian. And did the unthinkable.

The Chapter Master raised his hands to his neck and unlocked his helm. The hermetic seal hissed. Alaric removed his war-face, revealing a scar-marked visage and centuries of duty. He took a piece of bread from the table and ate.

— You asked if they forgot you are a weapon — Alaric said, voice now human, unfiltered, rough and paternal. — The answer is no. They know exactly what you are, Davian. They saw what I did at Incus Gravis. They know we are monsters when we must be.

Alaric pointed at the sliced pear on the neophyte’s plate.

— But they chose to treat the weapon as a son. Know why? — Alaric looked at Elara, who smiled at him from across the table. — So the weapon never forgets for whom it fires. The fruit is not nutrition, Davian. It is the bond. If you do not accept their sweetness, you become only cold iron. And cold iron breaks.

Davian looked at the plate. He looked at Elara, who was now wiping Leo’s mouth. He looked at Martha, who watched him with expectation.

With trembling hands, the neophyte unlocked his own helm. The kitchen air smelled like family. He took a slice of pear and put it in his mouth.

Sweetness exploded on his tongue. It was not combat ration. It was not nutrient paste. It was flavor. It was sun. It was gratitude.

— It is… efficient — Davian said, voice failing, eyes burning with moisture his implants could not suppress.

— It is sweet — Martha corrected, running her hand through the boy’s short hair. — Welcome to the table, son.

That night, Neophyte Davian learned the Limes-Lupus Chapter’s most important lesson. He learned the most valuable tithe was not promethium or bullets, but the ability to sit at table with those he protected and remember that, under the ceramite, he was still human enough to like pears.
