# Malakor V — Corbec vs Vorn (open-field duel)

The place he chose was open ground, because he wanted witnesses.

Not the kind that survive. The kind that die understanding the lesson.

We found the field by following its silence. Gunfire had been thick across Malakor’s flats for hours — stuttering bolters, distant detonations, the metallic crack of impacts on abandoned machinery. Here the sound fell away as if a hand had pressed it down.

The extraction yard opened like a scar. Rails intersected at angles that once guided ore carts; now they guided bodies. Crane ribs rose in black arcs. Pits yawned where machines had been pulled out and never replaced. Wind swept across the metal and carried grit in sheets that erased footprints quickly, as if the planet did not want to keep records.

The records were already there.

Bodies. Astartes. Some in PRIMA colors, some in stripped plates, some with sigils scraped away until the armor looked ashamed of its own history. They lay arranged around a crater lip in a rough semicircle, spaced like markers on a diagram.

Brann stopped behind me. His vox was quiet.

— He did this.

I did not answer. I counted. I traced patterns. I noted how each corpse faced inward toward the crater, as if they had been made to watch.

The corpses were not trophies.

They were instructions.

In the center of the semicircle stood Cassian Vorn.

He waited with the stillness of a man who believes time is his ally. His armor carried cuts that looked deliberate, as if he had crossed out the parts of himself that used to be lawful. His weapon rested in his hands at an angle that suggested comfort, not readiness.

He was not in full war-form. Not yet.

But his mass was wrong in the way I had learned to fear: the hint of a body rewriting itself while the mind stayed present enough to enjoy the process.

His vox opened.

— You came.

No greeting. No bravado. An audit line.

I stepped forward until I stood at the edge of his diagram. The halberd’s shaft felt cold through my gauntlet, its head heavy with a hook geometry designed for ending close-quarters equations quickly.

— I will not let you reduce witness to one voice.

Vorn’s helmet tilted.

— Two voices become one story.

— One voice becomes nothing at all.

I felt Brann shift behind me. I knew his intent without seeing it: he would try to flank, try to place a bolter shot, try to turn this into a gun problem because gun problems have clear solutions.

Vorn spoke again, as if he had overheard Brann’s thought.

— Do not move.

His tone was not command shouted. It was command assumed.

Brann froze anyway.

I watched that, and in the watching I understood the true reach of Vorn’s function. He did not need chains. He did not need vox domination. He needed only the kind of certainty that makes other men hesitate because they cannot locate where the authority is coming from.

— You are not the Legatus.

— No.

— I am the part that makes the Legatus durable.

The wind gusted. Grit hissed across metal. For a moment the whole yard looked like an old pict capture — grainy, shaking, unreal.

Then Vorn stepped forward and the unreality became immediate.

He moved with discipline. Not a berserk rush. Not animal momentum. Clean footwork across uneven ground, choosing angles as if he were inside a training cage rather than standing among corpses arranged like doctrine.

His weapon came up in a short arc meant to break my stance. I met it with the halberd shaft, absorbing impact through my forearms. The force traveled through armor into bone with the blunt honesty of physics.

He smiled behind the grille. I could hear it in the breath.

— Good.

— You regulate.

He wanted me to answer with hatred. Hatred is devotion wearing a different mask.

I answered with procedure.

I shifted my feet. I kept my center. I let the hook geometry do the work it was designed to do: control distance, force decisions, deny the opponent the comfort of clean lines.

Vorn stepped in anyway, closing against the halberd’s advantage as if he had already calculated the price of bruised ribs and chosen to pay it.

His shoulder slammed my chest. His weapon’s haft pinned the halberd shaft for half a second. His other hand reached for my helm — an execution gesture I had seen him use on wavering brothers.

I rotated the halberd, used the hook to catch his forearm, and tore it down and away.

The metal screamed as edge met armor.

Vorn’s breath hitched once. Not pain. Surprise.

— You learned.

— I watched.

He came again. Faster now. Not frantic — just escalating. Each strike aimed to force me into a choice between my own survival and the discipline that kept witness lawful.

Because that was his goal.

Not to kill me first.

To make me abandon regulation. To make me become a beast in my own right, so the archive could later merge categories and call the whole night inevitable.

I refused him.

I gave ground in measured increments. I denied him the flanking angles. I kept my breathing steady.

Behind me, Brann fired once — one shot only — at a shape moving at the edge of the yard. A turned brother, or a beast, or something between. The round snapped it down. Brann did not fire again. He was guarding the perimeter, not trying to win my fight for me.

Vorn heard the shot and laughed softly.

— You still think you can keep two truths separate.

— You still think you can file this honestly.

He drove his weapon into my halberd shaft and forced it down, then stepped on the haft with one boot.

For a heartbeat, I was disarmed.

The halberd was pinned to the ground. Vorn stood over it like a judge over a sentence.

He lifted his weapon for the killing blow.

I did the only honest thing left.

I closed.

I stepped into his range, into the space his weapon required to generate force, and I hit him with my shoulder like a breacher hitting a door.

We crashed together. His strike glanced off my pauldron and carved a shallow groove. I felt heat and then cold as the armor’s seal compensated.

His mass was wrong. He felt heavier than he should have been. Dense in a way that suggested the body had begun to build toward the war-form while the mind stayed intact enough to keep discipline.

The worst kind of opponent.

He tried to headbutt me.

I drove my knee into his abdomen and felt something give that should not have given under ceramite pressure.

He grunted. Not pain. Annoyance.

— You’re late. The conversion is already done.

— No.

— It’s still being enforced.

I reached down and tore the halberd free from under his boot. Metal shrieked. The shaft came up between us like a wall.

Vorn stepped back for the first time.

He did not look afraid.

He looked interested.

He lifted his weapon again and took a different stance — lower, heavier, built for ending the exchange rather than playing it.

I recognized the shift.

This was not duelist posture.

This was butcher posture.

He would try to break my arms. Break my legs. Reduce me to a body that could not carry witness. Leave my helm intact so I could listen while he executed Brann.

The thought tightened my throat.

I did not let it become devotion.

I let it become geometry.

I advanced with the halberd point leading, hook angled for chest entry. I did not swing wide. I did not perform. I moved like a man pushing a spear into a gap because gaps exist whether you want them to or not.

Vorn tried to sidestep.

The ground betrayed him — rail grit under his boot, a fraction of slip.

That fraction was enough.

The halberd head drove into his chest plate at the seam. It punched through with a sound like tearing cloth, except the cloth was metal and the tear was violence made precise.

Vorn’s breath left his body in a single sharp exhale.

His hands clamped around the shaft instinctively, not to pull it out, but to keep me from lifting him.

I lifted anyway.

Not for drama.

For sentence.

I drove my weight into the shaft and levered him off the ground. His boots left the grit. His body hung on the halberd head, impaled through the chest, lifted toward the storm-bruised sky.

For a moment his lenses were level with mine.

There was still mind behind them.

Still calculation.

Still a man present enough to understand what was happening and to hate me for doing it.

He tried to speak.

Blood frothed at the grille.

I held him there long enough for the yard to see.

Not for him.

For the theater.

So the rot would have one less lesson to teach.

Then I threw him down.

His body hit the metal hard enough that the impact echoed across the extraction ribs. The halberd tore free as he fell, leaving a wound that did not close.

He tried to rise.

Not quickly. Not like a beast. Like a man who still believed the ledger might let him write a different outcome.

I stepped forward and brought the halberd head down like an executioner brings a blade down on a block.

The edge met his skull with a finality that did not leave room for myth.

The helmet split. The skull beneath split with it.

The light in his lenses went out.

I stood over him with the halberd dripping and felt nothing like triumph.

Only the cold relief of an equation closed.

Behind me, Brann did not speak. He did not congratulate. He did not perform grief.

He keyed his vox once, private channel.

— Witness remains two.

I looked at Vorn’s corpse and made the choice that would matter later.

No trophy.

No banner ripped free.

No name shouted into the sky.

Just a body that could not stand back up, left in the open where the theater had wanted to teach a different lesson.

— We move.

And we left the yard before the wind could bury the proof.
