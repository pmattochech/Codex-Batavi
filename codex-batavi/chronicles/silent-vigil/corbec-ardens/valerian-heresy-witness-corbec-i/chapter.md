# Valerian Heresy — Corbec witness (I)

I learned the smell of Malakor V before I learned its map.

Oxide. Hot dust. Old coolant pushed through ruptured seals until it turned sweet and wrong, like fruit left too long in a sealed locker. The extraction flats had once been orderly — grids laid out by people who believed time would remain obedient. Now the flats were a sheet of abandoned intention: rail spurs ending in nothing, scaffold towers with their ladders torn away, pits where machines had been dug out and never replaced. Above, the sky carried the Cicatrix like weather. Not a wound you could point at with a gauntlet and say *there*. A pressure. A taste of iron on the tongue when you spoke too long. Green static at the edge of auspex returns. A low tremor in the teeth when the wind shifted across the metal.

We were five, and that count mattered — not because five could hold ground or change a theater, but because five was the smallest unit that could still carry truth without becoming rumor. A single brother can be misread. Two can be dismissed as coincidence. Three becomes a narrative. Five becomes a ledger entry that refuses to vanish.

Rauk Venn. Hadrik Sorn. Kessel Drave. Brann Oeric. And me. Line brothers. Not counselors. Not icon-bearers. Not the kind of men who get sculpted into marble on a world that still wastes stone on comforting fiction. We were *Prima* in the interval where *Prima* stopped being a Vexillatio and started being a stain.

Our brief did not mention the stain. It did not name the man who made it. The brief was thinner than a ration slate: witness. Survive. Keep the line’s truth intact long enough to reach the Castellan.

I did not like the word *witness* at first. It sounded like a priest’s job, or a remembrancer’s — someone safe, sitting behind glass, taking notes while other men died to generate a story. But the Strategium meant it the way it means *rotation* and *replacement*: a process that prevents the war from eating your future. Witness is a kind of ammunition. If you do not carry it, you will run out later when you need it most.

We moved with helmets sealed, because Malakor V was a place where air could lie.

The rot did not start with a howl. That comes later, in stories made by men who need the universe to announce its betrayals properly. It started with an order that arrived already decided. Our vox carried the First Legatus’s language in a clean tone that was almost comforting: a re-route, a consolidation, a small adjustment to the day’s geometry. Nothing that would make an officer’s hand tremble. Nothing that would make a line brother look up and say, *this is wrong.* And yet the order landed in our heads like a door closing behind us.

Rauk was the first to say it out loud, because Rauk had the least patience for subtlety.

— That path is dead.

He did not mean the literal path. He meant the logic that had used to sit behind the words: the logic that belonged to the Chapter, that made an order feel like a tool, not like a leash.

Hadrik checked his own slate anyway, because Hadrik checked everything twice, the way a man checks a seal when the void is one bulkhead away. Kessel watched the horizon where the extraction towers cut black lines against the sky and said nothing. Brann looked at me. I did not answer him with an opinion. I answered him with a procedure.

— No slogans. No speeches. We file and we move.

It was a small sentence, but it held weight: how you keep five men from becoming a mob, discipline from turning into performance.

We moved along a rail trench half-filled with drifting grit. Our boots made the sound of heavy things walking on cheap metal. The wind put static in the vox that came and went like a hand turning a dial.

We saw the first brother at the edge of a pit where a machine had once stood. He was on his knees, hands braced on the rim, as if he had been sick and tried to keep his stomach inside his body. His armor was intact. His posture was wrong. Brann knelt beside him without being told, because Brann had always been the first to close distance when a brother looked like he needed it. He did not speak. He ran a gauntlet across the kneeling Marine’s backplate, checking for the tremor you can feel before you can see it. Kessel watched the sky. Rauk watched the horizon. Hadrik watched the kneeling Marine’s hands. I watched the kneeling Marine’s throat — the throat is where the lie lives when the helmet is on.

The kneeling Marine’s vox was open. Not transmitting. Just open, like someone had pressed the switch and forgotten why. I keyed my own vox.

— Brother. Identify. Report.

He did not respond. His hands flexed once on the metal. His shoulders rose as he drew breath. The breath came out as a hiss that was not mechanical: an animal trying to remember language and failing.

I did not use the labels Drusus uses. Not then. Not in that year. We did not have the sealed words in our mouths yet. We had only the field reality: some brothers broke under pressure and became something else, and sometimes the break came with a delay you could not afford.

Hadrik tried the officer’s lie first.

— He’s stable — the way an officer says *the wall will hold*, a sentence meant to prevent panic.

— He’s not.

His bolter was down, but his finger had moved to the trigger guard without him thinking.

Brann lifted the kneeling Marine’s chin with two fingers of his gauntlet. It was a gesture you would have called gentle if our hands were not made of ceramite and steel. The kneeling Marine’s helmet lenses were yellowed at the edges — not the glow of a targeting array, not the orange wash of an industrial lens, but a stain from within, as if something had been burning behind the glass. Brann froze. Not from fear. From calculation. His body stopped because he needed one second to decide whether the brother in front of him was still a brother.

The brother moved first. He surged up with a speed that did not belong to his mass. His hands went for Brann’s throat. His vox spat one word in a voice that was too clear for the body that carried it.

— Liberation.

Rauk shot him in the chest twice. The rounds punched in, detonated, and blew out the backplate in a spray of torn metal and vapor. The Marine staggered, but did not fall. He looked at Rauk as if Rauk had just proven a point. Kessel fired next, measured and cruelly precise. He took the helmet lens. The left one first. Then the right. The brother fell — not like a man falling, like a machine collapsing when its support strut is cut.

Silence followed, and in that silence I heard the smallest sound: a thin, involuntary exhale from Brann, as if his body had been holding breath since before the fight began. Hadrik knelt by the body and began the boring work. Identity. Plate marks. Squad rune. Anything that could be carried forward into an archive that would later try to deny what happened.

I watched the corpse’s hands. They were still flexing — not in a seizure, in a rhythm, like a man counting. That was the moment I understood the rot was not only a sickness. It was a method.

Rauk spat the word he always spat when he wanted to make a complicated thing simple.

— Traitor.

I shook my head once. Not because I disagreed with the moral. Because I refused the comfort of a label that would let us stop thinking.

— Not yet. Not enough data.

Rauk’s head turned toward me. The red smear on his pauldron made his silhouette look like a banner, and I hated it for that. I did not want him to be a symbol. I wanted him to be a brother who could survive.

— He tried to kill Brann.

— I saw. File it. Do not decorate it — I said it flat as a slate.

Hadrik looked up from his work.

— Orders?

I checked the slate. The same route order still glowed there, the same “consolidation” instruction, the same gentle language that pretended we were still operating inside a lawful map.

— We move.

Kessel’s voice came through the squad channel, low and flat.

— Toward what?

Fair question. We were not marching toward a victory. We were marching toward contact, toward the thing that had already begun rewriting our corridor logic. I answered with the only thing that had kept the Chapter alive through the crucible: regulation of self.

— Service demands reason.

I did not say the rest of the phrase. Not because I was afraid of poetry. Because I knew what would happen if I let it become a chant too early. A chant makes men feel like they belong to something larger than their fear. That is useful. It is also how you stop noticing when the larger thing has turned against you.

We crossed the flats at a measured pace. The wind pushed grit against our greaves. The sky flickered. Somewhere far off, a tower collapsed with a sound like a cathedral breathing out its last air.

We found the second sign in the open, because Malakor V was not interested in subtlety once it had begun. It was a line of bodies — not civilians, not mortal auxiliaries. Astartes. They had been arranged. Not by scavengers. Not by random explosion. Arranged the way a surgeon arranges instruments before an operation: purposeful, ordered, meant to communicate something without words.

Rauk slowed first. Hadrik slowed with him. Kessel stopped completely. Brann’s stride faltered once, then steadied, as if he had stepped onto ice and corrected. I counted. Five. No — more than five. The first five were the closest, lying in a neat arc around a crater’s rim. Behind them, more, partially buried by grit, as if the wind itself had tried to file them away and failed. Helms were split. Plates were opened. Chest cavities were breached with a violence that looked almost practiced.

Kessel’s voice was a blade.

— Who did this?

Hadrik’s vox carried the smallest tremor.

— It’s *our* work.

And I heard what he meant: the cuts looked like Batavi geometry, but done without restraint.

Rauk stepped toward the nearest corpse, then stopped when he saw the symbol etched into the broken armor. Not a sigil painted. An etch. A mark made with a blade. He did not speak it. He did not need to. We all knew the shape of the claim, even if we refused to say the title aloud.

Brann’s gauntlet tightened on his bolter.

— This is a message.

I nodded.

— Yes.

Kessel asked the next question without warmth.

— For whom?

I did not look at the bodies when I answered. I looked at the horizon, where the extraction flats rose into broken scaffolding and the wind carried ash like snow.

— For the ones who still think refusal is possible. For us.

Rauk spat.

— Let him come, then.

I almost told him to shut up. Not because anger was wrong. Because anger was loud, and loudness is how you let the enemy set your rhythm. Instead I did something that felt like an insult and was actually mercy: I gave them a job.

— Rauk — overwatch. Hadrik, tags and identifiers. Kessel, scan for movement. Brann—

I began, and did not finish the word before Brann moved.

Brann cut in before I could finish his name.

— I know.

He was already doing the work without waiting to be told: checking the periphery, watching the wind, listening for the sound that does not belong.

And then the sound arrived. Not a howl. Not a roar. Footsteps — heavy, deliberate, too slow for a man running and too confident for a man stalking. Kessel’s auspex pinged once, then filled with static. Rauk’s bolter rose. Hadrik stopped his tagging mid-motion, his hand frozen above a fallen pauldron as if he had been caught stealing. I lifted my own weapon and felt, with a clarity that made my mouth dry, that I did not want to meet whatever was walking toward us with a bolter. Bolters end things at range. Bolters also allow myths. If you kill a monster from far away, you can pretend it was never close enough to touch you. We needed something else. We needed sentence.

I stepped back toward the crater lip where the bodies lay. Half-buried by grit, wrapped in the wind’s attempt to erase them, was a rack-case — *Prima* pattern, officer issue, stamped with a serial that meant nothing to anyone outside our own inventories. I knew the case. I had filed its checks. I had watched it move from ship to ship, from theater to theater, because *Prima* keeps certain tools close even when it pretends it does not believe in symbols. I released the clasps and drew out the halberd. Not the vexillum. Not the relay. Just steel and reach and hook geometry, honest in its function. The shaft was scarred from use. The head had been sharpened recently, the edge bright in a way that looked obscene against the dust.

Rauk’s vox snapped my name half-spoken.

— Corbec—

I cut him off.

— No slogans — this time a command, not a habit.

— Hold your arcs.

Footsteps continued.

Hadrik whispered:

— Who is it?

I did not answer with a name. Names create stories. We did not have the luxury of story. I answered with the truth we could already file.

— There is a First Sword behind this. And he is close.

The figure crested the rise of the flats beyond the crater. At first glance, he looked like one of us. That was the point — armor, mass, Astartes silhouette, the shape the Imperium expects to see when it looks for salvation. Then the light struck him and the illusion broke. His armor was marked with cuts that were not battle damage: cuts made deliberately, as if someone had taken a blade to the plate the way an admin clerk takes a stylus to paper, to cross out what used to be true. His helmet lenses were bright — not yellow-stained like the brother we had shot, but bright with a clean, cold glow that suggested a mind still present behind them. He carried no banner. He did not need one. The bodies at our feet were his banner.

Kessel’s whisper was still sharp enough to draw blood.

— That is him.

I felt the halberd’s weight settle into my hands like a decision.

The figure stopped at a distance that was polite, a duelist’s distance: close enough to promise violence, far enough to offer the lie of choice. His vox opened. The voice that came out was steady.

— You kept five.

No greeting. No taunt. An audit statement.

Rauk aimed at his head.

— Name yourself.

The figure did not answer him. His helmet turned, slowly, until his lenses faced me — not Rauk, not Kessel, not Brann. Me. He had measured the halberd. He had measured my stance. He had decided I was the only variable that mattered.

— You are carrying a tool. Good.

Brann’s vox went tight on my name.

— Corbec—

— Hold.

I did not know his name yet. That comes later, when the archive is brave enough to file it and angry enough to expunge it. But I knew his function, and function is more important than name. He was the one who made doubt expensive. He was the one who took wavering brothers and turned their wavering into corpses arranged like a lesson. He was the blade that made the new faith enforceable.

I kept my voice level.

— You are not the Legatus.

The figure tilted his head a fraction, as if I had said something interesting.

— No. I am the part that makes the Legatus possible.

The words landed with a weight that made my helm feel smaller.

I did not answer with anger. I answered with procedure, because procedure is the only language the warp cannot easily mimic.

— State your intent.

He laughed once, softly, a sound that did not match the dead around us.

— Intent? To finish a conversion — flat as an audit’s tone.

He lifted his weapon. Not a halberd. Not a sword. Something heavier, cruder, built for ending arguments rather than winning duels.

Behind me, Hadrik whispered a prayer he would later deny saying. Rauk shifted his stance to brace for recoil. Kessel’s finger tightened on the trigger in the measured way that said he was about to do something irreversible. Brann moved one step to my left, because Brann had decided that if I fell, he would pick up the halberd. Five became a formation without being told.

And in the first breath before the fight, I finally allowed myself the rest of the vow — not as a chant, not as comfort, but as regulation: reason directs duty without blind submission.

The figure took one step forward.

I stepped to meet him.
