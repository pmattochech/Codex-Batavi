# Run to the Wall (Corbec’s flight)

After Vorn died, the theater did not pause.

The extraction flats did not quiet themselves in respect. The Cicatrix pressure did not ease because a butcher had been sentenced. Wind still pushed grit across metal. Static still laced the vox. Somewhere beyond the crane ribs, devotion still moved like a tide.

Brann and I withdrew because withdrawal is not retreat when the objective is witness.

Our objective had narrowed into something brutally simple.

Reach Alaric’s net. Deliver truth that can be used. Do it before rumor becomes doctrine.

We moved along dead relay paths where towers had been snapped or stripped. The old infrastructure of Malakor V had been designed for industry: routes that assumed predictable traffic, predictable weight, predictable purpose. Now the routes were traps because the enemy knew them too.

We stopped trusting the map.

We trusted only friction.

If a corridor looked too clean, we avoided it. If a relay node was silent in a way that felt deliberate, we treated silence as bait. If a shelter had intact doors, we assumed someone wanted us to use them.

Brann’s breathing came hard through the squad channel. He tried to keep it out of my hearing. He failed. The duel had not touched him, but the theater had. Something had bitten him earlier on the line — deep enough that the seal kept compensating, shallow enough that he could pretend it was not a problem.

Pretending is devotion’s cousin.

I did not allow it.

— Report.

Brann’s pause was too long.

— Later.

— Now.

I repeated.

He exhaled once, then gave me the facts the way a line brother gives a munitions count: reluctantly, precisely, without emotion performance.

— Left side. Hip and ribs.

— Movement penalty. Not catastrophic.

I filed it in my head and adjusted our path to reduce climbs. That was the only mercy available: geometry that did not demand more from his body than it could give.

We crossed a stretch of open ground where the rails were buried under grit. On the far side, a scaffold skeleton offered cover. I signaled Brann to move first, because if something shot at us I wanted the first rounds to hit me, not him.

He did not argue. Brann had learned what Hadrik never got the chance to: in Malakor’s theater, arguing costs time you do not own.

He moved.

Halfway across, a voice hit our vox.

Not a direct command. Not a broadcast. A low transmission tucked into the edge of the channel, as if it had been waiting for a specific moment of exposure.

— Brothers. Hold. You are safe.

The transmission said it, warm with false familiarity.

Brann’s stride faltered.

Not because he believed it.

Because part of any Marine wants to believe there is still a safe word that can be spoken into the air and make the world obey.

I cut the channel.

Not gently. Hard mute. Hard shut.

Brann looked at me.

— That could have been real.

— If it were real.

— It would not need to be seductive.

We reached the scaffold and crouched under its ribs. The metal groaned above us as wind moved through it. Somewhere nearby, boots crunched grit.

I held my breath and listened.

The steps were not heavy enough for Astartes mass.

Mortals.

That was worse, in some ways. Mortals can be used as bait with less cost. Mortals can be made into witnesses who will tell any story you want if you scare them enough.

We waited until the steps passed.

When we moved again, we took the long route: through an extraction pit where the walls were lined with old ladder rungs, up through a collapsed conveyor tunnel, across a platform whose floor plates had been peeled back like skin.

Every piece of the route was ugly.

Ugly routes are honest. They cost too much to use casually. They are less likely to be prepared as traps.

At one relay stub, we found a slate nailed to a post.

No seal. No signature. Just a single line scratched into metal.

*The First holds you.*

Brann stared at it as if the words had weight.

I did not let them have weight.

I ripped the slate free and threw it into the pit.

— No slogans.

Brann’s voice was tight.

— We keep saying that.

— Because it keeps being necessary.

We reached a dead vox node and used it as cover to check our bearings. The containment perimeter was not visible, but I could feel it in the theater: the subtle difference between chaos that is feeding itself and chaos that is being constrained by a mind that refuses to enjoy it.

Alaric’s geometry has a taste.

You feel it in the way threats stop spreading freely. In the way paths begin to close. In the way the wind seems less like a voice and more like weather again.

We ran the last stretch under fire.

Not a coordinated volley. Not a Vexillatio response. Sporadic shots from behind ribs and pits, as if the theater were still trying to reduce witness to one voice before it was too late.

Brann stumbled once. His left leg failed for half a second and he caught himself on the halberd shaft I shoved back toward him.

— Hold.

He bared his teeth behind the grille.

— I am holding.

We reached the perimeter as the hook was closing.

You could feel the change without seeing a single Marine. The air itself seemed to become more regulated. Vox traffic sharpened into disciplined bursts. Movement in the distance stopped being random and started being denied.

A voice came through the channel — cold, clear, command without theatrics.

— Identify.

The channel demanded.

I gave our codes. I gave our names. I gave the one sentence that mattered most.

— Witness remains two.

There was a pause. Not uncertainty. Calculation.

Then the channel opened again, flat as a wall:

— Advance to point three. Weapons high. Do not stop for bodies. Drusus will file what you cannot.

That was Alaric’s mercy.

Not softness.

A route forward that did not allow us to drown in shame.

Brann’s breath came ragged as we moved into the perimeter.

I keyed private channel.

— You will survive.

Brann’s laugh was a single bitter sound.

— That’s not an order you can give.

— It’s not an order.

— It’s a filing.

He did not answer.

But he kept walking, and that was enough.
