# The sentence seen from below (Corbec as witness)

I did not see the duel.

That sentence is important because the Chapter will try to turn Malakor V into a single story if you let it. The human mind loves duels. Duels are clean. Two men, one outcome, a moral you can carve into stone.

Malakor was not clean.

Malakor was a theater where three hundred names died before the sentence was even possible.

When Brann and I reached the perimeter, they did not put us at the center of anything. They did not escort us into a command chamber so we could watch history happen. They treated us like what we were: two survivors carrying a fragile kind of ammunition.

They placed us where witness would be useful.

Close enough to confirm that the hook had closed. Far enough that I could not mistake my own fear for participation.

We waited behind a derelict rail frame, helmets sealed, weapons ready, while the perimeter’s geometry tightened. Vox traffic came in short, disciplined bursts. No speeches. No rallying cries. Orders without performance.

It was the only way to fight a rot that fed on devotion.

Brann’s breathing grated through the channel. His pain was no longer a secret. His left side kept failing in small increments, and every time it failed he forced it back into obedience through sheer will, the way a Marine forces a jammed mechanism to cycle because the alternative is unacceptable.

Brann whispered:

— Is he here?

He did not say the name.

I answered the only honest way.

— I don’t know.

That was chronological law: you do not pretend you have data you do not have. Pretending is how rot becomes doctrine.

Minutes passed. The sky flickered. Gunfire rose and fell like breath. Once, a sound rolled across the flats — deep, heavy, not a detonation. A kind of impact that suggested mass meeting mass in confined space.

Brann stiffened.

— That.

— Maybe.

We were not allowed to become narrators. We were allowed to be witnesses.

Then the vox went quiet in a way that did not feel like failure.

It felt like a decision.

For four seconds, the theater held its breath.

Then a voice keyed in — flat, calm, the sound of a wall speaking.

— Sector clear.

One second of pause.

— Biometric data collection authorized. Drusus’s team may advance.

The Castellan’s vox, unadorned.

No triumph.

No vow.

Only the next procedure.

Brann exhaled once, and in that exhale I heard what he did not say: *it is done.*

We were not ordered forward. We were ordered to hold. To keep perimeter discipline while others moved to collect the bodies, the numbers, the telemetry that would later become sealed doctrine.

That was the first lesson of the sentence: even when something ends, the Chapter does not allow itself the luxury of stopping.

Later, when the perimeter rotated and we were moved closer, I saw the aftermath from a distance.

Not a heroic tableau.

An extraction corridor mouth lit by firelight, bodies dragged into lines for cataloging, the stink of coolant and blood mingling with dust. Drusus’s teams moved like surgeons. Their hands did not shake. Their slates did not hesitate.

I did not see the face of the traitor. I did not need to.

I saw what the sentence did to the air.

Silence became heavier, not lighter.

Men who had been shouting earlier stopped shouting. Not because they were reassured. Because reassurance was a kind of devotion, and devotion was the rot’s favored food.

In the hours that followed, Strategium officers began doing something that looked mundane and was actually violence.

They rewrote maps.

Not the big maps with stars. The small maps: route overlays, anchor declarations, where *Prima* was "seated" on paper, which nodes were treated as homes, which nodes were treated as wounds you do not build shrines around.

They refused to let traitor geography remain a home.

I watched a staff officer strike out a coordinate and replace it with another, and the motion looked like execution done with a stylus.

Brann sat beside me, his back against a rail frame. He was still upright, but his posture had changed. He held himself as if his body were a machine with one too many cracked parts, kept running by refusal alone.

Brann said:

— They’ll put it on the forge world.

He meant: they will re-seat *Prima* away from the rift mouth. They will make paper do what paper can do: declare a severance even if the blood remembers.

I nodded.

— Good.

Brann’s laughter was small and bitter.

— We did all this so they could move ink.

— We did all this so they could move truth.

— Ink is how truth survives people.

He did not answer.

When Drusus’s medicae came for him, I watched their faces — sealed helms, no expression — and I could still read what mattered in the economy of movement. They handled him with care that did not pretend to be tenderness. They had already begun filing him into a future: repair attempts, degradation curves, the long slope toward a Castraferrum sarcophagus if the body refused to remain useful.

Brann looked at me as they lifted him.

— Two.

— Two.

That was all we had left of the five: a number and a duty.

In the days after, Malakor V became a paragraph in the Chapter’s records with an ending line that refused adornment. The sentence was delivered. The traitor was expunged. The theater’s blood-price was filed.

And beneath those clean lines lived the mess we were not allowed to make public.

The dead names.

The tags Hadrik had placed with hands that died still trying to keep categories separate.

Rauk carrying Hadrik until both were gone.

Kessel dying without letting his shots become rage.

Vorn lifted on a halberd and then made irrecoverably silent.

These were not songs. They were not myths.

They were data that had to be carried by someone still alive.

That someone was me.
