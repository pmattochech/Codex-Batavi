## I. THE LEDGER THAT BIT

Hive Dominus did not smell like war. It smelled like **paper** — recycled fiber, cheap adhesive, incense dragged through vent scrubbers until holiness and bureaucracy shared the same throat. The Ministry of Tithe Lex occupied the upper stack like a second spine inside the hive’s own: corridors wide enough for processions of clerks, lifts rated for pallets of sealed decrees, windows that faced inward on courtyards where statues of faceless tithe saints raised empty hands to a sky nobody could see.

The guild called itself a **confraternity**. In the lower markets, people still called them draymen who had learned to carry bolters.

When they seized the Ministry spires, they did not merely barricade doors. They wired **demo charges** to **load-bearing** voids marked in **red** on Administratum plans — plans they should not have possessed — and they read the names of those voids aloud over stolen *vox* as if prayer could make structural engineering negotiable. Behind the voids: chapels on **Schedule K**. Tithe schools on **Schedule K**. A Mechanicus liaison suite under treaty seal — also **Schedule K**, because the Imperium had promised certain walls would not be paid for in mass-reactive coin.

The first broadcasts from the guild sounded like revolution. The second sounded like **arithmetic**: *We will drop what you will not shoot through.*

Castra-Vetera’s Arbites requested Astartes support with language scrubbed clean of panic. The *Strategium* answered with a single name on the dispatch slate — not a sermon, not a threat, a **governor** who could speak **Lex** in the same breath as ballistics.

**Henric Kessler** took the stack with **II** *Secunda* as if accepting a filing cabinet the size of a world.

## II. THE MEASURED LINE

The mistake the confraternity expected was **Astartes impatience**.

They had seen hololiths of other Chapters — not Batavi, never quite Batavi — erasing hab-blocks to kill one cult sigil painted on a roof. They had built their trap on the assumption that grey ceramite would treat Schedule K as **suggestion** once blood ran hot enough.

What met them instead was **silence** in the stairwells.

Not absence. **Discipline**.

Stalker-pattern muzzles appeared at angles that admitted **one** corridor width and denied spray. Grav-guns locked elevator shafts with a rhythm that felt almost agricultural — *deny*, *deny*, *deny* — so no pallet of explosives could rise unchecked, so no “medical crate” could ascend without leaving a grav signature that lied. Suppression cells moved in **interlocking** geometry, each brother’s field of fire ending where another’s began, the overlap calculated so a ricochet could not wander into a **sanctae** facade even if the Emperor turned His face away.

Kessler’s voice on the open channel was dry as statute ink.

— **Packet Alpha:** east gallery, second mezzanine. Two shooters, partial cover behind fluted column. **No** mass-reactive on the column face — Schedule K adjacency. Semi-auto only. **Assigned targets:** eyes, hands, weapons. *Hold* until my mark.

Somewhere in the Ministry’s gut, a guild sniper learned that magnified optics worked both ways.

## III. THE SHAFT

Sergeant **Maron** — the chronicle names him because his grav-gun team wrote the doctrine addendum later — spent six hours watching a lift well that stank of ozone and fear-sweat. The enemy tried **three** times.

First: a pallet of sandbags — harmless, a probe. Grav caught it mid-shaft and held it until auspex cleared.

Second: a corpse in worker’s blues, rigged so movement would trip a scatter charge. They lowered it on cables from above; Maron’s gunner **pinned** the cable spool through a maintenance slot without touching the body. Explosives ordnance disposed by Ruin in **vacuum** configuration — one melta kiss, one breath, no echo down the K-list walls.

Third: a **child’s** doll on a float-board, a grotesque joke and a test of nerve.

Kessler’s order did not rise.

— **Stop the board. No fire on the doll unless auspex reads metal composite.** Silence Cohort liaison on channel two — pattern sweep. I want to know if this is **distraction** or **payload**.

It was distraction. The real package tried the **adjacent** shaft seventeen minutes later. Maron’s team **crushed** it against the guide-rail until the detonator’s power cell cracked wetly and died.

The Legatus logged nothing congratulatory. He logged **time**.

## IV. KAELEN’S NINE HOURS

Brother **Kaelen** worked a gantry no wider than his shoulders, a maintenance spine that ran behind stained plascrete the color of old wine. Through magnified glass he watched a **single** chamber: a side chapel listed on Schedule K, its rose window facing an interior courtyard the guild had turned into a firing pit.

The man behind the plascrete was not a thinker. He was a **thumb** on a dead-man switch.

Kaelen did not hate him. Hatred was a luxury *Secunda* trained out of stalker lanes; hatred made you **pull** early. Instead Kaelen counted heartbeats the way other men counted ammunition — not because the *Codex* demanded poetry, because the *Armourium* had proved that cardiac rhythm telegraphed decision before the brain finished its lie.

Nine hours.

When the thumb twitched — cleared the guard by **three millimeters** — Kaelen’s round took it. The harness. The idea that geometry belonged to the guild.

The chapel window **shivered**. It did not break. Somewhere below, a child who should not have been in a war zone looked up at the sound and thought thunder had learned to be **small**.

The Ministry later engraved Kaelen’s tally name on a plaque no one visits. Kessler filed a **dissent seal** in closed minutes arguing the plaque was **political** theater; *Strategium* let it stand. Politics, in Castra-Vetera, was sometimes a wall that stayed standing.

---

## V. THE FORTY-FIRST HOUR

Not every story in the stack ended with a clean receipt.

Twelve hostages died **before** *Secunda* could cross their thresholds — executed as leverage that failed, bodies left where cameras would find them, a lesson written in meat for the next negotiator who never arrived. Kessler walked one of those rooms in person, helm on, *vox* off, hands empty at his sides. Witnesses among the scribes swore later the Legatus **looked** at each corpse the way an Apothecary looked at a failed graft — not grief, **audit**.

Forty-seven lived.

Six civilians died in crossfire the Ministry would argue over for years — **enemy** fire during extraction, the Chapter’s own logs insisted, and Kessler would not sign a line that blamed a guild rifle if auspex could not **prove** it. *Secunda* did not outsource guilt to footnotes; it carried what it earned.

Twenty-three battle-brothers went down — shrapnel, partial structural collapse, one grav-well mishap that shattered a knee and did not excuse the gunner from debrief. Twelve returned to full line duty within ninety days. The rest carried scars into the next compliance war like **Lex** carried amendments.

Ruin never opened a vault door with theatrical contempt. The enemy had no Astartes-grade vault worth the **spectacle**. Kessler kept Ruin in reserve like a tool on a rack — unused, **present** — and the confraternity’s last leaders died understanding they had been out-waited by men who could have been **louder** and chose not to be.

---

## VI. SURVEY ZERO

When the last chamber opened, the Mechanicus liaison emerged on legs that shook under red robes — not, she would testify, because the guild had broken her faith in flesh, but because she had **seen** grey plate move through killing geometry with the patience of a machine counting rivets.

The *Armourium* survey took three days. Every Schedule K facade. Every hairline crack argued over with lamps and calipers.

**Zero** breaches attributable to Chapter weapons.

Kessler received the seal in a room that smelled like paper and ozone, same as the stack, as if the hive had followed him downstairs to **watch** him sign. He said nothing for public record. In the *Strategium* annex, he repeated his field quote for the minutes — not pride, **reminder**:

*A stray bolt is a lawsuit against the Emperor. Fire as if the Arbites were behind you — they often are.*

---

## VII. THE CLOTH

Years later, when *Armourium* stenciled the **crosshair over sealed arch** on the Second’s *Alabarda*, neophytes learned a truth the Administratum preferred softened: **II** *Secunda*’s greatest certified triumph was not a body count. It was **negative space** — the wall that did not fall, the chapel that did not become powder, the proof that the Chapter could be **lethal** without being **lazy**.

The Emperor dictates.

On Hive Dominus, **Secunda** complied — one round at a time.
