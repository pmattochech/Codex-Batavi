# The Crucible Book I — *Bait Doctrine*

**Foundation War** · **Chapter 5 of 38** · *Bait Doctrine*  
**Strategium anchor:** CV y3  
**Theater:** Pole I — the Moon Hunt  
*Tactical intent: force **Type A** (display/honor-kinetic) engagement; deny Type B pack geometry by isolation of a single stalker. Moral hazard: **accepted** (Castellan’s seal).*

---

They burned **Certo**’s name off the vox-net and left his mistakes on the shingle—war’s cruel romance: the dead teach; the living **pay**.

Kadmos laid the Glass Rills on hololith until the terrain looked like a wound stitched with water.

“If this is sin,” he said privately to Alaric, “let it be **ours** to carry.”

“It already is,” Alaric answered. Helm lamps off; presence nonetheless filled the tent like pressure.

---

Alaric inverted the hunter’s love of worth.

Addressing the Strategium—not oratory, *order*—he spoke the doctrine that would later collect footnotes and shame in equal measure:

“If it wants **worthy** prey,” he said, “we will offer it *shameful* prey—guided by those who know **shame’s geography**.”

Silence in the room learned new shapes.

Drusus: “Mortal casualty probability—”

“Listed,” Alaric said. “Not debated. Volunteers only. No pressed hands.”

Varro inclined his skull. “Bait is bait. **Coercion** is already a second mouth.”

The Needles came anyway—**five**, then **seven**—with Iria at their forward edge, hair hacked short for a rite older than the Aquila, shell-cloak traded for wading leathers that would not sing when wet. Bells—tiny, rude, tuned to annoy honest silence—were wired to spear-hafts with twine and spite.

They would walk the **Glass Rills**: channels where quartz grit and tide turned shallows into **lenses**, where each step could flash like accusation under the wrong sky.

“Auspex dulled,” Kadmos ordered. “If we look electric, we look **fed**.”

Marines sank into positions that violated *Codex* aesthetics of deterrence: not proud firing steps, not visible knotwork, not banners of intimidation—only **mud silence**, helmets dulled with soot and algae paste, bolters cradled like things ashamed of their appetite.

The romance of ambush is not sneak-thief glee. It is **discipline pretending to be absence**.

Iria walked the rills like a woman rehearsing clumsiness she did not own. Her bells spoke in petty insults to the wind.

Kessler—off duty by order, on the cordon by choice—watched with mechanical clenched jaw.

“If she dies,” Kessler muttered to Varro’s passing shadow, “I’ll lose a war *inside myself*.”

Varro paused. “Then keep it quiet,” he said. “Noise is what sells her.”

---

The stalker came as **hunters** do when the world is correct: alone, patient, **heat** shadow stitched thin across mimic-cloak mesh, the faintest suggestion of mandible curvature at the mask-edge—Administratum would later hunt a word; Drusus hunted **vectors**.

It did not rush.

It **appraised**—the sin Iria needed.

When she pretended to stumble—an insult to her true grace—it **committed**.

Alaric rose.

Not as rescuer. Not as knight.

As **sentence** delivered without courtroom.

What followed was no tournament. No duel story told to neophytes to make them brave. It was **butchery consecrated by necessity**—the chimeric seed answering violence with a violence the creature had *invited* as sacrament: a language older than vows, newer than shame.

For a heartbeat the stalker’s eyes—behind biomatrix—met Alaric’s lenses as if searching **kin** and finding *law* instead.

Then law **fell**.

Blood hissed on quartz brine. Rain tried to scrub the scene moral and failed.

The kill was clean enough to file.

Dirty enough to dream.

Drusus was already moving, voice stripped to procedure: “Containment perimeter **now**. Skitarii **denied** approach until biological hazard sweep completes.”

---

When the hunter fell, **Threx** descended anyway—*Lex Ignis* arrogance in red chrome, Skitarii stepping in that uncanny lockstep that mimics piety.

“Salvage primacy per Treaty Appendix Sigma,” Threx intoned, optics drinking the corpse. “Biologics of strategic—”

“**Denied**,” Alaric said.

No louder than weather. No softer than law.

Threx’s vox fuzzed indignation. “Castellan—**compliance**—”

“The Chapter owns the dead that **hunted** us,” Alaric replied. “Your ledger owns *risk deferred*. Pick one.”

Threx’s smile was augmetal—**stored** for later knives.

“Protest logged,” he said. “Opportunity does not expire. It **compounds**.”

Drusus knelt and sealed tissue in null-fields with hands that did not tremble *yet*—a detail his own Apothecarion would mark with unpleasant precision years later.

Varro prayed **silence** over the corpse: not Ministorum theater, not lyrical benediction—**binding** against listening that wore no ears. The Genetor mistook it for superstition.

Drusus did not.

Iria stood in the rills with bell-spear lowered, breath coming in human kicks she hated for being human.

Alaric turned his helm to her once.

He did not praise.

Praise would have been *instrumentalism* dressed as kindness.

“You walked shame’s map,” he said. “We will not ask it of you again **lightly**.”

Iria swallowed. “You will ask,” she answered. “Because mud always asks.”

---

Later, Threx’s complaint would ride cog-channels to orbit—lines of ink that could bruise treaties.

For that hour, rain cooling hunter blood on quartz, the Triumvirate simply **stood** within the fact of what they had done.

Kadmos exhaled, almost reverent despite himself.

“So,” he said, too quiet for any but them. “**Sovereignty** tastes like iron.”

Varro: “It tastes like **what we owe**.”

Drusus: “Both.”

Somewhere beyond the Rills, Type B cadence stirred—pack-soldiers answering noise with appetite not yet fed.

The crucible had won a lesson.

Lessons, in romance and in war, do not arrive without tuition due downstream.
