# Valerian Heresy — rot spread (theater geometry)

Malakor V did not feel like a corridor once you stopped believing in maps.

The first time I heard the word *theater* used for it, I resented the softness. *Theater* is where mortals perform bravery for an audience. *Theater* is where you pretend you can see the whole stage.

But the word held because it was not soft. It was accurate.

Corridors imply walls. Walls imply that the enemy will arrive from a direction you can mark. Malakor V was open extraction ground — flats, pits, scaffold ribs, derelict rail spurs, the skeleton of industry built for a world that still believed it had a future.

And above it all: the Cicatrix pressure, like a hand on the back of the skull.

After our first contact with the butcher-logic, we tried to return to unit thinking because unit thinking is how you keep war honest. Five brothers. One formation. One purpose. A truth you can carry.

The theater refused.

Orders came like weather. Sometimes from channels we had never used. Sometimes stamped with the right codes but written in a syntax that was almost correct — the same phrases, rearranged so the meaning changed while the mouth still recognized the taste.

Rauk kept calling them *lies*. Hadrik called them *corruptions*. Kessel called them *noise*.

I called them what they were.

— Weaponized command.

Brann looked at me once, and I could tell he wanted to argue. Not because he disagreed. Because naming something that cleanly makes you responsible for surviving it.

We moved anyway.

The extraction flats were not empty. They were populated by the same thing all war eventually creates: men who are still alive and do not know why. Some wore *Prima* colors. Some wore other vexils rotated into this mouth. Some had stripped markings entirely. In the distance, we saw groups moving with too much cohesion for civilians and too much hunger for disciplined Astartes.

It would have been easier if the rot had drawn a clean border.

It did not.

The first "bulk" we met came in a half-squad line, helmets sealed, bolters up, posture correct. They moved like brothers on patrol. They spoke like brothers on patrol. They used our own call-and-response. For ten seconds, my mind tried to accept the comfort.

Then I heard the devotion in the tone.

Not faith. Devotion. The thing that sounds like certainty because it refuses to examine itself.

Rauk recognized it too and did what Rauk does when something feels wrong: he tried to force it into the shape of a target.

— Identify your sergeant.

Rauk barked across the open ground.

The half-squad stopped. One Marine stepped forward. His armor was familiar. I could have named his forge batch. I could have told you the scratch on his right greave was from the Incus rail.

He raised his chin as if answering an honor guard roll call.

— We are the First.

It was not an identifier. It was a slogan wearing an identifier’s clothing.

Kessel’s aim tightened. He did not fire. He waited for me, because we were still five and five is a ledger you do not break casually.

Hadrik whispered:

— Corbec.

I could hear the question. Are they turned? Are they bait? Are they still brothers?

I answered with the only thing that mattered: what we needed to *file*.

— Ask them to describe the order chain.

Hadrik did it. Calmly. Professionally. A routine verification request.

The Marine in front did not answer. His helmet tilted as if the question were beneath him.

Then his vox opened again, and for a moment I heard another voice under his — faint, layered, like a choir you cannot locate in a room.

— No chain. Only the Legatus.

Brann exhaled once, hard.

— That is not how we speak.

The half-squad moved again.

They advanced in a line that was almost perfect. Almost.

The mistake was in the spacing. Too tight for discipline, too loose for assault. The spacing of men who have stopped thinking of each other as brothers and started thinking of each other as mass.

Rauk fired first because Rauk understood the part of war that precedes philosophy. His bolter barked and took the lead Marine in the chest. The Marine stumbled but did not fall. Behind him, the line did not adjust correctly. They surged forward together, as if pulled by a single rope.

I saw then what made them so dangerous.

Not that they were fearless.

That they were relieved.

Relieved to stop deciding. Relieved to stop regulating. Relieved to let devotion carry them where reason had demanded too much for too long.

— Reason directs duty.

I said, and the phrase felt wrong in my mouth because it did not change what was happening.

Hadrik shot to disable knees. Kessel shot to disable lenses. Brann shot to cut the flank and prevent encirclement. Rauk kept putting rounds into center mass until his bolter clicked empty and the line finally broke.

When the last of them fell, the flats went quiet again.

And then, as if the theater itself had been listening, another line rose from behind a scaffold rib.

Not Marines this time.

Versibar.

Not the full war-form you remember from later years’ sealed files. Not yet. But close enough that my instincts screamed the same warning: *do not let it close.*

The worst part was not the bodies.

The worst part was the timing.

It was too precise to be coincidence.

We had fired for less than a minute. We had exposed our positions. We had done the thing every predator waits for: we had made noise.

Now the beasts arrived, not wandering, not random, but on a vector that made sense.

Kessel’s voice went tight.

— They’re being used.

He did not mean “used” the way a commander uses a squad. He meant used the way you use a fire: you aim it and let it consume.

I watched the Versibar line and forced my mind to do a colder kind of work.

At range, a brother in devotion and a beast in partial war-form both look like the same problem: something you must stop before it reaches you.

But the archive cannot treat them as identical.

If you merge them, you build a comforting lie: that it was all biology. That treason was just a stage. That a brother was not responsible once the body changed.

Malakor V was teaching us the opposite. The body was only one weapon in a rack of weapons.

Voice. Fear. Availability of violence. Hunger for relief. The sudden permission to stop being disciplined.

We withdrew in a measured arc because the flats offered no walls to save us. Withdrawal is geometry, not shame.

Rauk grunted once as we moved.

— We can’t hold this.

He did not sound afraid. He sounded offended — like the universe had presented an equation that refused to balance.

— We weren’t sent to hold it.

That was the closest thing to comfort we had.

We were not the wall. We were the receipt.

We moved from relay stub to relay stub, using broken infrastructure as cover. Every time we crossed a new patch of open ground, we saw more bodies arranged into messages. Every time we tried to locate a stable friendly node, the node either went silent or answered in devotion.

The Chapter was not simply "falling."

It was being harvested.

Brann said it first, because Brann was the one who still wanted the world to make sense in human terms.

— This is too organized. It isn’t just the Legatus.

Rauk wanted to argue, because Rauk’s anger needed a single face to attach to. Kessel did not argue because Kessel had already reached the same conclusion in the language he trusted: observed patterns, repeated intervals, predictable reactions.

Hadrik looked at me.

I did not want to say it. Saying it meant we were hunting a mind, not just surviving a storm.

But witness is ammunition. You do not keep ammunition unused because it feels heavy.

— There is a second hand.

— A mind that isn’t preaching. A mind that is executing.

Rauk spat dust.

— So we kill him.

— Not now.

— Not yet. We keep five intact.

— We keep the line intact.

Hadrik corrected quietly.

He was right. Five was a means, not an idol.

We could not save the Vexillatio. We could not "undo" the rot. We could not make devotion become reason again by force of will.

We could do one thing.

Carry a lawful witness core through the theater long enough for Alaric to build a sentence that could not be argued away later.

Service demands reason instead of devotion.

I did not say the line aloud.

I lived it, one measured step at a time, across the extraction flats that had stopped being a place and become a proof.
