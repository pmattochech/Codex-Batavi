# Valerian Heresy — Reading B (Corbec’s view)

There are two ways an archive lies.

One is by omission. You simply do not write down what hurts your doctrine.

The other is by merging. You take two separate truths and grind them together until they become one smooth, comforting fiction.

Malakor V forced me to learn the second kind.

We reached the containment line at dusk by a clock that no longer mattered. The sky did not change color so much as it changed density: the orange of burning dust thickening into a darker bruise, the green static in the clouds becoming more frequent as the Cicatrix pressure shifted.

The line itself was not a wall. It was a set of decisions made by people who had given up on walls.

Derelict hauler frames laid on their sides to create hard cover. Extraction cranes toppled into improvised barricades. Railcars welded into angles that forced approaches to slow. Everything old industry had once done to move ore now did a different job: it slowed living things long enough for bolters to speak.

We arrived and found Drusus’s language in the air without Drusus being present.

Tags.

Manifests.

Numbers on slates.

The kind of work that looks like cowardice to men who think war is only about killing.

Hadrik saw it too, and because Hadrik was the one among us who still believed the universe could be made orderly through labor, he looked relieved.

— This is an archive line.

Rauk snorted.

— It’s a slaughter line.

Both were correct.

The cages were in the distance—Black-Cell infrastructure, half-buried, partially dismantled, the kind of facility you build when you admit that some problems cannot be solved and can only be contained. In the older years, you could keep a clean boundary around it. You could say: what is inside the cages is a separate category of horror.

Malakor V had dragged the cages into the open.

Not physically. Not with cranes and chain. It dragged them into the same story.

Ahead of us, on the line, brothers were changing in real time. Not all of them. Not the full war-form. But enough: bodies swelling, posture breaking, voices doing strange things in the vox—sometimes too animal, sometimes too clear.

I watched a Marine I recognized—Kessel recognized him too, by the way his aim hesitated—drop his bolter as if the weapon had become a foreign object. He grabbed the barricade, fingers digging into metal, and his vox opened.

— I am fine.

The sentence was perfect.

His body was not.

Hadrik reached for him by instinct.

I caught Hadrik’s wrist.

Not hard. Not a fight. A stop.

Hadrik looked at me as if I had slapped him.

— He’s—

— We do not merge.

Rauk’s helm turned toward me.

— What?

I did not have sealed terminology. I did not have Drusus’s later labels. I had only the necessity of keeping the Chapter from writing a lie it would later use as absolution.

— If we treat every beast as treason, we will execute men who never chose devotion.

Kessel’s voice cut in, cold.

— And if we treat treason as a beast, we’ll forgive it.

Brann said nothing. He watched the changing Marine and then looked toward the cages in the distance, as if he could see two futures at once and hated both.

That was the split.

Not between mercy and cruelty.

Between categories that must not be allowed to eat each other.

Hadrik swallowed.

— What changed now, and what existed before.

I nodded.

— Yes.

Rauk spat.

— It’s all the same thing. It kills.

— It kills.

— But it does not mean the same.

The line erupted in gunfire before we could argue further.

A wave hit the barricades—men in armor moving with the relieved cohesion I had begun to recognize. Devotion faces behind sealed lenses. Not every brother. Not even most. But enough that the line’s defenders had to make the same decision again and again: do you call them brothers or targets?

The answer changed depending on the next five seconds.

We fought as five because five was the only number we could still keep lawful without supervision.

Rauk anchored the left. He fired until his barrel glowed. He did not waste speech. When he spoke, it was to call threats, not emotions.

Hadrik worked the center behind him, doing the ugly job: marking bodies, checking for life signs, filing what could be filed while fire stitched the air. Every time a brother dropped and did not rise, Hadrik’s hand moved in the same pattern—tag, slate, move—because repetition is how you survive grief when the theater refuses to pause for it.

Kessel watched the right and made every shot count. He did not aim for drama. He aimed for function: knees, lenses, throats when the helm seal broke.

Brann did what Brann always did: he moved into the gaps. He prevented the five from becoming four by accident.

I carried the halberd.

Not as a symbol. As a solution to a specific problem: when you cannot trust the vox and you cannot trust the route overlays, reach becomes honesty. Hook geometry becomes the last form of regulation you can still apply with your hands.

The first time I used the hook to pull a brother off the barricade, my stomach turned.

It felt like dragging a man out of a ditch.

It also felt like execution.

He was not in full war-form. He was not a monster. He was a Marine whose body had begun to break in ways none of us understood in that year. His hands had already torn grooves into the metal. His voice in the vox was a wet hiss, half prayer, half hunger.

Hadrik shouted his name.

I did not answer Hadrik. I did the job and left the question for later, because later is the only place questions can safely live.

The next wave came from the direction of the cages.

That was when the line almost lied by merging.

I saw it in the defenders’ posture: the moment they began to treat everything that moved as one category. Beast, turned brother, pre-crisis horror, conflict-hour horror—one kill order. One simplification. One relief.

Relief is poison.

It is the same drug devotion offers.

If we let relief win, the Chapter would survive the night and die later in a different way: it would become the kind of institution that executes first and justifies afterward.

Rauk took a round through the shoulder and did not fall. He grunted once and kept firing as if pain was a clerical error.

Hadrik went down next.

Not from a clean shot. From a brother’s hand.

A Marine—plate intact, movement wrong—reached over the barricade and grabbed Hadrik by the helm. There was no rage in the gesture. Only possession, like a man claiming a tool.

Hadrik’s vox opened and I heard his breath.

— Corbec.

Hadrik said, and in that single syllable I heard everything he had believed about order and labor and the possibility of saving people through correct procedure.

I swung the halberd and cut the hand off at the wrist. The Marine withdrew, and for a moment Hadrik was free.

Then the Marine’s other hand hit Hadrik in the throat with a force that caved the seal.

Hadrik collapsed with a sound like a valve giving way.

I could not go to him. If I went to him, the line would break.

Rauk saw it happen and roared a curse that was not a slogan and not a prayer. He vaulted the barricade, bolter forgotten, and drove his combat knife into the attacker’s chest in a motion too raw to be doctrine.

The attacker did not bleed like a man. He bled like a machine leaking oil.

Kessel fired and put a round through the attacker’s helm, and the body fell away from Hadrik as if it had been cut from a rope.

Hadrik was still breathing. Barely.

Rauk tried to lift him.

Kessel said:

— No time.

Rauk snarled:

— He’s ours.

Kessel’s voice did not change.

— He’s dead in two minutes. If you carry him, you’ll make it one.

That was the kind of mercy Malakor V taught: the mercy of not lying to yourself about time.

Rauk carried Hadrik anyway.

He did it with one arm, firing with the other, because Rauk refused to let the theater decide which debts were payable.

It cost him.

A second wave hit, and in the chaos a Versibar—conflict-hour, newly manifesting, fast and wrong—cleared the barricade and landed among us. It moved with the irrational mass of a thing whose mind had already stopped negotiating with restraint.

Rauk turned to meet it with Hadrik in his arms.

For half a second I saw the math on Rauk’s posture: he had decided to be a wall.

The Versibar tore him open anyway.

Not dramatically. Efficiently. Like a machine tool cutting through material it was designed to cut.

Rauk hit the ground with Hadrik still held against his chest, as if he had chosen to keep carrying the debt even while dying.

Hadrik’s breathing stopped.

Rauk’s vox opened once. No words came out. Just static and a thin sound that might have been the beginning of a vow or the end of a prayer.

Kessel killed the Versibar with four shots to the throat when it turned to chase Brann. He did it without hesitation. Without triumph. Without noise.

Then Kessel was hit from the flank by a brother who should not have been there.

That was the other thing weaponized command does: it puts familiar faces where they should not be, so your instincts betray you.

Kessel’s head snapped toward the movement a fraction too late. The brother’s blade went under his pauldron into the neck seal.

Kessel made a sound I will not write down.

I killed the brother with the halberd hook, pulling him close and driving the head through his sternum. The brother’s vox whispered one word as he died:

— First.

The word tasted like rust.

Kessel went to his knees.

Brann tried to reach him.

I stopped Brann the way I had stopped Hadrik earlier.

Brann’s head turned toward me, and in that turn I saw the moment his faith in "we can save everyone" finally broke.

— We keep the split.

Brann’s voice came out hoarse.

— He’s Kessel.

I nodded.

— I know.

Kessel looked up at me. His lenses were dimming. His blood ran down the front of his plate in a line too steady to be accidental.

— Do not—

Kessel tried to say.

I leaned close enough that my helm almost touched his. Close enough that the words stayed in our private channel and did not become a speech.

— I will not merge it.
— I will not merge it. I will not let them write a lie with your death — I told him.

Kessel’s mouth moved once behind the grille as if he were trying to smile and remembered he had never wasted muscles on that kind of expression.

Then he went still.

By the time the line stabilized again, five had become two.

Brann and me.

The defenders around us were still firing, still shouting, still trying to survive. Somewhere behind the barricades, someone was already updating slates, because the Chapter cannot stop filing even while it bleeds.

I looked at the cages in the distance, and then at the bodies at our feet.

I understood then what Reading B would become in Drusus’s hands, years later, once sealed words and clinical models existed.

But in that hour it was simpler.

If we merged categories, we would give the rot what it wanted most: relief.

Relief from thinking.

Relief from responsibility.

Relief from the burden of being lawful in a universe that rewards hunger.

Brann’s vox came quiet.

— What now?

I looked at the extraction flats and saw, in the distance, movement that was too deliberate to be a wave.

A mind walking through chaos as if it were a checklist.

— Now we carry truth with us.

I lifted the halberd and felt its weight answer like a tool that does not care about my grief.

Service demands reason instead of devotion.

This time I spoke it aloud.

Not as a chant.

As a refusal to let the archive lie.
