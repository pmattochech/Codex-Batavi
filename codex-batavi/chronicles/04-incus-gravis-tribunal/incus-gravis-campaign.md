# CHRONICLES: THE Incus-Gravis CAMPAIGN (Blood and Iron)

## THE WEIGHT OF BLOOD AND THE SHADOW OF ULLANOR

**(Restricted Record: Incus-Gravis Containment Zone | Joint Forces: Cohors Batavorum and Blood Angels)**

The fog of burned promethium and volcanic ash had cut visibility in the Primus Sector to less than ten meters. The air stank of ozone, corrosive acid, and boiled xenos blood. The front lines had disintegrated into a lethal chaos of melee against the remnants of an invasion that was already mathematically doomed.

At the carnage’s epicenter, Veteran Gideon of the Blood Angels fought to hold cohesion. His crimson armor was blackened with soot, and his breath came rough through his helm’s filters. He was exhausted—not physical exhaustion; Sanguinius’s biology rejected fatigue—but a deep spiritual erosion. For hours the static of battle had been giving way to whispers at the base of his skull. Echoes of a past that was not his, the ancestral, terrifying grief of his Primarch carved into the DNA ribbons of his gene-seed.

It was in that tactical void, under the stroboscopic light of a shorting generator, that Gideon looked back to reposition his squad.

The smoke spun and parted, revealing what his frayed mind had no capacity to process.

Through the murk, a colossal silhouette emerged. Three and a half meters tall. The outline of a *Castra-Lupus* chassis, where the architecture of Terminator armor fused with the brutal mass of an Indomitus Dreadnought. On monstrously broad pauldrons rested the asymmetric bulk of two heavy wolf pelts: Viggo’s on the right shoulder, the Batavian Wolf’s on the left.

The amber light, projected from behind that gray-iron mass, crushed the Chapter’s colors and left only the topography of shadow. The failure in Gideon’s mind was not a tactical choice; it was a biological detonation ten thousand years deep. The “Lupercal Anomaly” did its work. Gideon’s brain did not see the Lord Castellan of Noviomagus Prime marching to aid. He saw the Warmaster, Horus Lupercal, walking triumphant through the smoking wreckage of the *Vengeful Spirit*.

The Black Rage, the supreme flaw of the Angels’ line, obliterated the last barrier of his reason.

— HORUS! — Gideon’s scream tore the allied *vox* channel, a roar of pure agony, grief, and absolute hate that overrode the artillery’s noise.

The Blood Angel ignored the enemy horde, spun on his heels, and hurled himself at Alaric von Helis with the force of a ballistic missile, his power sword raised for a suicidal decapitating strike.

Less than thirty meters away, operating in his 0.05 seconds of synaptic latency, Alaric von Helis processed the event not with surprise but with industrial coldness. The sensors of his crimson helm logged the spike in adrenaline and the lethal cardiac acceleration of the approaching warrior. The Lord Castellan did not engage *Wolf Stance*. He did not touch the hilt of his force gladius.

The mental equation formed in the silence of his atheist mind: *Friendly asset. Critical mental stability failure. Risk of fratricidal fire. Required action: Non-lethal suppression.*

When Gideon leapt, boosted by jump pack for the final blow, Alaric did not step back. A sub-millimeter tightening of his human jaw, hidden beneath the lupine snout helm, was the only shift in his emotional stasis. He took a single step forward, anchoring the claws of his adamantium boots in the ferrocrete.

The impact of a maddened Blood Angel would shatter a common Astartes, but Alaric used the three tons of his own biometric and metallic mass to intercept him midair. Alaric’s two colossal gauntlets struck simultaneously against Gideon’s shoulders and crimson breastplate. With a mechanical twist of his torso, calling the brutal force of the *Castra-Lupus*’s industrial servomotors in harmony with the hypertrophy of his chimeric gene-seed, Alaric canceled the Angel’s inertia and drove him onto his back.

The impact spiderwebbed the deck.

Gideon thrashed with *berserker* strength, snarling curses and weeping Sanguinius’s name, saliva staining his own helm’s grille. It was useless. Noviomagus’s gravity had claimed him.

Alaric knelt on the fallen Angel. The Batavian applied exactly 2.54 tons of static pressure to the Blood Angel’s ceramite breastplate—the exact threshold to crack the armor and fully pin the warrior’s arms to the ground, but calculated to the millimeter not to crush his primary hearts.

Without altering his breathing cadence, holding Gideon pinned under the anchor of his own monstrous body, Alaric keyed the *Strategium* vox.

— Batavian command to Red Fleet. We have a gene-containment breach in the Primus Sector. Friendly asset immobilized. Bring a Chaplain of the Ninth Legion to my coordinates. Priority alpha.

The Lord Castellan did not move. He ignored the battle’s flow fifty meters away, ignored the sparks of short circuits, and, above all, ignored the “Traitor” and “Monster” the Blood Angel spat on his visor. Alaric took no offense; the insult of damaged equipment is only static noise.

Five minutes later, the temperature around the crater plunged. Nearby flames seemed to recede, as if oxygen were being siphoned by an invisible entity. Soft, funereal footsteps echoed.

From the ash fog emerged Mephiston. The Chief Librarian was wrapped in crimson robes, his gaze hidden by the hood’s darkness. His psychic presence was so crushing the air seemed made of lead. He took in the scene: the Industrial Beast holding a son of the Angel to the deck with the same efficiency a hydraulic press holds a plate.

Mephiston extended a pale hand. The air cracked with Warp energy. He did not touch Gideon’s flesh; his power slid straight into the warrior’s shattered mind. Within seconds the Blood Angel’s screams ceased, his body slackening into deep psychic stasis.

— Containment is assured. You may release him, Lord Castellan — Mephiston’s voice was a cadaverous whisper that scraped the inside of the mind, but Alaric’s pragmatic, atheist biology batted the psychic pressure aside like water on plasteel.

The Castellan’s servomotors whined under load compensation as he eased the tonnage and rose slowly, returning to his majestic, terrifying height of 3.5 meters. The pelts swayed on his shoulders.

Mephiston lifted his pale face. His eyes found the Batavian helm’s orange lenses. The Lord of Death, who walked the Immaterium and knew the shape of nightmares, studied the visual anomaly.

— I see what he saw — Mephiston murmured, his voice carrying the weight of a grim finding. — In shadow, your chassis’s architecture and the bulk of your pelts wear our executioner’s sins. You carry Lupercal’s ghost on your shoulders, Castellan.

The motors in Alaric’s neck locked with a dry click. The **Axiom of Reason** dictated that form followed function, but there, in the silence between the two leaders, lay tacit recognition of parallel curses.

Alaric neither stepped back nor showed shame. His empathy spoke in the only language he knew: the pragmatism of wounded biology.

— We all have monsters chained in our blood, Lord Mephiston — Alaric’s voice came through the helm’s filters, low, without moral judgment, but heavy with structural condemnation of the Arch-traitor’s failure. — I stabilized mine, the **Wolf’s Curse**, in the forge. Your warrior lost the key to his own cage because his gene-sire’s loyalty echoed too loud. It is a tragic biological failure.

Alaric took a mechanical step aside, a micro-gesture of muted respect, clearing the path for Mephiston to recover Gideon’s body.

— My silhouette is only the weight of metal and memory that keeps me anchored. — Alaric pointed to the Blood Angel in stasis. — Collect your brother, Lord of Death. He fought well before the chain broke.

Without waiting for answer or approval from a warrior of legend, Alaric von Helis pivoted on his adamantium boots. The crimson cape dragged over the cracked deck while the Hand of Judgment rested on the gladius’s hilt. The Batavian turned his back on Mephiston and marched back into the ash fog, leaving the Ninth Legion to deal with its own shadows. The Wall could not stop. Friction waited.

## THE TRIBUNAL OF IRON AND BLOOD

**(Restricted Record: Commander’s Quarters, Cruiser *Bloodcaller* | Joint Forces)**

The air in Commander Dante’s quarters smelled of sacred incense and Baal’s aged wine. To the hypersaturated senses of the delegation from Noviomagus Prime, that space was a melancholic, almost suffocating contrast to the sterile stink of ozone, dried blood, and machine oil they themselves exuded.

Dante, stripped of his golden helm, seemed to carry millennia’s weight carved into every line of his exhausted features. A few steps away, Mephiston stood motionless in the peripheral shadows, a statue of crimson velvet radiating contained, crushing psychic power.

On the other side of the hololith table, the Forge rose in silence.

The **Triumvirate** formed a monolith of repulsion to aesthetics: Lord Castellan Alaric von Helis, massive in his *Castra-Lupus* chassis; Chief Apothecary Drusus; and High Chaplain Varro, whose wolf-skull mask seemed to swallow the room’s thin light. Behind them, as immobile as lead statues, two veteran Batavian guards flanked a Batavian Neophyte.

The young warrior showed micro-tremors in his hands and discreetly quickened breathing—the first physiological signs of **Furor** stage. Varro had brought him not as geometric punishment but as discipline *in situ*; the boy needed to witness the weight of duty without surrendering to hatred of his own flesh.

— The Apothecarion informed me Veteran Gideon lives — Dante’s voice was rough, cutting the static. — He succumbed to the Black Rage. But my reports say you subdued him with your own hands. You did not spill his blood, Castellan.

Alaric remained static. Only the faintest whine of his gyros signaled processing. — Containment was applied solely to preserve a friendly asset’s life.

Mephiston slid from the shadows. The temperature around the Chief Librarian plunged in response to his presence. — You spared his body, Castellan. But your shape broke his mind. — Mephiston’s gaze crossed the room, dense as the void. — In shadow, your chassis’s architecture and those pelts wear our executioner’s sins. You project the ghost of Horus Lupercal. You think we do not feel the irony? You think we do not see the direct insult to our Father’s memory?

The silence that fell on the compartment was not cold indifference but a dam about to give under hydrostatic pressure.

Varro struck the base of his *Crozius* on the deck. Not a challenge blow, but a hollow, mathematical, tired thud. — You believe you hold a monopoly on pain, sons of Sanguinius — Varro’s cavernous voice growled behind the bone skull. — You suffer for love of a perfect father who died. We suffer for hatred of a failed father who condemned us.

Dante lifted his eyes, visibly surprised by the break in the Bataves’ sterile protocol. — What do you mean, Chaplain?

Alaric raised his right gauntlet. Not to silence his brothers, but to transmit a silent tactical command that violated the Visage Doctrine and the holiest law of the *Cohors Batavorum*. Through a data pulse fired across short-range *Noosphere*, the order executed without the inefficiency of a single word.

The hiss of compressed gas venting whispered through the quarters in perfect sync. With heavy mechanical clicks, Alaric, Drusus, and Varro lifted their hands to their gorgets and removed their helms. The Batavian Neophyte at the rear held his breath, witnessing an act of vulnerability the entire Cohort was forbidden to see. Dante and Mephiston took an involuntary step back. A micro-gesture of transhuman horror.

The faces of Noviomagus’s three leaders were not the features of men hardened by war. They were abominations meticulously contained. Drusus and Varro, stabilized at **Exanimus** stage, showed flesh rewritten by predatory, hostile biology. Facial hair, rigorously shaved and trimmed to military pattern, was visibly thick as a beast’s pelt. Their bone structures were bestial, built for crushing, and from lips split by relentless microsurgery projected long, pale, sharp lower canines. Their eyes were burning pits of captive flame in orange, yellow, and red. At the formation’s center, dictating the group’s geometry, Alaric von Helis imposed the inhuman stasis of his intermediate stage, the Alpha Anomaly. His scarred human physiognomy showed severe maxillary hypertrophy, crowned by deep crimson eyes with greened rims and slit pupils. The chronic tension of his masseters and the Castellan’s own predatory stillness made the three seem locked in a perpetual snarl, even in absolute, frozen silence.

They were monsters applying terrifying discipline to refuse their own monstrosity.

Drusus took a millimetric step forward. His mutilated face held no trace of hatred or shame, only frozen pragmatism. His orange-flame eyes swept Dante from crown to boot. — Commander Dante, the aesthetic anomaly your mind registered is not a tactic of scorn — the Chief Apothecary’s voice sounded raw, stripped of his surgical helm’s synthetic filtering. — It is a calculated psychiatric contingency. Our biology demands visual adaptation. Hating our own existence and form is thermodynamic waste that drives operational failure and morale collapse. The symbols and anchors on the Lord Castellan’s armor are what stop **our Bataves**—and the civilian population that serves us—from seeing us purely as the aberration our blood tries to impose. The iconography is prophylaxis. It keeps us executing our function instead of drowning in aversion.

Alaric stepped forward, covering Drusus’s coldness with his own colossal mass. The Castellan’s terrible face, with the alpha-anomaly eyes—crimson ringed by a greened edge—bore secular exhaustion. A millimetric tightening of his lupine jaw preceded his words.

— The industrial carapace around my flesh is neither trap nor vanity, Regent — Alaric’s voice was a low, irrevocable thunder, vibrating in the chests of those present. — I am not a fallen warrior, and I do not inhabit a cyber-sarcophagus. Appropriating a Dreadnought’s plates is the limit of physics. It is the only material cuirass able to hold my three and a half meters of living muscle mass without shattering under my own motive force.

He raised his armored hand, pointing to the heavy organic adornments on his shoulders.

— And these pelts are no insult to Ullanor. They are my untouchable mourning. The black pelt of the Batavian Wolf attests the bond with the frontier, our people’s liberation. And the white pelt… is Viggo. The lethal ally who stabilized my sanity during the Silent Vigil—under the world’s acid rain, with nothing to offer but cold. It is respect for a utility time took too soon. I carry my dead on ceramite because I cannot plant them in the soil.

Alaric turned his neck a hair toward the Batavian Neophyte trembling at the rear of the room.

— Your warrior broke because he heard the echo of the past, Dante. I do not have that organic luxury. I am the only one who descended to the abyssal bottom of our genetic curse and dragged my own mind back. You mourn the tragedy of the Black Rage. I am forced to put down my own sons with these hands when their eyes lose utility to the Throne. I hear their bones crack as they become irrational beasts, and I drive my gladius into the chest of warriors who trusted me to hold the cohesion of their souls.

Alaric turned his gaze to Mephiston, absolute contempt shaping every syllable.

— We hate the Warmaster with a structural fury your Chapter of angels will never understand. Because the weakness of his ego is the rot that demands continuous containment in our veins. He yielded. He bent to Chaos’s whispers because his moral engineering was inefficient. And we are forced to look in the mirror every solar cycle and see the shadow of his pathetic failure reflected in our own faces and the shape of our steel.

Mephiston lowered his head, the aura of psychic power withdrawing inward. He tried to read Alaric’s mind, seeking the void of a cold machine or a barbarian’s rage. He found neither. He found an iron pillar holding up a collapsing sky; a will that repudiated failure and refused to fall.

— And yet… — Dante murmured, voice choked with the purest tactical and moral respect. — You do not break.

— We do not break — Alaric confirmed. The perpetual snarl of his scarred face did not change, but the economy of his gaze carried a lethal promise. — We will never bend. *Ferriculum* does not yield. The Arch-traitor yielded to the curse out of vanity. We chain it to the forge and convert it into motive force to serve the Imperium. We are the Wall, Dante. And the Wall’s function is to bear.

With extreme precision and economy of movement, Alaric lifted the heavy gray-iron helm and lowered it over his head. The pneumatic hiss of pressure locks sealed the monster once more inside the machine. Drusus and Varro executed the same mechanical motion in unison.

Dante issued no order. He placed his hand on his own breastplate, where the Imperial eagle of his golden armor rested, and bowed his head. It was a deep reverence, stripped of empty ceremony, reserved only for those who understand the annihilation of self in duty’s name.

— Let the stars witness Noviomagus Prime’s burden — said the Regent of Baal, words heavy with solemnity. — Let our fleets follow separate vectors, Lord Castellan. But hold empirical certainty that the Ninth Legion will never again dare question the purity of your steel.

Alaric answered with a millimetric nod, purely military. He pivoted on his heels, Viggo’s heavy pelt and the Batavian Wolf’s following the motion’s inertia.

The **Triumvirate** marched from the quarters, flanked by their veteran guards and the Neophyte—a soldier who had entered that room afraid of the beast in his blood, and left walking with the erect, geometric posture of one who had just understood that his curse was not a sentence, but the ultimate test of his utility.

## THE WEIGHT OF MERCY

**(Restricted Record: Landing Zones on Incus-Gravis | Joint Forces: Cohors Batavorum and Blood Angels)**

The agreement to separate the fleets and the tactical truce had no time to be processed in silence. Before the heavy extraction ships could descend through Incus-Gravis’s blackened atmosphere, realspace’s geometry tore. An unmapped anomaly vomited a strike force of Red Corsairs, drawn to the coordinates by the thermal trail of recent bloodshed.

Side by side, by strict mutual survival necessity, Baal’s noble angels and Noviomagus Prime’s iron colossi formed an improvised defensive line of shields and bolters. Combat transmuted at once into chaotic Zone Mortalis confinement.

It was at the epicenter of that grinder of flesh and plasteel that Commander Dante, operating with his line’s lethal grace, witnessed the terminal cost of Batavian steel.

The Batavian **Haldor**, a veteran of the **10th Vexillation** (*Guard of the Anchor*, colloquially the Castellan’s *pack*), who had stood guard beside Alaric hours before, hit the limit of his organic elasticity. The succession of extreme kinetic stress, accumulated peripheral damage to the armor, and chiefly the corrupting psychic influence of the wound in the sky acted as the final chemical catalyst. **Inner Vigil** collapsed. The iron cage broke.

Dante, after hewing a heretic with his crimson power axe, froze at an unnatural sound. Not a cry of pain or battle fury. The dull, terrible noise of industrial ceramite cracking from within under impossible biological pressure.

The Blood Angels’ Commander watched, instincts paralyzed by horrified fascination, as Haldor’s gray-iron cuirass shattered. The Batavian’s body entered aggressive, terminal hypermetabolism. Bones lengthened with nauseating cracks, piercing tendons and expanding musculature in an instant, terrifying, irreversible metamorphosis.

In less than twenty seconds, there was no longer an Adeptus Astartes soldier there. There was only the final stage of the **Wolf’s Curse**—the **Execratio** stage. A colossal predatory abomination four and a half meters tall, a bestial chimera of hypertrophied muscle, barbed-wire fur, and elongated jaws, blind with the purest organic irrational fury.

But the beast did not strike its allies. The atavistic instinct locked in Russ’s genetics identified the nearest territorial threat: the Traitors.

What followed was a massacre that silenced even war’s clamor. The **Versibar** that had once been Haldor plunged into the Red Corsairs’ formation center. Astartes veterans of the Long War were dismembered with the same physiological ease a human would tear cloth. Black keratin claws pierced Terminator armor; jaws crushed helms as if they were glass.

When tears in reality’s fabric began to spew Immaterium horrors, the creature simply slaughtered them. Dante witnessed a moment of total inversion of the natural order when a Khorne Berserker—swollen with daemonic possession and roaring promises of endless blood—charged with his chainaxe against the beast. The fight lasted less than three tactical seconds. The Batavian abomination absorbed the axe blow, advanced its monstrous mass, and subdued the Blood God’s champion, crushing armor and inner daemonic entity under pure biological hydraulic pressure, before tearing the heretic’s head off with its own claws.

But entropy has its price. With no more enemies in the immediate quadrant, the beast’s silver, bloodshot eyes swept the perimeter. The Throne’s light had been extinguished in that mind. Only hunger, pain, and agony remained.

The beast turned its colossal body toward the Imperial defensive line, snarling, flexing elongated limbs. Before the Blood Angels could train weapons to contain the monster, an even denser silhouette crossed the battlefront.

Alaric von Helis marched toward his lost son.

The Lord Castellan did not raise the *Mobile Bastion* as a ram. He did not engage aggressive *Wolf Stance*, nor did he draw the immense frost axe he used to shear heavy armor. An axe blow would fracture bone needlessly and inflict inefficient, lingering agony. For a fallen Batavian, the **Triumvirate** offered only the mercy of the clean cut.

With economy of movement, Alaric drew his force gladius, *The Sentence*. The soldier’s short, surgical blade.

The beast roared, a bellow that froze the air’s moisture, and launched in a predatory leap at its own master. Alaric neither blinked nor dodged. A sub-millimeter contraction tensed the powerful tendons of his neck. He locked the *Castra-Lupus*’s gyros, anchoring his three tons of metal and mourning in the ferrocrete, and absorbed the beast’s full physical impact. The abomination’s claws raked violently at Alaric’s gray-iron breastplate, seeking his throat.

But the Castellan bore the weight, unmoving. Using Second Blade doctrine, he braced the beast’s arm and, in a motion stripped of hate—driven only by clinical coldness and duty—guided the gladius. The blade slid along the base of the beast’s skull, entered the exact joint of the cervical vertebrae, and severed the brainstem in one instant anatomical strike.

Haldor’s nervous system shut down milliseconds before his brain processed pain. The beast’s massive body collapsed, but Alaric did not let it fall in the field’s filth. He held the dead weight of the beast in his mechanical arms, lowering it to the ground with near-reverent slowness.

Dozens of meters away, Mephiston stopped breathing and closed his eyes. The Lord of Death’s mind, anesthetized against the Immaterium’s atrocities, was hit by a shock front of psychic pressure that did not come from the Warp, but from the Lord Castellan’s hermetic mind. There was no hate. There was no “Rage” that moved Fenris’s line or Chaos’s maddened wrath. Mephiston felt grief so old, cold, and crushing it seemed the bottom of a lightless ocean. It was petrified sorrow across centuries. The geological pain of a father forced to put down his own sons personally to save them from themselves, carrying that annihilation as a daily gear of duty.

In the center of the soot-covered crater, Alaric von Helis knelt beside the deformed, irreversible body. With colossal hands moving in precise micro-adjustments, he cleared the wreckage of ceramite armor still hanging from the veteran’s flesh.

Inside, he found and withdrew two chains dark with blood. The first plate was the crude iron rectangle every citizen received in the frontier hives, the seal of humanity; the second, the ceramite aquila that marked his rise as a Batavian warrior.

Alaric lifted the two plates. Slowly, he tied them to a heavy steel cord hidden under the white fur lining of Viggo the *Thunderwolf* on his right shoulder. The pelt’s weight held most of the cord silent against ceramite—but at the exposed edges, where metal escaped the skin’s weight, dozens of plates chimed softly in the dark against the armor’s breastplate.

Dante and Mephiston watched the rite in absolute silence. Understanding settled on Baal’s angels. For the *Cohors Batavorum*, the Batavian had not died as a cursed monster; he had died as a son of Noviomagus. His legacy would stay intact, clean, forever catalogued on his master’s shoulders. Blood failed, but the Wall’s memory would never rust.

Alaric rose with the slowness of a load-bearing column. He made one dry motion with his arm to clear the gladius of biological ichor, seated the blade in its magnetic scabbard, and turned his crimson helm toward the front line, orange lenses reigniting with clinical precision to process remaining targets.

The beast had found peace, but the Wall still operated.

## THE RITE OF ASH AND THE WALL’S ECHO

**(Restricted Record: Incus-Gravis Spaceport and South Wall | Observers: Ninth Legion)**

Incus-Gravis’s sky bled ash. Dense gray flakes fell on the cracked ferrocrete of the landing zone, shrouding battle wreckage and xenos ichor in a pale shroud. The silence that fell on the joint forces was absolute.

At the center of the landing strip, a funeral pyre had been raised. It was not built of sacred wood or adorned with purity scrolls. It was a forge-world’s altar: twisted plasteel girders, empty heavy ammo crates, and solid promethium blocks. Upon it lay **Haldor**’s body.

The **Execratio**’s colossal bestial shape had been hidden under heavy ballistic canvas, lashed tight with anchor chains. The procedure was not to hide shame, but to return to the Batavian, in death, the contained form and dignity the **Wolf’s Curse** had stolen in his last seconds of life.

Alaric von Helis stood ten paces from the pyre. The Lord Castellan did not move. Both gauntlets rested on the gladius’s pommel, the *Mobile Bastion*’s edge at his side; he was his son’s silent, immutable sentinel. Viggo’s white pelt and the Batavian Wolf’s dark hide hung heavy under the ash-snow.

Chief Apothecary Drusus was the first to approach the structure. His medical servomechanisms hummed in a low, lugubrious tone. At other Astartes Chapters’ funerals, the Apothecary would be the bearer of hope; the one who lifted harvested gene-seed to prove the warrior’s essence would live in new Neophytes. Drusus, however, kept his white gauntlets empty.

The multi-lenses of his surgical helm focused on the body under the canvas.

— The biological furnace collapsed — Drusus declared, sterile voice echoing cold and mathematical through the *vox-caster*. — **Execratio** mutation devoured the code. Progenoid glands were incinerated by the beast’s cellular rewrite. There is no harvest, Castellan. Haldor’s blood ends here. The equation reached zero.

It was a clinical diagnosis, but it carried the brutality of absolute extinction. Without gene-seed, Haldor would have no descendants in the Chapter. He drew a flask and anointed the canvas not with sacred oils and incense, but with purified machine oil—the same lubricant that kept **the Vexillation’s weapons** firing.

— Let iron return to earth. Let flesh return to the forge.

Drusus stepped back, yielding place to High Chaplain Varro. Noviomagus’s Judex walked to the pyre, wolf-skull mask impassive. Instead of belting glory hymns at high decibels, Varro raised his *Crozius Arcanum* and performed the *Conclamatio*—the old, austere rite of calling the dead.

Varro struck the *Crozius*’s flat base on the ferrocrete. The impact sounded like a siege cannon’s discharge. — *Haldor of the Tenth.* — Varro’s cavernous voice summoned the warrior. Only Incus-Gravis’s wind answered.

Varro struck the *Crozius* a second time, fracturing the floor. — *Haldor, Shield of Noviomagus.* — No answer.

The Chaplain delivered the third and final blow, driving adamantium into stone. — *Haldor, Son of the Wall.*

The silence that followed was dense and absolute. Absence of response was final certification of death. Varro lowered his head a hair. — He does not answer the call. The watch is ended. Service is complete.

The Chaplain activated an ignition cylinder and threw it onto the pyre. Solid promethium roared in a burst of orange flame and black smoke, swallowing the canvas and heating chains until they glowed like embers.

— The Emperor dictates… — Varro began, tone as heavy as lead. — …we comply — Drusus and Alaric finished in perfect unison, metallic voices sealing the brother’s fate in the fire’s light.

A short distance away, Commander Dante and Mephiston witnessed the rite. Flame-light danced on Dante’s golden armor, reflecting a leader’s exhaustion who understood loss.

— No warrior should die like this — Dante murmured, voice heavy with shared grief. — Our Death Company brothers fall in madness, but our Blood Priests can still save their genetic legacy. Their death is not the end.

— For the Bataves, it is absolute annihilation, Lord Dante — Mephiston replied, eyes fixed on Alaric’s motionless silhouette. — Their curse does not steal only the mind; it incinerates the future. Haldor was erased from the line. In minutes, nothing will remain there but metal slag and ash.

Dante looked at Alaric. The gray-iron colossus still stood guard before the fire, a few meters from the pyre, bearing the extreme heat wave without a single step back. The Regent of Baal remembered the two identification plates—the civilian’s and the Aquila’s—that Alaric had taken from the monster’s shattered body, tying them to the cord hidden under his pelts.

— That is why he keeps the names under the mantle — Dante understood, voice choked by a grim epiphany. — Drusus said the equation reached zero. But Alaric refuses that math. If Haldor’s flesh cannot live in new recruits, his name will live on his master’s armor. Alaric carries the dead because their blood cannot.

Mephiston nodded slowly. The Lord of Death’s mind, anesthetized against the Immaterium’s atrocities, was submerged by mute psychic pressure radiating from Alaric. It was not Fenris’s hot fury, nor Chaos’s hate. It was old, petrifying grief. The tectonic pain of a father forced to put down hundreds of sons to save them from themselves.

— They do not pray for miracles, Commander. They believe in friction. And friction dictates that everything wears and breaks one day — Mephiston concluded. — Their funeral is not a farewell to the beyond; it is recognition that the machine stopped.

Hours later, the fire had died, but the Wall’s echo still rang.

At the base of the immense South Wall that shielded Incus-Gravis’s main hive, local *Ministorum* servitors operated their laser chisels under the soot-rain. With mechanical exactitude, a new name had been carved into the ferrocrete foundations: **HALDOR**.

The Batavian frontier raised no vain statues, no marble memorials. When a warrior fell, his name was fused into the city’s foundation. He ceased to be the wall’s guard and became the Wall itself. The vigil remained.

Dante and Mephiston, walking through the spaceport’s shadows to inspect repairs to their own fleet, stopped at the memorial. What they saw defied their notions of Batavian coldness.

At the foot of the carved name, a small altar of rough stone had been improvised. Before it, kneeling in corrosive dust, was a civilian family. A frail old woman in a worn shawl; two adults with hands marked by weld burns; and four skeletal children. They laid small offerings on the altar: dark forge bread, a glass flower carved from Incus-Gravis desert sand, and a small bottle of purified water. All of it came from the family’s meager resources—nothing diverted from the Tithe, for that would offend the Law.

The citizens wept. Not with the reverential fear of losing an untouchable god’s protection, but with the raw pain of losing one of their own household.

Heavy industrial footsteps echoed behind the Blood Angels. Veteran **Daxtos**, Shield-Brother who had fought in the **same Vexillation** as Haldor. His armor was dented and carbonized. He stopped beside the angels, orange lenses fixed on the family.

— It is an anomalous sight — Dante commented, voice subdued so as not to disturb the civilians. — We are revered as demigods or feared. But we are not wept for as intimate mortals. Why do they weep for one of you this way?

— The **Execratio** in our blood tries to turn us into beasts — Daxtos explained through the helm’s filters, stating a purely clinical fact, as Drusus would. — We are stabilized through mathematics and terror. But there is an irrevocable side effect to our lupine anomaly. An atavistic biological instinct of preservation. We suffer from **imprinting**.

Mephiston narrowed his pale eyes. — Bond of adoption. You bond to mortals.

— We are forged to protect the whole frontier, Lord of Death — Daxtos answered. — But our minds, pressed by flesh’s failure, often choose focal anchors so we do not go mad in the dark. One citizen. One child. One family. When a Batavian forms the bond with a lineage, they cease to be “civilian population.” They become our pack. Haldor protected Incus-Gravis, but his heart beat to ensure that old woman and her grandchildren could sleep in peace. He adopted them.

Dante felt the revelation weigh on his shoulders. Noviomagus Prime’s sterile coldness was not the product of dead hearts; it was the camouflage needed to protect a silent, violent, fiercely possessive love.

Daxtos asked no permission. The metal colossus advanced, his steps shaking the ground, until he stopped directly behind the kneeling family.

When the civilians noticed the giant shadow and the gray-iron armor, the adult children instinctively stepped back. But the old woman did not. She rose with difficulty, lifted her wrinkled, tear-washed face, and looked straight into Daxtos’s orange lenses.

— He did not return for his shift, Lord Daxtos — the old woman whispered, voice trembling with cold and pain.

— His watch on this side of the line is ended, ma’am — Daxtos replied. His mechanical voice dropped to a frequency that simulated the maximum softness a war synthesizer could manage. — He is the foundation now.

What followed froze Dante and Mephiston. The old woman took a stumbling step forward and threw her arms around Daxtos’s enormous ceramite breastplate. She embraced the colossus.

For any other Astartes, that contact forced by an unauthorized civilian would provoke recoil or revulsion. But the Batavian understood the physics of pain. Daxtos bent his hydraulic knees, ceramite knee striking the deck with the sound of rock settling, reducing his formidable 2.5-meter height so as not to intimidate her. With surgical precision calculated not to crush such fragile ribs, Daxtos wrapped his huge arms around the old woman, returning the embrace with perfect economy of movement.

He was the Wall. And the Wall embraced back.

— His blood runs no more — Daxtos told the woman and the children, who now approached to touch the edges of his pauldrons. — But I assume my brother’s watch. His pack is now my pack. Haldor’s gaze rests on your lineage from that wall, and my blade ensures you will not see dark days while I breathe. We honor the Wall.

— We honor the Wall — the children repeated in a whisper, like an amen.

At a safe distance, Dante crossed his arms over his golden breastplate. Initial prejudice against the Bataves’ monstrous look and grim tactics had been burned away.

— They are crude, ugly, and brutal, Mephiston — the Regent of Baal murmured, voice choked with unprecedented respect. — Their armor is stripped of glory and their galaxy stripped of light. But, by the Golden Throne… may the Emperor forgive me if I ever doubt that these monsters are the purest of all His angels.

## INTERLUDE: THE SHIELD OF LOGIC (The Audit of Fire and Blood)

**(Restricted Record: Primus Sector Ruins, Incus-Gravis | Observers: 9th and 18th Legions)**

Dust on Incus-Gravis did not fall with snow’s melancholy softness; it plummeted under the planet’s punitive gravity, scouring ceramite like ground glass. Captain Leonas of the Sons of Sanguinius wiped dark, thick blood from his crimson sword’s blade while he watched the ruined horizon.

A few meters away, a **column** of the *Cohors Batavorum* marched back toward the heavy transports. They moved in absolute, terrifying silence. Only the deep hum of oversized servomotors and the unison thud of adamantium boots on cracked permacrete. No victory song. No glorification of the fallen. Only blood-smeared gears returning to the toolbox after the shift’s end.

Leonas tightened his sword’s hilt, a tension line breaking the angelic perfection of his features.

— They disturb me, N’Keln — said the Sanguinary Angel. His voice, though filtered by the helm’s grille, carried the stressed nobility typical of his line.

The Salamander Captain, kneeling near a heap of industrial rubble, stopped what he was doing. Faithful to Nocturne’s doctrine and the tactical exchange that had brought him to Noviomagus’s frontier, N’Keln was not resting; with a precision torch mounted on his forearm, he finished welding the mechanical leg of a civilian load-servitor damaged in the crossfire.

N’Keln’s eyes, red and bright as pure coals on charcoal skin, lifted to follow the Bataves’ gray march.

— They are sons of Noviomagus, cousin. Cold is the only language their forge allows — the Salamander’s voice was a warm, comforting rumble, the exact opposite of the frozen dead scene around them.

— It is not the cold that disturbs me, N’Keln. It is hypocrisy. — Leonas sheathed his sword with a harsh click, turning to his ally. — Listen to how they speak. *“The Emperor dictates, we comply.”* *“We hold.”* The damned **Axiom of Reason**. They pride themselves on being pure tools. They claim to have purged emotion, grief, organic failure. They say there is no faith in that Chapter, only blind duty, pragmatism, and mathematics.

Leonas pointed his red gauntlet at the Batavian column’s rear, where High Chaplain Varro passed. The monstrous figure in black armor and wolf-skull helm exuded an aura of clinical terror, wreathed in acidic smoke from metal censers that smelled not of myrrh but of motor oil.

— But I watched them before battle, Salamander. I saw them in the southern trench. I saw a Batavian trace that superpredator’s rune, the Wolf of the Tides, on his chest plates. I saw them murmur focus orders to the wind as if Aethelgard’s ashes heard them from the other side of the cosmos. And when the mortal *Auxilia*’s defenses nearly broke in the Primus Sector, I saw Lord Castellan Alaric von Helis.

The Salamander shut off the torch. The flame’s glow died, but the heat of his presence remained. He smiled slightly, a micro-gesture of understanding. — You saw the Chapter Master in fury?

— I saw him… irrational — Leonas corrected, lowering his voice as if he feared offending the war spirits that dwelt in that Wall. — The line was collapsing. Any logical commander, any pure devotee of the *Codex Astartes*, would have pulled the mortals to safe positions and ordered saturation bombardment on the lost position. That would be perfect mathematics. But Alaric did not fall back. He broke formation. He and the beast fused to his armor dove alone into the enemy tide to draw all heavy fire, ensuring the human militia was not crushed in retreat.

Leonas shook his head, awestruck. — There was emotion there, N’Keln. There was brutal protection, an… organic desperation under all that Dreadnought steel. And yet, if you ask him, if you question that **Triumvirate** of mutilated faces, they will say with dead eyes it was only a “friction calculation” or a “logistic recalibration.” They lie.

N’Keln rose. His heavy emerald-green armor creaked under Incus-Gravis’s punitive gravity. The Nocturne giant walked to the Sanguinary Angel and placed his vast, still-smoking hand on Leonas’s shoulder.

— You and I, Leonas… we were forged in fire, openly. Your fire is blood, art, the dramatic sacrifice of your Angel. Mine is the furnace, the shield, the tear shed for those who burn. When we love humanity, we let them see our shine. We embrace their fragility.

The Salamander turned his face to the horizon. In the distance, the colossal silhouette of Alaric von Helis boarded his *Thunderhawk*’s ramp, the heavy *Mantle of Providence* swaying under ignition turbines.

— But the Iron Wolves do not have that luxury, cousin — N’Keln continued, voice heavy with old wisdom. — The curse in their blood turns them into chained beasts. Their universe is an absolute void of acid rain and loss. The frozen reason they preach so hard is not a lie; it is the containment wall they build so madness does not leak out and drown them.

N’Keln crouched and gathered a handful of Incus-Gravis’s cutting ash, letting radioactive soot run through his ceramite fingers’ cracks.

— You call them hypocrites because you see repressed grief and ritual inside the mathematics, Leonas. But the encrypted superstitions, the refusal to fall back, the human shield they make for the mortals of their frontier… that is not hypocrisy. It is the purest need for stasis. If Alaric von Helis admits, for even one millisecond, how much he and his Bataves love the fragile lives they protect… if they accept the weight of that affection… their hypertrophied hearts would shatter under loss’s gravity.

The wind howled through the ruins, carrying the sound of Batavian engines climbing skyward.

— They suffer from what they call **imprinting**, — N’Keln finished. — They adopt mortals. And so as not to die of grief every time a mortal falls, they pretend to be only tools. They pretend to be unbreakable machines, purely so they can survive the weight of their own humanity.

Leonas watched the Bataves’ transports lift off, orange propellant fire cutting the gray ash blizzard, leaving behind a saved world they would never call victory.

For a long moment, the Sanguinary Angel, aware of the Black Rage curse running in his own veins, envied Alaric von Helis’s iron shield. He wondered whether, at the end of all things, at the universe darkness’s threshold, the kindest, holiest lie an Astartes could tell himself was exactly that: to claim he felt absolutely nothing, while pouring every drop of his blood so others could keep feeling.

## EPILOGUE: THE PACT OF BLOOD AND IRON

**(Restricted Record: Primus Spaceport, Incus-Gravis | Joint Force Demobilization)**

Incus-Gravis’s cutting wind swept the spaceport’s vast ferrocrete aprons, carrying corrosive soot that clung to armor like a physical reminder of the dead world. The Ninth Legion’s transports’ immense plasma engines were already warming, emitting a high whine that warped the air around red-and-gold fuselages.

A few meters from the flagship *Bloodcaller*’s embarkation ramp, the two command fronts stood face to face for tactical dismissal.

On one side, the tragic nobility of Blood: Commander Dante and Chief Librarian Mephiston, flanked by their Honor Guard. On the other, Iron’s architectural repulsion: Lord Castellan Alaric von Helis, massive in his *Castra-Lupus* chassis and adorned with the *Triple Mantle*, flanked by Apothecary Drusus’s clinical silence and Chaplain Varro’s cadaverous shadow.

There was no exchange of banners. The *Codex Batavorum* repudiated celebrations and campaign trophies as logistic inefficiencies. The war had ended, airspace was secure, and duty dictated immediate separation of assets.

Yet Dante broke the protocol’s cold symmetry.

The Regent of Baal raised his hands to his gorget and, with a depressurization hiss, removed his golden helm. The commander’s impossibly ancient face was exposed to Incus-Gravis’s toxic air. But he did not stop there. With deliberate motions, Dante unlocked his right forearm’s clasps and removed his own ceramite gauntlet, handing it to one of his guards.

He stepped toward the **Triumvirate** and extended his right hand. Naked. Unprotected. A piece of fragile mortal flesh offered to Noviomagus’s war machine.

Drusus logged the act in his optics with purely statistical confusion. Exposing an unarmored limb in Incus-Gravis’s atmosphere was an uncalculated biological risk. But Alaric von Helis understood the gesture’s geometry. Dante was not offering a military handshake; he was offering the most absolute, vulnerable, irrational trust.

The Lord Castellan’s heavy servomotors whined as he raised the Hand of Judgment. The colossal adamantium gauntlet, whose fingers ended in thick Aethelgard Claws meant to shear siege tanks, closed around the Angel’s bare hand. Alaric did not squeeze. Operating with zero-latency precision, he calibrated his monstrous gauntlet’s hydraulic pressure to apply exactly the force needed to firm the grip, without fracturing a single metacarpal of the old warrior.

Dante looked at the orange lenses gleaming under the Castellan’s lupine snout.

— I saw your silent mourning on this world, Lord Castellan — Dante said, rough voice carrying an epiphany’s weight. — I saw how your beasts protect the weak with a love so brutal and absolute it humbles our own nobility. You carry hell inside so mortals need not see it outside.

The Blood Angels’ Commander tightened his grip on the iron gauntlet.

— If night falls finally on the Imperium Nihilus — the Regent continued, framing an oath that transcended any Administratum bureaucracy. — And if Baal’s defenses are besieged to the breaking point… know I will not hesitate to call your steel.

A sub-millimeter contraction tightened Alaric’s human jaw beneath the helm. The **Axiom of Reason** allowed no false promises, boasting, or empty honor. A summons would require a friction calculation, fleet redeployment, and the expenditure of thousands of lives and tons of promethium. And still, the Anchor’s answer was immediate and unshakable.

— And Noviomagus’s colossi will answer, Commander — Alaric’s voice echoed, low and metallic through *vox* emitters, sounding not like a heroic promise but the statement of a physical law. — Gravity pulls, powder burns, and the *Cohors Batavorum* marches. It is a mathematical constant.

Dante nodded, an imperceptible smile of respect marking his dried lips. He released the monster’s gauntlet.

Mephiston bowed his head a hair to Varro and Drusus, tactical acknowledgment of the lethality those death-priests and death-healers represented, before following his Commander back aboard ship.

The *Bloodcaller*’s heavy hatches closed. Within minutes, the Ninth Legion’s fleet rose into ash-stained skies, pushing smoke aside with propellant fire until they vanished into black orbit.

On Incus-Gravis’s soil, the **Triumvirate** did not wave. They did not salute the skies, nor watch allied ships’ contrails. The transaction was sealed. Friction had been paid.

Without a single order spoken, Alaric von Helis pivoted on his heels, Viggo’s heavy pelt and the Batavian Wolf’s lashing the poisoned air. The Chapter Master turned his back on the stars and began marching back toward the macroforges’ shadows, followed in perfect sync by his two generals.

The imperfect monsters returned to the darkness, ready to hold the door once more.
