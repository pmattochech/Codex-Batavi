## I. Mud Worn Into Greaves

South rotation had arrived with mud worn into greaves like scripture worn into thumbs — clay that remembered each step and refused polish, the kind of dirt that outlasted claims of *forward march* on parchment. The Batavi column had crossed the same ridges twice on purpose, punishing prediction until even stalker cadence looked bored; mud did not care about doctrine. It clung. It testified.

When the command tent rose for the Quarter close, pegs bit into ground that still tasted like vein-mud from the wreck-beaches, and the canvas smelled of mildew, lamp oil, and the metallic honesty of wet plasteel — war’s perfume, bottled.

Inside, the lamps hissed. Condensation traced the ridge poles; officers pretended not to wipe their brows because sweat could be read as doubt. The hololith woke as it always did at these closes, throwing light too clean for the memories that paid for it — columns assembling in air, numbers hanging like gallows built for conscience.

**Kaell**’s shroud was not named on the agenda. He occupied the briefing anyway, the way silence takes weight when everyone agrees not to point. Young captains learned early that some ghosts were logistical: they did not rattle chains; they bent schedules. Older sergeants learned that logistics could haunt harder than chains, because a schedule was a promise, and broken promises outnumbered broken manacles.

Alaric stood helmed before the projection as if ceramite were the only skin strong enough for what numbers would do. His shoulders were squared not for parade but to keep the hololith’s city of absence from leaning on mortals more than it already had — because mortals in the front row would break if the Castellan broke first, and breaking was a luxury the wall could not afford.

Behind him the Triumvirate held their usual geometry: **Drusus** with slate and scanner, fingers resting on surfaces as if surfaces could be calmed; **Varro** with censers cold enough to dare anyone to confuse prayer with comfort, *Codex* death-skull catching lamplight in shallow gleams that refused to become a face; **Kadmos** with the forge stare that turned spare parts and deadlines into verdict. They were not audience. They were ballast — the living argument that the wall had a spine even when maps pretended war was only vectors.

Nothing in the formal packet referenced Pier Nine’s truncated capture or the **LIVE-CAPTURE / TYPE A** buffer Drusus kept sealed under a second lock. The Chapter’s captains were not ready to hear *instruction* speculations while they still flinched at cages and at children who had called Astartes honest; some truths needed a smaller room than a Quarter close, and Drusus had decided — without asking permission he would not have granted himself anyway — that the smaller room would be Triumvirate first, Strategium second, captains third.

A young captain — **Rothmar**, still wearing his first company’s kill-mark like jewelry — leaned forward when the hololith woke, hungry for a war that looked like the posters in the Strategium anteroom. He would learn, in Incus ash and gulf teeth, that posters were **lies with better stationery**; tonight he only learned that some numbers did not appear on screens at all.

— Transhuman band — he began.

Alaric turned his helm one degree. One degree was enough to end sentences.

Rothmar sat back. The lesson cost him nothing but pride, and pride, on Aethelgard, was cheaper than blood only until it wasn’t.

## II. Mausoleum Built From Integers

Still, when the Apothecary stepped to the lectern line his voice carried an extra grain of stone, as if his throat had been scored by the comparison he had almost proven on an auspex overlay and refused to waste on gossip. He did not clear his *vox*-grille. He did not offer preamble. Casualty ledgers do not weep; they sum.

He read the First Quarter close in High Gothic scrubbed to the bone because poetry belonged to survivors, and the Apothecarion owed the living the insult of clarity.

**Transhuman dead** remained **classified** — not secrecy chasing glory, but procedure tempered by mercy: kin would hear totals from the Triumvirate before *vox*-net folklore minted martyrs or pinned monsters with the wrong faces. The number itself sat in the air like a weight only Astartes could lift without flinching; mortals heard the shape of it in what was *not* said, the way you hear a missing stair in a dark staircase.

Captains stopped rearranging their feet. The smallest motions of a Quarter close — throat clearing, slate tapping, the nervous slide of a thumb along a bolt pistol’s grip — vanished while the classified line hung, because flinching in front of mortals was a currency Alaric did not spend cheaply. Liaisons learned, again, that there were rooms inside the Imperium where even asking the shape of a secret could cost a career; they wrote nothing down and hated themselves for feeling relieved.

**Mortal dead** landed in the cruel band between *too many* for a conscience trained on efficiency and *too few* for a sector trained on indifference — the arithmetic jurisdictions deploy when tithe collectors need moral excuses they can file in triplicate. Drusus did not soften the band. Softening was a different department, one that wore robes and never knelt in mud.

He let the hololith stack columns into a city you could walk through if you hated yourself — streets of empty bunks, windows of missing IFF tags, avenues of *non-combat environmental* that meant mud had eaten men the way mud always ate men, quietly, without issuing receipts. After a while the projection seemed less like data and more like architecture: a mausoleum built from integers.

**Seventy-two** appeared once in the mortal band as a subtotal the scribes had tried to file under *tide return* until Kadmos had threatened to drown them in their own ink. The line stayed. The tide’s arithmetic remained honest.

**Void dead** wore names the mirror-route annex still quarreled over — **Pryce**, **Yew**, *Promise of Tithing* — while blame shuttled between Navy, Genetor, and Navigator factors like a hot shell nobody wanted caught holding. Drusus let the names hang. Names were not arguments; they were wounds with spelling. When he paused on that line, the tent’s humidity seemed to thicken, as if the hololith’s light had learned to sweat.

**Kadmos** stared until staring became work, face neutral in the Batavi way that meant *I am still listening* even when listening felt like being buried alive in someone else’s math. **Varro**’s stillness beside him grew louder the longer the tent avoided gratitude, as if thanking the Emperor for arithmetic were the wrong kind of blasphemy; his *Codex* death-skull did not turn toward the light, because turning would have looked like seeking comfort from numbers.

**Sergeant Kessler** sat third row with hands bandaged from Pier cordon labor and firebase shame — fabric tight, knuckles dull beneath wraps, the small indignities of a body that kept working even when the mind wanted to abdicate. Her eyes stayed forward like someone who had decided not to die of embarrassment because deaths cheaper than hers still needed a mind awake enough to count them. When Drusus paused on mirror-route casualties her jaw flicked once — not theater, *tally* — the small fidelity of a sergeant who could not unlearn names once she had carried them through mud.

Death’s section ended; **continuation** began, and the tent exhaled the way a lung exhales after holding breath too long.

## III. Double-Stamp Manifests

**Valerius** stepped to the slate and pinned miracles to paper in a dry voice that made miracles sound like duty: rations by tonnage, propellant by crate, medicae throughput measured in wounded per hour and the cruel conversion of *hour* into *survival*. He spoke repair torques on auspex arrays **Threx**’s people had *calibrated* twice and stolen honesty from once — language mild enough for a Quarter close, sharp enough to make Mechanicus liaisons shift in their seats.

— Marine lifts — he said — remain **priority** on south embarkation. Auxilia holds **secondary** but not **disposable**. Double-stamp manifests where admiralties pretend *swapped* is clerical humor.

Alaric listened the way a wall listens to scaffolding — not romance, necessity — because without staff arithmetic war turned to theater, and theater killed the way plays killed when crowds forgot where the exits were.

Orbit conceded what mud had delayed. Through cog-channels and Navy pressure that left admiralty throats raw from swallowed pride, ***Lex Ignis*** received a fresh dig warrant into **Incus-Gravis** strata — hive labyrinths where labor and faith braided tighter than power cable, where output quotas could hide a second pulse if nobody listened for two heartbeats at once.

Threx appeared on a secondary pane, aug-eyes bright, smile gleaming augmetal even through grainy relay.

— Compliance creates *opportunity* — he said, voice scrubbed of everything but appetite.

Alaric’s stare could have tempered steel.

— Opportunity kills — the Castellan answered — if misread as invitation.

Threx bowed by microns — insult and prudence sharing a spine.

— Careful digging — he promised — with the candor of a man who knew everyone heard the lie and would sign anyway.

The pane dimmed. The tent did not relax. Relaxation was for cities that believed they were safe.

A Navy liaison at the back — **Lord-Captain Varche**’s proxy, spine stiff with borrowed dignity — asked whether double-stamp manifests would *delay* spearhead solvency. **Valerius** answered before Alaric could spend hinge-voice on arithmetic that mortals mistook for cruelty.

— Solvency is not speed — Valerius said. — Solvency is **knowing which debt you are paying when the tide asks**.

The liaison wrote nothing down. Writing would have admitted fear. Fear, in admiralty rooms, was filed in private slates or not at all.

Drusus advanced the hololith one more notch — routes south, embarkation windows, the color-coding of Marine lifts versus auxilia holds — until the projection looked less like a mausoleum and more like a machine designed to move grief efficiently. Kadmos murmured a correction to a rally grid; Varro did not murmur; Alaric nodded once, the economy of motion that meant *yes* and *no* at once, and the captains copied the nod the way children copy adults when they do not understand the lesson but understand the danger of misunderstanding.

## IV. Two Wars With One Cord

At the tent flap rain worked percussion on Aethelgard’s eternal grudge. **Iria** stood half in and half out of the lamplight, watching night-birds flee the floodline — creatures too small for auspex and honest in their panic.

— Hunters withdraw when the world grows too loud — she said, neither quite to Alaric nor quite to herself. — Soldiers remain — because duty is a smell.

Auspex saw neither clean exodus nor pure remnant, only schism in patterns the Moon Hunt had filed as a single threat until Pier Nine proved otherwise: Type **A** and Type **B** no longer pretending one ecology — hunter and soldier twinned on the same hunt — grammar splitting into two dialects the Strategium would have to learn on separate ledgers.

Drusus heard Iria’s sentence and filed it without looking at her, because filing was how he admitted truth without granting it the power to derail a close. Truth, tonight, would travel in parallel: spoken in Needles salt, spoken in Apothecarion numbers, spoken in Castellan silence.

Kadmos pulled him aside before the flap dropped — channel narrow, voice flat.

— If hunters leave and soldiers stay — he said — we are fighting two wars with one cord.

— Then we cut the cord into two ledgers — Drusus answered — and pray the Chapter does not mistake ledger for **law**.

Alaric heard without turning. Hearing was enough. Answering would have made schism **policy** before Incus taught them what trust eaten from within felt like.

**Varro** stood at the tent’s dark edge, *Codex* death-skull catching lamplight wrong, and listened to Iria’s sentence settle into the room like ash — hunters withdraw, soldiers remain — without adding theology. Theology would have turned schism into **sermon**. He filed the shape of it instead: *two dialects; one cord; cut into ledgers* — margin only, Triumvirate eyes, sequence not prophecy.

## V. Seal on Deployment Order

They struck camp to the romance of logistics: pitons pulled, *vox*-relays spooled, mortals lifted onto transports with the careful brutality of shepherds who know sheep still matter when wolves are statistical.

**Drusus** sealed the Pier Nine buffer — **LIVE-CAPTURE / TYPE A** crossed out, *repeat capture required* still private — and did not brief captains. Briefing would have turned *instruction* speculations into **policy** before Incus taught them what trust eaten from within felt like. Triumvirate first. Strategium second. Captains third. Sequence, not prophecy. Someone cursed a stuck winch; someone else laughed once, harshly, and cut the laugh off as if laughter were a breach of discipline. Mud fell from greaves in clods, Aethelgard refusing full release, as if claiming partial ownership of anyone who had bled there.

Alaric did not say *We hold* — not yet, not as litany worthy of the word — and set his seal on the deployment order instead, the legal romance of continuing where signature performed oath without poetry. Wax hissed; smoke rose; the smell joined the tent’s older smells and made a single narrative of *we are leaving* and *we are not free*.

Kessler passed him on the duckboards, saluted sloppy-hard, and did not wait for return — because some salutes were not requests for acknowledgment; they were receipts.

Valerius watched the gesture and filed it among the small mercies that keep armies from rotting from the inside: a sergeant’s pride, a Castellan’s burden, a staff officer’s quiet decision to remember instead of optimize.

**Iria** passed the embarkation line with a tide chart folded into her cuff — correction for interval risk, same vaccine Kessler would later teach cables — and did not seek Alaric’s eyes. Seeking would have turned witness into **spectacle**. Alaric saw her anyway, periphery doing the work eyes did when helm law forbade the romance of face.

He nodded once. She nodded once. Two nods, no audience, enough receipt for a quarter that had closed in integers and opened in ash.

**Drusus** saw the exchange on a peripheral slate and did not log it. Some data corrupted when you filed it; some debts stayed honest only while they remained **unstamped**.

**Kadmos** posted embarkation cordon one meter deeper — geometry as insurance — because quarter close was integer that ate trust from inside and trust required witness routes only before inverted hive taught hooks.

**Merit** tried once to clip a recorder to tent hololith until **Valerius** stamped **NON-COMPLIANT** — witness routes only, no throughput dressed as quarter-close packages when Pier Nine trace still traveled south as sequence, not prophecy.

**Varro** listened to Type **B** cadence march inland without adding theology — *Codex* death-skull catching lamplight wrong — because theology would have turned schism into **sermon** before Q2 opened in kin-network grammar.

Rain worked the tent flap like a metronome Aethelgard refused to charge for. Somewhere inland, Type **B** cadence still marched — soldiers remaining while hunters withdrew, two dialects on one cord — and the quarter closed knowing Incus would teach them what trust eaten from within felt like when quotas sang in rounds and midwives counted beside throughput charts.

## VI. First Named Cord-Cut — Cross-File

Drusus entered **Kaell** into the Versibar appendix as **first named cord-cut** — not last — and cross-filed the entry to the sealed Pier Nine trace the way a clinician ties lesions to a single vector: not proof, *continuity*. The tap of his stylus sounded louder than it should have in the damp air. He closed the slate, then opened it again to confirm the cross-file had saved, because some fears were not spiritual; they were clerical.

He appended a one-line cross-ref to the mirror-route annex stub — *Sigma-Black; harmonic coupling watch; Incus ingress* — not as prophecy, as **sequence**: first mud, then glass, then hive ash, then whatever mathematics preferred when it stopped pretending to be weather.

**Kessler** passed the Kaell filing on peripheral hearing and did not ask questions. Questions would have turned correlation into **oracle**. She counted embarkation mouths instead — rebreathers, hands, canteens — because counting was the only vaccine that had never lied to her yet on beaches, in Ring tubes, on spines where silence zones would later price throats in chalk ticks.

Varro breathed a bind so low it might have been wind — *let what ends here not echo where we go* — and dipped his *Codex* death-skull a fraction as the flap dropped, sealing lamplight inside canvas and rain outside canvas, as if the world could be partitioned that cleanly. It could not. Everyone knew it. They partitioned anyway.

## VII. Braid — Not One War

Outside, the second quarter opened under roofs that pretended they were not mouths, and **Incus** waited to teach trust eaten from within while the Chapter carried Moon Hunt mud another hundred miles into a war that pretended cleanliness could follow if they marched fast enough.

Thunderhawks coughed heat into the storm. The tide, somewhere behind them, kept sorting debt the way tides always did — patient, thorough, indifferent to which quarter humans named on their calendars.

Alaric stood one minute longer on the duckboards where the tent had stood, boots still carrying firebase ash and Glass Rills grit in the same tread — proof the First Quarter had not been one war but a **braid**: bait, mirrors, cages, cords, ledgers, names that would not round down.

He looked south toward hive-light the auspex said was still days away and thought of Iria’s sentence — duty as a smell — and of Kaell’s laughter steaming in rain, and of forty-six void gaps that had taught them instrumentation could lie while mud remained literal.

The Moon Hunt was **closed** on paper. On Aethelgard’s skin it would linger like oil on the Black Fan: visible only when the light hit wrong, persistent when you wished it gone. Type **B** cadence still marched inland — soldiers remaining while hunters withdrew — schism filed in parallel columns because merge was how convoys choked.

## VIII. Second Syllabus Begins

He turned toward the embarkation line. The second syllabus had already begun.

**Rothmar** boarded a Thunderhawk with kill-marks still polished like jewelry and learned, in the first hour of south transit, that hololith columns did not include Pier Nine’s empty cage or Kaell’s laughter or the boy’s sentence — omissions that made posters look like **lies with better stationery**. An older sergeant — not Kessler; someone who had survived the seventy-two by counting mouths — told him to unpolish the marks before Incus ash found them.

— Ash — the sergeant said — doesn’t care whose god is prettier.

Rothmar wiped. The cloth came away gray. Montage had not begun yet, but the lesson had: **Shared Sweat** before slang, shame before compass, placement before virtue.

**Varro** walked the embarkation perimeter without speeches — *Codex* death-skull steady, censers cold — laying binds along choke points where mortals might bunch when fear found a shape. Bunching killed on beaches; it would kill in hive ash too. Prevention was chaplain work wearing logistics uniform.

**Drusus** checked Kaell’s baseline one last time on a slate he should not have opened in rain — correlation, not proof — and sealed it before captains could mistake rhyme for **oracle**. Sequence, not prophecy. Triumvirate seal. Witness routes only.

Inverted hive waited in ash and quotas. Output quota. Ring of teeth. Exanimus Choir still years away on annals, but Furor slope was already climbing in telemetry nobody yet dared name doctrine.

Threx had promised careful digging. Valerius had promised double-stamp manifests. Drusus had promised correlation under Triumvirate seal. Kadmos had promised two ledgers cut from one cord.

Boy had said *you keep cages too*. Kaell had taught firebase what friendly fire sounded like when it laughed.
