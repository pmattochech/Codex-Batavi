# Cassian Vorn — First Sword (expunged)

I saw Cassian Vorn before I heard his name, which is how it should be.

Names make men into stories. Stories invite mercy you cannot afford and hatred that feels clean because it has a shape.

Vorn was not a story. He was a function.

We had just buried our count in blood—five reduced to two—and the containment line behind us still spat gunfire into the dusk as if it could shoot the rot back into the Warp. Brann and I moved along the extraction flats with the kind of speed that looks calm until you try it. Helmet sealed. Vox disciplined. No unnecessary speech. No unnecessary glance at the bodies we left tagged in the sand.

That is what witness becomes when you are still alive: you learn to keep moving without pretending movement is victory.

We crested a low rise where the ground sloped toward a pit field and saw another fight unfolding in open ground.

It did not look like a battle.

Battles have churn. Crossfire. Misjudgments. Men dying in ways that look accidental because too many variables are in motion.

This looked like work.

Marines were down—some in *Prima* colors, some in plates that had been repainted over old heraldry, some stripped raw—arranged in a pattern that forced survivors to step where they were meant to step. Every movement channelled. Every choice narrowed.

In the center of it was a figure moving with deliberate slowness, as if he had all the time in the world because time itself had decided to favor him.

He carried a weapon that was too heavy for elegance. Not a duelist’s blade. A tool meant to end arguments, meant to break bone through armor when the bolter has already done the easy part.

He did not swing wildly.

He swung once, each time, and each swing accomplished what it was intended to accomplish.

When a Marine tried to retreat, the figure stepped into his path, not to chase, but to block. When a Marine froze—hesitation, wavering—the figure did not preach. He touched the Marine’s helm with the flat of his weapon, almost gently, and spoke a sentence I could not hear across the wind.

The Marine turned and killed his own brother.

That was when I understood the role.

Valerius was the magnet. The claim. The new faith that promised relief.

Cassian Vorn was the enforcement.

He was the part that made the faith usable.

Brann whispered his question in our private channel.

— Is he…

Brann did not finish. We did not have Drusus’s sealed words in our mouths yet. We had only the field reality: some bodies changed, some minds broke, and occasionally the two did not align in the way you prayed they would.

I watched the figure’s posture.

His mass was wrong. Not in the way a Versibar is wrong—towering, obscene, unmistakable. Wrong in subtler ways: a thickness in the shoulders that suggested the body was beginning to re-write itself. A looseness in the neck that suggested the armor no longer sat on human geometry.

And yet his movements were clean.

He paced himself like an officer on inspection.

He was lucid.

I hate writing that word because it sounds like a comfort. It is not. Lucidity inside a monster is not "self-control." It is choice.

Choice is what makes a butcher more terrifying than a beast.

— He’s doing it on purpose.

— Yes.

We watched him for thirty seconds, which is an eternity in a theater that kills by the minute. In those thirty seconds, he did not look at the sky, did not look at the pits, did not look at the horizon.

He looked only at people.

And the people he looked at stopped being people.

They became instruments.

The figure turned, and for a moment the light caught his lenses in a way that made them seem too bright. Too clean. As if whatever corruption had latched onto him had chosen to preserve the part that could assess and decide.

Brann’s gauntlet tightened on his bolter.

— We should shoot him.

— At this range?

Brann hesitated. He knew the answer. Bolter rounds can kill a Marine. They can kill a monster too. They can also fail, and a failure at this distance is not a tactical error. It is a sentence you hand to the enemy.

I shifted the halberd in my grip and felt the hook’s weight answer.

— He’s killing loyalists.

— He’s converting — I corrected.

— He’s making doubt too expensive to keep.

We moved down the slope, using derelict rails and broken crane ribs as cover. The closer we got, the clearer the pattern became.

Vorn was not chasing everyone.

He was selecting.

He left the convinced alone. Devotion does not need enforcement.

He hunted the wavering.

Every time a brother hesitated to execute another brother, Vorn appeared. Every time a brother’s gun drifted off target because his mind tried to remember a shared campaign, Vorn stepped into his peripheral vision.

Then the brother fired.

Not because Vorn threatened him.

Because Vorn made the alternative unbearable.

I understood, in a cold clarity that made my mouth dry, that this was the true battlefield.

Not Malakor’s flats. Not the Cicatrix pressure. Not even Valerius’s claim.

The true battlefield was the moment inside a Marine’s mind where he decides whether duty can still be regulated without becoming devotion.

Vorn reached a wavering brother and spoke again—still too far for me to catch the words.

The wavering brother looked down at his own hands, as if the hands could tell him what he had become.

Then he raised his bolter and executed the brother beside him.

Vorn did not nod. He did not praise. He did not perform triumph.

He simply stepped past the corpse as if the corpse were a box ticked.

— How many has he done?

I did not answer with a number because I did not have it. Witness does not invent completeness.

I answered with the truth that mattered.

— Enough to make the rest possible.

At the edge of the pattern, Vorn finally looked up.

Not at Brann.

At me.

He saw the halberd.

He saw the stance.

He saw the one thing in this theater that did not fit his conversion math: a brother still regulating himself without seeking relief in devotion.

His vox opened.

— Two left.
— Two left — like an auditor reading a ledger aloud.

Brann stiffened beside me. He raised his bolter.

Vorn’s voice remained steady.

— One is enough.

I felt, in that moment, the true shape of his threat.

He did not need to kill us all.

He only needed to reduce witness to a single voice—because a single voice can be called delusion, can be called grief, can be called a personal vendetta. One voice can be merged into a story.

Two voices are harder to erase.

Five voices become doctrine.

I stepped forward and let Vorn see that I understood.

— You are not the Legatus.
— You are not the Legatus — I told him, repeating the line I had said before, because repetition is how you build a wall inside your own head.

Vorn’s helmet tilted a fraction. The gesture was almost curious.

— No.

— I am what makes him durable.

The wind shifted. The sky flickered green. Somewhere behind us, a gunline screamed and then went quiet, as if the theater itself had decided a paragraph had ended.

— Corbec.

He meant: *we should withdraw*. He meant: *we can’t win this now*. He meant: *we are two and this is a butcher.*

He was not wrong.

But the math was not about whether we could win today.

The math was whether we could survive long enough to keep witness intact.

If Vorn lived, he would keep doing what he did—selecting the wavering, making doubt expensive, reducing witness by attrition until only devotion remained.

We could not let him become a persistent function of the theater.

I tightened my grip on the halberd.

— Service demands reason. Reason directs service without blind submission. Duty upheld through regulation of self — and this time I did not stop at the first clause.

Vorn laughed once, softly, as if I had recited a child’s lesson in a room full of knives.

— Good.

— Then regulate this.

He stepped forward.

I stepped to meet him, already knowing the truth that would later become a clean sentence in an archive.

This duel was inevitable.

Not because fate wanted it.

Because the theater demanded it.
