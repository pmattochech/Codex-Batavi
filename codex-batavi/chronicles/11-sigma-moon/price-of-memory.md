# THE PRICE OF MEMORY

*(568.M42 — Noviomagus Prime / Luna Sigma)*

---

## I. THE NEWS

The transmission came through at 03:47 of the work cycle, encoded in *Carmine* protocol — the channel reserved for events that could not wait for dawn.

Elara Solis was eighteen and asleep in the room Martha had decorated with star charts when the vox chime woke her. She took the handset before the second signal.

The voice was a First Vexillatio sergeant she knew by name:

— Miss Solis. The Castellan is in the *Apothecarion.* — A pause. — He was taken off the field.

She stayed quiet two seconds. Not shock — calculation. *Taken off the field* was not the same as *dead*. It was Batavian lexicon for *survived, but cost was high.*

— Prognosis?

— Chief Apothecary says data suggest recovery.

*Suggest.* Drusus did not use that word unless there was real margin of uncertainty.

— I’m on my way. — She closed the handset and sat on the bed edge exactly five seconds. Then stood.

---

Tobias was in the kitchen when she came down, as if he knew — or as if he had not slept properly that night. He had a mug of tea and the face of someone who had calculated the news before hearing it.

— You were called — he said. Not a question.

— He’s alive. — She took her coat from the hook. — Drusus said data suggest recovery.

Tobias was still a moment. Then rose from the chair with the usual weight of one who had carried bricks for decades.

— I’m going with you.

Elara stopped. There were a thousand tactical reasons to say no. Tobias was civilian, old for the trip, no formal role on Luna Sigma.

She looked at him — at the face that had taught Alaric to hold a child without crushing, at the hands that had carved the wooden sword she had raised before a Primarch at eight.

— You need half an hour to pack.

— Five minutes — said Tobias, and went to the room.

---

## II. THE MOON

Luna Sigma was not built for visits.

It was an industrial extraction moon — **adamantium**-class ore bodies, **plasteel** feedstock, and promethium fuel nodules pulled from rock guts by servitor shifts and Imperial penal-labor squads. No plazas, no markets, no children. Mining shafts, drain pipes, cargo hangars, hab-blocks with basement architecture. The sky — if the term applied — was Noviomagus’s gray belly, huge and low, filling half the horizon like a wall of petrified cloud.

Functional population had been evacuated forty-eight hours before invasion on the Lord-Castellan’s order, when **Batavian Cipher** Librarians raised the alert. Those who remained could not be moved in time: lobotomized servitors without autonomous evacuation capacity, maximum-security cell prisoners protocol did not authorize freeing, essential techs keeping plasma reactors below collapse threshold.

Elara did not know how many had survived the battle.

No one told her.

---

The transport set down in the secondary hangar — the main one had been destroyed — and she walked the stretch between landing pad and improvised *Apothecarion* with Tobias beside and a Batavi sergeant ahead. The corridor smelled of burned promethium and plasma cautery.

In outer yards Batavi teams collected World Eaters dead. They were huge even dead — blood-crimson armor, faces behind open helms showing Butcher’s Nail architecture in the skull. None of the collectors spoke. The work was mechanical and silent, like men processing something too large to process aloud.

Tobias looked at the bodies and said nothing.

Elara neither.

---

## III. THE APOTHECARION

Drusus met her at the medical facility entrance — an industrial parts depot hastily converted — helm sealed, posture as always: still, clinical, no warmth.

— Miss Solis. — A barely perceptible head tilt. — Master Tobias.

— How is he? — said Elara.

— Regenerative coma. Biology is working. — A pause. — Lost right arm below elbow. Four implanted organs in temporary failure. Extensive fractures to the left hemithorax. Regeneration processes anomalous — no documented precedent. He should wake. Estimate: sixty to one hundred days.

— What happened out there?

The pause was 0.4 seconds. For Drusus, an eternity.

— Operational details are under restriction protocol — he said. — The Inquisitor in the field is competent authority for external communiqués.

Elara looked at him.

She had known Drusus since before she could read. She knew what *“competent authority for external communiqués”* meant from his mouth: *don’t ask further.*

— Understood — she said. — May I enter?

---

Alaric lay on a monitoring platform at the room’s center, wired to more sensors than she had seen on any patient. *Castra-Lupus* had been removed — what remained was stacked in fragments in a corner, shattered ceramite and fused servos. Without armor he was only a man too large for the world he lived in, breathing slow and steady, left side of the chest covered in dermal-regenerative dressings.

Where the right arm should have been was a pale pink tissue bulge fist-sized. Growing.

It was the first time she had seen him genuinely unconscious.

She had grown up with the idea Alaric was the fixed point — the thing that did not move while the universe reorganized around. Seeing the Wall on a medical platform, smaller without the chassis, was information the brain took time to process correctly.

She pulled the nearest chair and sat beside him.

Tobias stayed in the doorway, leaning on the jamb.

— You two need a minute — he said, low, and stayed outside.

---

## IV. THE INQUISITOR

He arrived on the third day.

Elara was in the *Apothecarion* outer corridor when she saw him come — a figure in dark gray cloak, escorted by two acolytes in work armor, no identifiable heraldry. He was tall for unmodified human, with the posture of decades of unquestioned authority. The brooch emblem was an eye — she recognized the general Inquisition symbol, not the specific branch.

He saw her and stopped.

— You are the Castellan’s pupil — he said. No question in the tone.

— Elara Solis — she said. — What happened on Luna Sigma?

A brief pause. The kind before a prepared answer.

— There was a Chaos incursion — he said. — The threat was neutralized with support from a specialized Inquisition force. Details are classified Sigillum Obscurus. — He looked at her with the expression of weighing an interlocutor and deciding the weight insufficient. — I can assure you the Lord-Castellan fought with distinction and the operation succeeded.

— What specialized force?

— Classified.

— How many losses in the contingent that remained on Luna Sigma during the battle?

— Information restricted to operational command.

— I am the—

— You — he said, with courtesy the sharpest form of interruption — are the Lord-Castellan’s civilian pupil. Your authority within Castra Vetera Subsector is recognized and respected by the Chapter. — A pause. — You are outside Castra Vetera Subsector.

Elara stood still.

It was technically correct. Luna Sigma lay within the Noviomagus system — but formal Chapter jurisdiction over unaccompanied civilians ended at the military installation border, and none of her informal titles converted to credential before an Imperium Inquisitor outside Chapter context.

She was eighteen and held no rank.

— When may I speak with the Lord-Castellan? — she said.

— When he wakes. — The Inquisitor bowed his head in formal courtesy and continued into the *Apothecarion.*

---

Tobias found her in the corridor two minutes later. He had heard from the entrance.

— Who was that man? — he asked.

— Inquisition — she said.

Tobias was quiet a moment.

— He lied to you?

She looked at the empty corridor where the Inquisitor had vanished.

— He told me nothing that could be proved a lie — she said. — Which is different.

---

## V. NINETY-TWO DAYS

She got nothing more.

Drusus answered medical questions with precision and tactical questions with “restriction protocol.” Tyvar, the auramite Custodes who stood guard beside the *Apothecarion*, answered no question — medical or tactical. First Vexillatio sergeants who had been on the field were polite and completely impenetrable. The Inquisitor left on the fifth day without speaking to her again.

Tobias returned to Noviomagus on the tenth.

— Martha needs me — he said, at the hangar. Then looked at her with eyes that always knew more than they showed. — You staying?

— Until he wakes.

Tobias nodded. Set his hand on her shoulder — mason-callused, warm and solid as always.

— That man’s too stubborn to leave anyone waiting — he said. — And when he wakes you’ll get at least one answer from him. May not be the one you want. But it’ll be honest.

She did not answer. But rested her head on his shoulder two seconds — exactly the time Elara Solis allowed herself to be eighteen.

Then he boarded the transport and she went back inside.

---

She spent the next eighty days learning the hole’s contour.

Things she knew for certain: Alaric had fought something on Luna Sigma’s open field. *Castra-Lupus* had been destroyed in a way requiring force vastly greater than a standard Astartes. The arm had been torn, not cut. Ceramite fragments showed overwhelming pressure impact, not blade or projectile. World Eaters — and she knew enough of Traitor Legions to recognize Khornate iconography on armor — could not do that damage to a Batavi Terminator in the open field alone.

What had been on the field with Alaric was not only a World Eaters fleet.

She did not know what it was. No one told her.

The Inquisitor had called “specialized Inquisition force” the unit that neutralized the threat. She knew the Inquisition had branches specialized in supernatural threats — any student of Imperial history knew that in general terms. But names, functions, protocols were classified at levels no civilian reached.

She was left with the hole’s contour and learned the shape of what she did not know.

---

## VI. THE HALF-TRUTH

On the ninety-second day, *Apothecarion* monitors logged change in Alaric’s cognitive parameters at 06:12.

Elara was in the chair beside the platform — same chair as the first day — when his eyes opened. Slit pupils. Crimson iris with greened rims. Staring at the ceiling.

— How long? — he said. Voice as always.

— Ninety-two days.

— Luna Sigma evacuation complete before impact?

— Yes.

— Losses in the contingent that stayed?

She had spent ninety-two days trying to learn exactly that.

— Forty-two Batavi on the battlefield — she said, with what she had confirmed from funeral records. — The rest, they didn’t tell me.

Silence.

Alaric turned his head toward her — slow movement, neck still stiff from three months immobility. He looked at her a moment.

— What do you know? — he said.

Not *what do you want to know?* A genuine question about the current state of her knowledge.

— I know something on the field destroyed *Castra-Lupus* in a way World Eaters couldn’t. — She kept tone neutral, technical — the tone she had learned from him. — I know an Inquisition force was involved in neutralizing the threat and details are Sigillum Obscurus classified. I know the field Inquisitor lied to me by omission. — A pause. — I know you’ll do the same now.

He was quiet three seconds.

— Corruption ran too deep — he said finally. — Excision was necessary. — A pause. — That is all I can tell you.

She had expected the half-truth. Had rehearsed mentally receiving it — frustration, urge to demand more.

What she felt when it came was different.

It was Alaric’s voice. The same weight as when he had said *“she needs help, but I don’t know what to do”* carrying her as a baby to the medical wing. The same calibrated weight of someone who measures each word not for evasion, but for responsibility.

He knew she knew it was half-truth.

And had chosen to say it that way anyway.

— Understood — she said.

Not because she believed it enough. Because she trusted it was the most he could give.

---

Alaric stared at the ceiling another moment.

— You stayed here all ninety-two days — he said. Not a question.

— Yes.

— Why?

She thought the honest answer. The chair that had become her post. The eighty days learning the hole’s contour. How she had spent nights rereading funeral reports of the forty-two Batavi dead on the field, trying to extract information from what was not written between lines.

— Because someone needed to be here when you woke — she said. — And there was no one else I trusted to do it.

Alaric was silent.

Then said, low, on internal vox channel — not to an army, not to a Vexillatio:

— *We hold.*

Elara Solis was eighteen and asked nothing more.

But when she left the *Apothecarion* an hour later, she went straight to the improvised *Strategium* and spent four hours cataloging everything she had observed on Luna Sigma over the last ninety-two days — ceramite fragments, impact patterns, enemy force composition, Inquisitor presence and protocol he had invoked.

It was not a report for anyone. It was a file for herself.

For the day she held rank enough to access what lay behind Sigillum Obscurus.
