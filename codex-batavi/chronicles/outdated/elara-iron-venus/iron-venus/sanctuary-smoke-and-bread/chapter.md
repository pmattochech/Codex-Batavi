# The Sanctuary of Smoke and Bread

Martha’s kitchen was no longer just a service room at the back of the *Castrum Ferrum*; over the decades it had become the geographic and spiritual heart of the Noviomagus system. What had once been a modest space of brick and mortar now bore the scars of a necessary, brutalist refit: industrial adamant beams, painted a soft cream to disguise their military origin, held up a ceiling raised three meters. The furniture had been adapted not for aesthetics but for tonnage. The central table was a slab of solid iron oak, reinforced with hydraulic pistons, able to bear the weight of demigods without groaning.

Outside, Noviomagus’s acid rain lashed the cathedral gargoyles, eating the metal. Inside, the air smelled of roast meat, dried herbs, and the faint ozone of armor reactors on standby.

The “royal pack” was gathered.

At the head of the table, on a bench that looked more like a reinforced stone throne, sat Alaric von Helis. He wore no helm. His face, carved by the deep scars of the *Alpha Anomaly* and the weight of the Silent Vigil, was bare. The lupine cast, the broad jaw, the crimson irises with greened rims — a sight that would make planetary governors tremble and Orks flee — was met with familiarity here, except by one pair of eyes.

Sara, Jonas’s wife, sat at the far end, both hands on her eight-month belly as if trying to build a physical barrier. She looked at the three giants in ceramite — Alaric, Drusus, and Varro — and despite Jonas’s reassurances, her instincts screamed danger. They were killing machines at the dinner table. They were monsters that smelled of promethium and old blood.

Little Leo, four years old, broke the tension by trying to balance a toy soldier on Alaric’s pauldron.

— Structural stability is compromised, Leo — murmured Alaric, his deep voice resonating like a smooth engine. He locked the arm joint to keep the toy from falling.

Sara flinched at the sound of his voice. Jonas felt the tremor in her hand and squeezed gently, but she did not relax. Her eyes met the red lenses of Varro’s **Executor** wolf-skull helm, and she held her breath.

— You fear for the integrity of the vessel, Sara — Drusus cut through the air, voice filtered through the white helm’s *vox*. The Apothecary was not looking at her but at biometric data on his visor. The mouth plate of his helm slid aside with a click so he could take nutrients. — Your cortisol levels are elevated. That is inefficient for gestation.

Sara swallowed. — It’s just… they’re so big, Jonas. What if… what if there’s an accident? What if war follows them here? — she whispered, but to Astartes hearing it was a shout.

Alaric stopped playing with Leo. He lifted his gaze, crimson eyes fixing on Sara. The kitchen fell absolutely silent. Even the older children stopped eating.

Varro rose. The shadow of his black armor swallowed the table. He walked toward Sara with the weight of a mountain, but stopped at a respectful distance.

— You are right to fear, daughter of Noviomagus — said the Chaplain, voice grave as stones rolling. — The universe is a place of hate. The void wants to freeze us, the xenos want to devour us, and Chaos wants to corrupt us. Death is the only common coin of the galaxy.

Varro raised his gauntleted hand, halting it in the air above Sara’s head in an old gesture of blessing.

— And that is why you are stronger than we are.

Sara blinked, confused. — Me?

— We are made of iron and gene-craft — Varro went on. — We were built to destroy. But you… you create life inside a universe that wants its death. That is the greatest act of defiance there is. The Emperor gave us the sword, but He gave you the miracle. The sanctity of a mother outranks any warrior’s, for you build the future we only guard.

Drusus nodded from across the table. — Biologically correct. The complexity of generating a sentient life exceeds the complexity of fabricating a bolter. You are the holiest factory in the Imperium. We are merely the walls that protect production.

Alaric, who had stayed silent, leaned forward. Firelight danced on his scars, softening the ferocity of his face.

— Sara — said Alaric. — Look at me.

She looked. And for the first time she did not see the monster. She saw the millennial weariness of a guardian.

— I have faced horrors that would make suns weep. I crossed hell and returned. And I did it all so there could be a place where someone like you could sit and fear only a strange dinner, not real monsters. — Alaric pointed at her belly. — What you carry is the reason I exist. While I draw breath, no shadow will touch this house. Chaos hates us because we dare to live. And you, Sara… you are living proof of our daring.

Sara felt tears run, but the fear had gone, replaced by overwhelming certainty. She nodded, took the water glass with a steady hand.

— Thank you, Lord Alaric.

— Uncle Alaric — corrected Martha, passing behind him and heaping more meat on the giant’s plate. — Eat, Alaric. You talk too much and chew too little.

Alaric obeyed at once, but his eyes shifted to Jonas. Martha’s eldest son, a man grown now, held the giant’s gaze.

— Jonas — called Alaric.

— Sir? — Jonas straightened in his chair.

— I read Factory 4’s audit reports. Your detection of the supply diversion last week was… surgical. You applied friction logic to the ledgers.

— I thought it necessary to secure the tithe, sir.

Alaric shook his head slightly. — The tithe matters. But I saw how you used your extra pay. You reinforced your mother’s house thermal insulation and secured the best supplements for Sara.

Alaric stretched his colossal hand across the table. Jonas, without hesitation, offered his own; the human hand vanished inside the Astartes ceramite gauntlet in a firm grip.

— You protect what is yours, Jonas. You provide. You watch. — Alaric squeezed the young man’s hand with pride, a rare gleam in his eyes. — Many men carry weapons and are cowards. You carry a pen and a home, and I see more iron in you than in many Guard officers. You are a good patriarch. Noviomagus is lucky to have your blood.

Jonas smiled, tired but dignified. — I learned from you, sir. The wall protects the root.

— Negative — corrected Alaric, releasing his hand and returning to his food. — I am only the watchdog. The root is your mother.

Martha, at the table’s true seat of honor, snorted but smiled. — Stop inflating each other’s egos and pass the potatoes. And Varro, stop scaring the girl with stories of heretics burning. Tell the one about the rabbit again.

— The rabbit that used asymmetric guerrilla tactics? — asked Varro, hopefully.

— That one.

Dinner went on. Sara’s fear had turned into a quiet certainty: there, among monsters who praised life and ate like starving men, her child would be born in the safest place in the galaxy.
