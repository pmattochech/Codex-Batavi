Haarken Worldclaimer had already spoken the world’s death into the vox.

The fortress-hive’s mid-curtain was a wound of Hab-block and gun-bastion, smoke stacked in industrial layers the way ash stacks after a manufactorum fire — thick, patient, unwilling to rise. Imperial Guard still clung to the fire-step: mortal, thin, useful. Their lasguns made a brittle glitter in the gloom. Some prayed. Some only breathed. Gloomtalon Raptors stooped through the breach-smoke on jump-packs, terror-first, the way Abaddon’s herald preferred it: break the will, crack the command spine, then let the Black Legion’s heavier meat arrive to own the corpse.

Haarken drove the Helspear’s point into a ferrocrete landing as if the hive were already a receipt.

— This world is claimed — he broadcast, joy under the grille. — In the Warmaster’s name. In the Despoiler’s coming. Kneel or be counted as ash.

A Guardsman unit broke along the left traverse, men becoming motion and discarded kit. Another held because holding was all that remained between them and the drop into the understack. Haarken liked both results. Fear was a tool. Claim was a tool. The spear was a signature — Helspear tasting rock, promising the Despoiler a world that had not yet finished dying.

Then the iron-grey arrived.

Not Navy angels in parade gilt. Not glittering Custodes. Astartes in industrial plate, wolf-echoes stamped without Fenris theater, moving as if ammunition were a moral law. They did not announce a Chapter. They did not answer the claim. They slotted into the Guard line the way a machine takes missing gears — bolt lanes measured, gaps sealed, mortal backs covered without sermon. Haarken had seen Astartes use humans as sandbags. He had rarely seen them treat mortals as parts that must keep working.

He wrenched the Helspear free of the ferrocrete — the claim-mark left a smoking puncture — and descended into the breach to read them properly.

Haarken’s Raptors laughed on the warband net.

— Grey mongrels — one hissed. — Warmaster’s bastards in loyal paint. XVI echo. Look at the wolves. Look at the siege temper. Horus left more sons than the Imperium admits.

It was a sweet misread. It fit the black gospel Haarken carried: the galaxy still belonged, in pieces, to the old Legion’s shadow. These unknowns looked like edge-warriors who understood necessary tyranny. Wolf marks. Industrial silence. A lord among them with a Corvus beak and chains rolled on one forearm — debt worn like kit, not jewelry.

The grey lord did not come as a duelist. He came as a gauge.

It was not a simple halberd and not a Champion’s show-glaive. Long shaft, officer mass, a vexillum panel lashed below the head: four fields Haarken could not read as heraldry he respected.

And the grey lord barely moved.

Where Raptors spent jump-fuel for terror angles, the iron-grey around him spent rounds like a forge spends bar-stock — counted, placed, intolerant of heroic waste. A Gloomtalon dove for a Guard officer and never reached him: two grey brothers stepped into the dive-lane and ended the scream with bolt and blade while their lord stood, polearm at rest, orange lenses reading the breach like a tally sheet. Another Raptor tried the high angle for the Corvus beak; a third grey caught him mid-stoop and put him into Hab-rubble hard enough to silence the pack’s laughter. The curtain that should have collapsed recalibrated — not because the gauge chased kills, because his force made chasing unnecessary.

Haarken felt annoyance before rage. Annoyance was rarer. Rage he understood.

— Name your master — he called across the smoke, Helspear wet with mortal blood from the fire-step’s edge. — If you are the Warmaster’s lost curs, kneel and be rewritten. If you are Throne-dogs, kneel and be ended. Either way, this hive is claimed.

The Corvus beak turned. Orange lenses. No chapter rant. Brothers to either side reset a heavy bolter cell with the boredom of men who treated reload as prayer, and dragged a wounded Guardsman behind ceramite without looking for thanks. The vexillum cloth stirred once in the breach-wind and hung again.

When the grey lord spoke, it was low, few words, the voice of a man who preferred manifests to myths.

— No.

Haarken laughed, and the laugh had edges.

— No is not an answer to the Despoiler’s herald—

— No claim — the beak said, as if correcting a false tally. — No kinship. No Warmaster. You are loud. You are early. You are wrong.

Around them the iron-grey tightened. Guard who had been one breath from flight found a wall of measured fire at their shoulders and discovered they could stand another minute. Haarken’s terror-arithmetic failed a line-item: mortals did not break cleanly when the grey treated them as parts that must keep working. The hive’s mid-curtain stopped sounding like a massacre and started sounding like a factory that had remembered its purpose.

A Raptor sergeant voxed the sweet lie again — Sons’ mongrels, Lupercal’s leftover dogs — because wolf and silence and edge-war made a pretty story for men who needed the past to justify the spear.

Haarken almost believed it.

Then he watched the chains on the grey lord’s right forearm catch the lumen-flash — rolled iron, maintained like an instrument — and the broken-cog mark at the gorget, and understood he was looking at something adjacent to treason without being treason’s friend. Not a Warmaster’s heir preening in loyal paint. Something that had already met a hunger like the Black Legion’s sales-pitch and refused to become the product. Haarken did not know the expunged name. He did not need it. He could smell the adjacency the way a herald smells a world that will not kneel on schedule: this was the response that survives after a brotherhood almost buys what Abaddon sells as destiny.

He came in on screaming pack-thrust, Helspear seeking ownership — because if the gauge would not move for Raptors, Haarken would make him move.

Only then did the Corvus lord shift.

Not a charge. A step. A turn of the long shaft. The head came up from rest as if a tool had been lifted from a rack, not a relic from an altar. Helspear met power-field with a hard, ugly ring that punched through the breach-noise. The grey lord gave Haarken nothing theatrical: a short bind, a shove that stole the jump’s leftover height, a single answering cut when the herald overcommitted and paid for poetry with balance. Around them his brothers kept the breach sealed so their lord did not have to become a whirlwind. They killed the angles. He killed the claim.

— You wear chains — Haarken mocked, spear locked to haft, trying to make the fight a proclamation loud enough for tomorrow’s scripture. — Badab learned chains. Colchis learned chains. The Warmaster learned—

— Debt — the grey lord cut, sparse, patient, intolerant of the waste in Haarken’s speech. — Not your collar. We carry what failed. We do not become it.

The clash was gap and gauge, not spectacle. Haarken fought as herald and Raptor-lord: leap, proclamation, spear-thrust meant to plant ownership in flesh. The Corvus lord moved only when the next second required it — then stopped again, polearm returning toward readiness, while iron-grey fire made sure the warband could not punish the stillness. When Haarken opened a wound in grey ceramite, the answer was not a roar. It was one corrective blow, then the line absorbing the rest as if their lord’s blood were simply another logistics problem already being solved.

Gloomtalons tried to peel the Guard off the fire-step with shrieking stoops and chainblade punctuation. The iron-grey — still without a name Haarken could steal — refused the peel without needing their lord to leave his mark-point. Bolt volume as moral law. A mortal sergeant who should have fled found himself still alive behind a grey pauldron, cursed once in a hive dialect Haarken did not bother to translate, and chose, absurdly, to reload.

Haarken drove for the claim again, voice wide for the vox-recorders that would feed Abaddon’s distant warrant.

— In the Despoiler’s name—

The long shaft came up once more — only as far as needed — and stopped the Helspear in a bind that was pure industrial contempt.

— Hold the cell — the beak told his own net, not Haarken. Then, to the herald, one more cut: — Your Warmaster is a story you use to steal worlds. We are not in it.

No kinship granted. No Lupercal flirtation. No Chapter name offered to fill Haarken’s archives. Only the industrial refusal of a seat that had already buried the brotherhood’s most famous hunger for that kind of glory — spoken by a lord whose force had already made most of the killing his unnecessary.

Haarken disengaged when the breach stopped being a claim and became a meat-bill. Raptors lifted on screaming packs, jump-wash kicking ash into the Guard’s eyes. He did not drive the Helspear into the ferrocrete again. The first puncture still smoked behind him like a cancelled deed; he would not honor these greys with a second signature. On the warband channel he spat new scar-names for the unknowns — gauge-wolves, chain-dogs, the ones who will not be claimed — and filed the warning Abaddon’s vanguard must carry if the Despoiler’s mouth was to remain useful:

They look like the Warmaster’s echo if you are drunk on old Legion poetry.  
They fight like a forge that already executed its own traitor and kept the receipt.  
Do not proclaim at them. Break their ammo math — or do not come early.

Behind him, on the mid-curtain, the Corvus lord was already still again. He checked a Guard ammunition crate with the same attention he gave dying brothers, counted what remained, and did not chase. Chasing was waste. The wall was the answer.

Haarken climbed back into black sky on a Raptor’s wash and the hive’s rising heat, and understood first contact as a herald hates to understand it.

The world was not claimed.  
The grey had denied the kinship his warband wanted.  
And the man in the beak had felt, somehow, like the living rebuttal to whatever brotherhood-failure had almost made them Black Legion’s cousins — adjacent to the archtraitor’s temptation, forged into refusal.
