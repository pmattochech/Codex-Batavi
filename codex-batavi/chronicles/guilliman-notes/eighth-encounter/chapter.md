Guilliman did not schedule the eighth encounter as a ceremony.

Ceremony was how institutions taught themselves lies they could live with.

He scheduled it as an audit conducted under noise.

There were Chapters that performed piety for visitors and called it virtue. There were Chapters that performed cruelty and called it purity. The Batavi performed neither. They performed work. That, Guilliman had learned, was its own kind of danger: work could become a myth without ever meaning to.

He chose Batavi ground that was not Noviomagus, because he did not want the forge-world’s authority to do the speaking for them. He wanted the Chapter in a place where the planet’s pride could not cushion the truth.

The location was a trenchworks lattice welded into the Nine Phalanx mesh — one of the Castra Vetera bastions that existed to be ugly and therefore honest. Pylon-adjacent earthworks and prefabricated ferrocrete, a fortress-dock’s underbelly where supply met blood. The air tasted of propellant residue, machine oil, and wet stone. The lumen strips flickered just often enough to remind a mortal mind that this place did not care whether you were comfortable.

Guilliman arrived without banners.

Not because he feared worship — he assumed worship was the default failure mode of any frightened population — but because he did not want his own presence to become an excuse for theater.

They did not greet him with a choir.

They greeted him with a work line.

Humans in dull fatigues and rebreather masks moved munitions crates and sealant drums along a rail track. An Astartes in Batavi plate stood among them like a load-bearing pillar that had chosen to lower itself into the same gravity. He did not lift with exaggerated care. He lifted with exactness. His gauntlets were too large for the human harness grips, so he adapted without comment, using his own fingers as the clamps the harness should have been.

The humans did not shrink away from him.

They watched him the way men watched a heavy machine that could save them or kill them, and then — when it did not kill them — allowed themselves to breathe a fraction easier. One of them passed him a strip of cloth for wiping condensation from a crate’s seal. The Astartes took it without hesitation, used it, and handed it back as if it were an instrument, not a relic.

Shared sweat.

Guilliman filed the observation with a bitter memory of the Great Crusade: how quickly “shared hardship” became a story, and how quickly stories became absolution.

He looked for the other half of the equation: the boundary line.

The Batavi did not eat with the humans. They did not remove helms. They did not offer affection. They offered participation. It was colder than friendship and safer than worship.

Beside Guilliman stood the escort he had requested: not a Triumvirate mask, but a watch-certified veteran from the local Vexillatio. Henric Kessler, Legatus of II Vexillatio — Secunda, wore his authority the way the Batavi wore everything: as function, not as theater. His armor bore the scars of this trenchworks’ geometry. His posture was not ceremonial. It was the posture of a man who had learned to keep a commander alive by telling him when the wall had hairline cracks.

Guilliman watched a small rite occur without announcement.

A Batavi boarding a transport rapped his gauntlet knuckles, three quick dry taps, against the adamantium lip of the hatch before stepping through.

No one reacted. No one smiled. No one named it.

It was simply done.

He had read their approved mysticisms, the way they masked compulsion as telemetry and superstition as protocol. What mattered was not the belief. What mattered was that the Chapter had engineered a culture that allowed pressure to discharge without becoming permission.

— You live among them — Guilliman said quietly. — Watching the human line flow around the Astartes like water around a pillar.

Kessler answered without pride.

— We work where the work is.

Guilliman waited for embellishment. It did not come.

He moved deeper into the trenchworks, into a planning node cut into the ferrocrete. Inside, the air was drier, the lumen steadier. A hololith projected the local defensive grid: kill-boxes, retreat lanes, supply caches, casualty corridors. It was not art. It was a map designed by men who assumed the universe would do its worst.

Alaric von Helis was already there.

Helm on. Presence heavy. Stillness that carried more authority than any raised voice.

Guilliman did not begin with greetings. Greetings were a social lubricant. He had not come to slide anything.

— I wanted to see the Chapter’s day-to-day shape — he said. — Not a trial. Not a verdict. A habit.

Alaric’s answer came as it always did: minimal and sufficient.

— You are seeing it.

Guilliman inclined his head a fraction. Then he waited.

He did not have to wait long.

The objection came the way a bolt round came: unromantic, direct, inevitable.

A battle-brother stepped forward from the edge of the hololith. Brother Jannik Voss, II Vexillatio — Secunda, did not preface the act with any formula. He did not ask permission to disagree. He did not posture. He spoke as if the plan were a machine part he could already hear failing.

— Lord. The western traverse is a false economy. We will bleed there.

The room did not flinch.

Guilliman felt his own old instincts rise anyway — the instincts of empire and chain, the instincts that had kept Legions from becoming mobs. A subordinate had just contradicted the Chapter Master’s authored plan in front of an external sovereign.

He turned his gaze to the objector.

— You are breaking the chain of command — Guilliman said. — His voice was not loud. It did not need to be.

The battle-brother did not look away.

He did not apologize.

He did not justify himself with ego.

He waited, as if this question was expected too.

Kessler stepped half a pace, not to shield the objector, but to translate the Chapter’s bone into language Guilliman could file.

— No, my lord — Kessler said. — This is the chain functioning. We institutionalized it after the Foundation Wars, when objections were voiced and ignored and men died for the comfort of uninterrupted authority. We require structured dissent before an order is sealed because mission failure is a more lethal insubordination than disagreement.

Guilliman’s eyes narrowed.

— You permit this universally.

— We require it — Kessler answered. — Before an order is sealed.

Guilliman turned back to Alaric.

— And after?

— After — Alaric said — compliance.

The word did not sound like a slogan. It sounded like a lock closing.

Guilliman looked again at the objector.

— Proceed — he said.

The battle-brother’s posture altered by a fraction. Not relaxation. Alignment. He began the structure without naming it — performed as instinct rather than theater.

— The break is the traverse itself — he said. — It assumes we can hold the angle under sustained fire without collapsing the human supply line behind us.

He touched the hololith, indicating the corridor’s choke.

— The failure occurs here and here. When the second resupply push hits the bend, the enemy’s indirect fire will pin the mortals. We will be forced to move to protect them, and our movement will expose the flank.

He did not stop there. He offered the only thing that made an objection lawful under the Batavi’s own harsh logic: repair.

— We do not commit to the traverse. We commit to a counter-approach: we fortify the southern culvert, reroute supply through the drainage grid, and anchor the traverse with remote turrets instead of bodies. The mortals move under cover, we hold angles without being baited into motion.

There was no pride in his voice.

There was also no softness.

Guilliman recognized a dangerous kind of discipline: the kind that trained a man to speak truth without indulging the ego that loved truth.

Alaric answered without heat.

He did not treat the objection as insolence. He treated it as a test that had to be marked.

— The culvert cannot take the mass you are routing — Alaric said. — It will collapse under that structural load, and then you will have created a tomb for the very mortals you intend to protect.

Guilliman heard Alaric name the failure mode in mass and physics, not in slogans: load as fact, not abstraction.

Alaric continued.

— We have survey returns from the last cycle — he said. — The culvert’s reinforcement is cracked. You assume enemy fire will remain indirect. It will not. They will shift to direct pressure once they smell a reroute. The drainage grid cannot be sealed in time without exposing the engineers.

He paused, and Guilliman saw the pause’s purpose: not drama. Education.

— And your proposal assumes the human line can keep cohesion in a confined, wet, dark corridor under bombardment — he said. — They will panic. Panic will block the grid. Blockage will kill them.

Guilliman watched the objector hold still and receive the correction like an impact that did not knock him down.

Alaric finished without humiliation.

— Your instinct to protect the supply line is correct — Alaric said. — The culvert is not. If you want the same outcome, you must find a route that does not become a panic trap and does not require structural integrity we do not possess.

It was a refusal to crush the man for being wrong.

It was also a refusal to let him keep the comfort of his own proposal.

Guilliman could have left it there.

The doctrine had functioned. The chain had not fractured. The plan had been stress-tested without becoming a duel. The subordinate had been corrected without being degraded. The room had not turned into a theater of dominance.

But Guilliman’s mind did not stop where custom wanted it to stop. He had not survived by respecting the end of other men’s sentences.

He stepped closer to the hololith.

— Both of you missed an angle — he said.

The room stilled in a different way.

Not offended. Alert.

Guilliman pointed at the traverse’s bend, then at a secondary line that neither had referenced: an Administratum supply schedule overlay, stamped with Imperial bureaucracy’s blind certainty.

— Mass and time are not only physics — he said. — They are policy. Your second resupply push is not under your control. It is under the Departmento Munitorum’s convoy doctrine. The mortals will arrive when the slate says they arrive. They will not wait for “optimal geometry” because the Imperium does not understand geometry; it understands compliance.

The objector’s helm angled slightly, as if he had been struck by a new kind of threat.

Guilliman continued, clinical.

— The enemy does not need to pin the mortals — he said. — They need only to learn that the schedule exists. A single captured slate. A single vox leak. They will time their pressure to the bureaucratic cadence, not to your tactical cadence.

He let the words sink in. Then he delivered the part that neither Alaric nor the objector could deliver, because it belonged to a ruler’s ugly intimacy with the Imperium.

— The Guard will not panic only from fear — he said. — They will panic from contradiction. If your line commanders are ordered to move by Munitorum schedule and then counter-ordered by Astartes geometry, they will freeze. Freeze becomes massacre.

Guilliman looked at Alaric.

— Your correction was accurate. Your doctrine is valuable — he said. — But your solution cannot be only “find another route.” It must include control of the policy layer. The Munitorum schedule must be brought into the same audit structure or it will become a fracture you cannot correct in time.

Alaric did not speak immediately.

Guilliman recognized the moment: the wall weighing whether to treat an external sovereign’s words as intrusion or as additional load-bearing constraint.

Alaric chose the latter.

— Understood — he said.

Not “agreed.” Not “thank you.”

Understood.

Guilliman turned to the Legatus.

— How often does this occur.

The Legatus did not hesitate.

— Daily, my lord. Sometimes more than once.

Guilliman’s brow tightened.

— And outside your Chapter.

The Legatus’s pause was small but real. Not shame. Calculation: how much truth was useful.

— It creates conflict — he said. — Other Chapters hear an objection and assume insolence. They do not know the structure, only the act. In the Watch, it can save lives. In joint campaigns, it can fracture alliances. Some take it as a public challenge. Some punish it. Some learn.

Guilliman watched the objector again. The battle-brother had not moved. He had not basked in being corrected by a primarch. He had not looked for reward.

He looked like a man who had learned that the highest honor was not being praised.

The highest honor was being made less wrong.

Guilliman found himself returning, briefly, to the fear that had lived under all his encounters with the Batavi.

Not gene-line.

Not numbers.

Not even the curse behind their discipline.

The fear that an institution so obsessed with avoiding stupid death might become contemptuous of ordinary living.

He watched the trenchworks beyond the node’s slit viewport: humans sweating under weight, a giant in ceramite moving among them without tenderness and without distance, a culture that tried to be friendly without becoming intimate, to be present without becoming worship.

He filed the final note with the same restraint he had demanded of Alaric.

Structured objection, on Batavi ground, was not rebellion.

It was a confession that even walls had blind spots.

And a refusal to pay for those blind spots in blood when instruction could pay the cost first.
