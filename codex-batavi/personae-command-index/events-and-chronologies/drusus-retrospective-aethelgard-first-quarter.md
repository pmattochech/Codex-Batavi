## I. THE LANDING (015.M42)

There were 287 Marines on the first transport. More arrived in the following months in successive waves until the founding contingent of just over a thousand was complete. Primaris, newly stabilized chimeric gene-seed, Tacticus plate with the provisional crest’s paint still wet. None of us had fought together before the landing. The formation was a sketch — numbered Vexillationes, not tempered. The *Codex Astartes* was the only common denominator.

Aethelgard Prime received us with the indifference of a world that had already culled everything weak. Swamps of dark water to hip height, forests of trunks with oxidized metal bark, and the black stone peaks the natives called the Needles — from whose heights local fauna descended to hunt. The ecosystem’s apex carnivore, the *Canis Batavorum*, was famous among the natives for leaping from those rocks straight into the great tides to crush the armored skulls of young Leviathans. The Astartes noted the animal with the technical interest reserved for a functional ambush archetype. None of us imagined, in that moment, that we would be the prey.

The Castra Vetera system was described in Administratum reports as “low-priority contested territory” — bureaucratic wording for a subsector that had lost three colonization attempts to “unclassified” causes. We landed on Aethelgard with orders to pacify and establish mineral-extraction priority. The reports mentioned localized xenos resistance.

The reports did not describe **what**, exactly, was hunting us.

Writing now, decades later, we file them as catalogued xenos with dossiers and countermeasures. In 015.M42, we had no name for them. We only had deaths that followed no pattern the Codex had foreseen.

## II. FIRST CONTACT (015.M42 — Aethelgard, Sector Delta-7)

The 3rd Vexillatio was the first to vanish.

No combat was logged. No broken vox. No distress signal. Twenty-three Marines went on a surface reconnaissance and did not return. When we sent the 1st Vexillatio to investigate, we found the gear intact — armor, weapons, field supplies — arranged with an organization that seemed almost deliberate. As if the enemy had sorted out what it did not need before taking what it did.

No bodies. No blood. No conventional trail.

I spent the next sixteen hours cataloguing the site centimeter by centimeter. I logged soil temperature variation, pressure patterns in sediment, and marks on metal surfaces that matched no weapon in the Imperial inventory. In the end, my report ran forty-three pages of carefully gathered data and a single conclusion line: *“Event origin: undetermined.”*

It was the first time in my life I wrote those words in a medical-tactical report. Not the last.

## III. THE MANUAL THAT LACKED THE RIGHT PAGES (015–016.M42)

The *Codex Astartes* is a masterpiece of war geometry. It covers open battle, urban siege, guerrilla war, Genestealer-infestation purges, civil-uprising suppression, combat against Orks, Tyranids, the sons of the Ruinous Power. It is built to teach Astartes how to win.

What it does not cover: an enemy that does not want to win. That wants to hunt.

The hunter-enclave did not seize positions. It did not defend territory. It did not build supply lines or establish permanent bases. It did not offer battle in the open. It hunted. Alone, in pairs, rarely in threes. It always chose moment and terrain. It avoided any setup where the number of Astartes exceeded the number of stalkers in play. It waited. Patient as geology.

In the first eight months, the Chapter’s response was doctrinal: increase patrol coverage, reduce isolated units, consolidate positions, establish kill zones. The Codex said that was correct procedure. We lost forty-seven Marines applying correct procedure.

Alaric called a Vexillatio meeting after the fourteenth loss. I remember him at the center of the table, armor still stained from the last field, hands open on the map of Aethelgard. Very still. Stiller than usual.

*“Does anyone have something different from the Codex to offer,”* he said. It was not a question.

No one did. We were the Codex. The Codex was what we had.

## IV. THE INVERSION (016.M42)

The first time I understood what was happening to the Chapter’s biology, I was watching Sergeant Caelus Mervant hand in a patrol report.

Mervant was meticulous. Every report on time, every datum correct, every field filled with precision that honored the training. That day he entered the *Strategium*, set the report on the table, and when I said *“wait — I need confirmation on the eastern sector data,”* he turned toward me at a speed that was not normal for an Astartes at rest — not combat speed, which I recognize at once, but something else. More instinctive. Like an animal that hears something behind it in a place that should be safe.

He checked himself in a fraction of a second. But I saw.

I began to monitor. In silence, without formal protocol, because I did not know what I was monitoring. What I found in the following weeks was a pattern the *Apothecarion* had no nomenclature for: Marines who had always been stable showing a shift in response threshold. Nothing catastrophic. Nothing that compromised immediate tactical function. Only a different frequency — as if the volume of all emotional states had been slightly, but permanently, raised.

I documented. I wrote *“elevated basal reactivity — cause undetermined”* and filed.

I only realized I was in the same report when Alaric asked me, six weeks later, whether I had slept. I answered that there were no relevant data on the subject. He looked at me for three seconds without speaking.

*“It was a question,”* he said.

It took me two days to process that those three seconds of stare had triggered irritation completely disproportionate to the context.

I was in the report.

## V. WHAT THE CODEX CALLS DEFEAT (016–018.M42)

Losses in the first quarter were not massive by Astartes war standards. In three years we lost one hundred sixteen Marines — under forty percent of the initial contingent. In another context, that number would not define catastrophe.

What the number does not capture: how each loss arrived.

There was no front line. No battle with beginning, middle, and end. There were patrols that came back short. There were Marines the auspex still tracked but vox no longer reached. There were positions held for days and abandoned overnight by an enemy that left only the smell of keratin and residual body heat on sensors. There were brothers who died whose bodies were never recovered.

That last point had an effect the Codex had not foreseen: the impossibility of performing the full funeral rite. Gene-seed could not be extracted from bodies that did not exist. The names could not be inscribed on any surface, because to inscribe them was to declare a permanent boundary — and there was none. The dead stayed in the log as “status unconfirmed” because to recognize them officially as dead meant accepting the Enclave had taken something we could not recover.

Varro began to perform the rites without the bodies. It was outside protocol. I questioned the liturgical validity. He answered that validity was not the point. That the line needed some way to turn loss into something defined, or the undefined would eat discipline before any stalker had to do more.

It was the first time I understood what Varro did that I could not. Not liturgy. Collective psychological collapse management through means biochemistry could not measure.

## VI. THE FIRST FUROR (017.M42)

The exact date is logged: seventeenth day of the third month of 017.M42.

Legionary Tarkan Vosse, 7th Vexillatio. Twelfth year of service. Clean record. The follow-up report noted *“tension above basal standard, within acceptable margin”* — the same phrase I was writing for two-thirds of the Chapter in that period.

Vosse’s patrol entered an abandoned mining complex in the northern sector. The auspex showed thermal signatures of four stalkers. They cleared the location per Codex protocol — two simultaneous entry points, mutual cover, status vox every two minutes.

The stalkers were not inside. They were outside. And when the patrol realized it had walked into a trap — that the complex was the destination, not the enemy — two Marines were down before the first shot.

Vosse reacted. The other three in the patrol described what they saw: he advanced on the stalker’s fallback position alone, without waiting for cover, without confirming whether the others could still support. It was not protocol. It was efficient, by the geometry of one thing only: the distance was short and Vosse was fast. He reached the stalker and killed it. Then he stood on the body and did not move for forty seconds while the rest of the patrol consolidated.

When he returned to the *Strategium*, his irises were orange with red rims. Slit pupils. Beard grown beyond regulation — twenty-three hours of accelerated growth.

I recognized it because I had catalogued the parameters two weeks earlier, when I had noticed the same mutation in myself in the mirror at two in the morning after my sixteenth straight hour of field analysis. I had not written it in the report because there was still no nomenclature.

Vosse had Furor. I had Furor too. The difference is I had catalogued mine before it appeared in anyone else’s report.

By the end of that month I had seventy-two confirmed cases. One-third of the Chapter.

## VII. THE MEETING NOT IN THE CODEX (017.M42)

Alaric called the Triumvirate — myself, Varro — to a meeting that had no name in protocol, because protocol had not foreseen the situation.

We sat in the *Strategium* with the map of Aethelgard between us and the report of seventy-two cases on the table.

Varro said: *“The real number is higher. Those are the ones you could measure. What has no instrument yet is larger than what you catalogued.”*

I agreed the datum was plausible but not verifiable with available instruments.

Alaric looked at the map for a long time. *“The Codex has no protocol for this,”* he said. *“I know. I checked.”*

*“The Codex did not foresee an enemy that hunts,”* said Varro.

*“The Codex was written for Astartes who attack,”* said Alaric. *“Not for Astartes who are prey.”*

There was silence. Not the silence of people with nothing to say. The silence of people processing something that reframes the whole board.

*“Then we need a protocol that is not in the Codex,”* said Varro. *“Why are the seventy-two cases still functioning?”*

I had the answer. I had spent the previous nights analyzing exactly that. *“Duty. Those who entered the stage and did not fully collapse are the ones with a function that cannot stop. It does not work for everyone — some collapse regardless of function. But it is the most consistent variable. The Marine with a specific, concrete obligation anchors the state better than the Marine who does not.”*

*“Then we give obligations,”* said Alaric.

*“It is not that simple,”* I said. *“The obligation must be real. It cannot be fabricated. The organism knows the difference.”*

Varro looked at Alaric for a moment. *“You have an obligation that cannot stop.”*

Alaric did not answer at once. When he spoke, it was only: *“The line cannot yield.”*

That was the embryo of what would become the *Axioma Rationis*. Not philosophy. A clinical observation that duty works as an anchor when nothing else does — and that naming it, making it explicit, and teaching it might be the difference between a Chapter that holds and one that shatters.

We did not have the name yet. We only had the observation. And the observation was that three members of the Triumvirate, all with seventy-two percent biometric collapse probability by my own data, sat around a table debating how to save the Chapter instead of collapsing.

I logged that too.

## VIII. INVERTING THE HUNT (018–023.M42)

The second quarter of the war began when Alaric stopped trying to kill the Enclave and started studying it.

It was not an elegant strategic decision. It was brutal recognition: we were losing because we were using an attacking army’s manual against a hunting enemy. To fight a hunter, we had to think like hunters. And the Chapter, as we came to understand, bore **chimeric** gene-seed: one strand answered the war as **apex hunter** — ice-plain instinct, oath before betrayal — and a **second strand** we did not yet have language for, except that it **judged** treason the way the first judged prey.

The inversion was gradual and brutal. We stopped patrolling by the Codex and started patrolling by the environment. We stopped consolidating positions and started using positions as bait. We stopped trying to create predictable patterns and started studying theirs.

The Enclave was extraordinary. But it was predictable within extraordinariness — because every hunter has patterns. They preferred high ground but not exposed ground. They preferred ambushes to direct confrontation even when direct confrontation was mathematically favorable. They preferred to separate before isolate before eliminate. Those were habits. And habits can be used.

The first stalker we took alive — and who survived capture — was in 019.M42. I spent ninety-six hours analyzing its biology. I catalogued every organic system, every adaptation, every measurable vulnerability. In the end, my report ran two hundred thirty pages and one conclusion line: *“Adversary catalogued. Countermeasures possible.”*

It was the first time in that conflict I wrote those words in a report. The last of the previous series had been “undetermined.” These were “possible.” The distance between the two adjectives is where the Chapter learned to be Batavi.

## IX. THE COST OF LEARNING (015–023.M42)

By the end of the first quarter, we had lost one hundred eighty-three Marines. Of the first transport’s contingent — the 287 who landed before reinforcements arrived — one hundred four survived with mind intact. Those who arrived later fared no better. Most not as combat losses but as Versibars.

That number needs explanation for anyone who did not live it.

Furor was not the problem. Manageable Furor — sixty percent of the cases I documented stabilized with the anchor-by-duty protocol. The problem was what happened when Furor did not stabilize: progression to Exanimus, and from there to Execratio. A Marine in Execratio is no longer a Marine. It is an abomination that uses memories of Astartes tactics to hunt Astartes. We killed thirty-seven that way — by Alaric’s hand, by Varro’s hammer, by bolter fire from Astartes who, contrary to their oaths, never thought they would have to kill their own brothers.

Most bodies were burned in the field. There was no way to perform full rites. Alaric began taking the identification plates from the first Versibars he executed — the Root Plate with the civil name, the Iron Plate with the Batavi name — and lashing them to a steel cord against his armor. No rite instructions. No associated liturgy. He simply stopped leaving the names behind.

Those who survived the first quarter — the one hundred four Marines who reached the second quarter with mind intact — were, without exception, those who had found their own version of what we today call the *Axioma Rationis* anchor. Not necessarily the strongest, nor the most experienced, nor the most biologically resilient. Those who had something they could not let go.

They became the first Legati. The founding elite. Those who taught the *Axiom* to later generations not as philosophy but as the specific method by which they themselves had not died.

Alaric’s cord grew across thirty years of war. Each pair of plates is a name we learned to save too late.

## X. COMPILER’S NOTE (048.M42)

This record was compiled thirty-three years after the events described, from field reports, biometric data, and Triumvirate personal memory. Some figures are estimates. Some dates have a margin of weeks. Some deaths are logged without name because the names did not survive the events.

What matters: the loss count. The causal sequence that led to the *Axioma Rationis*. The fact that the Chapter that exists today was built on what we learned by not knowing what we were doing.

This file is not a chronicle of victory. It is the record of the price of learning not to lose.

The Batavi who came after inherited the *Axioma Rationis* as doctrine. Those who were here built it as survival. The difference is not philosophical. It is the plate cord Alaric still carries — ninety-seven kilograms against his armor’s ceramite, the physical weight of the cost of discovering what we are.

*“We did not fall back. We held. The Codex did not save us.”*  
*“What saved us was harder to name than any protocol it contains.”*

— Drusus, *Codex of Das Erbe*, Preface.
